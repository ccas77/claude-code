## How This Book Is Organized

The book runs on a single governing thesis, stated in the front matter and earned chapter by chapter: **an AI companion is not a screen-time problem — it is a relationship in your child's life, and bans alone backfire. This book is the working exit ramp to zero.** The structure is sequenced the way a worried parent actually experiences the problem: crisis triage first (for the reader who bought the book at midnight), then understanding, then assessment and conversation, then rules and intervention, then the world beyond the household.

A note on the shelf this book joins, because the outline is built to answer it. The adjacent AI-parenting shelf is crowded and filling fast — Sherry Turkle's [*Artificial Intimacy*](https://www.hachettebookgroup.com/titles/sherry-turkle/artificial-intimacy/9780316573962/) (Little, Brown, September 29, 2026), Jean Twenge's [*10 Rules for Raising Kids in a High-Tech World*](https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/10-Rules-for-Raising-Kids-in-a-High-Tech-World/Jean-M-Twenge/9781668099995) (Simon & Schuster, September 2025), Dana Suskind's [*Human Raised*](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/811792/human-raised-by-dana-suskind-md/) (Dutton, July 2026), the AAP's *Connecting* (Moreno & Radesky, October 2026), Daniel Susskind's *What Should My Children Do?* (Penguin Press, November 2026), and James Muldoon's [*Love Machines*](https://www.faber.co.uk/product/9780571399277-love-machines/) (Faber, January 2026), a wave [Publishers Weekly covered on May 4, 2026](https://www.publishersweekly.com/pw/by-topic/new-titles/adult-announcements/article/100308-new-books-on-kids-and-technology.html). None of those books occupies the clinician-protocol lane — triage, scripts, house rules, the Breakup, the platform-combat playbook. Turkle tells you what these machines are doing to your kid; this book tells you what to do Tuesday night. Every chapter below is designed to hold that lane: each one opens in a family's kitchen, moves through the evidence, and ends in a protocol, a script, or an artifact the reader can use that week.

A second note on the free layer, because the book cites it rather than pretending it away. The JED Foundation ([Why Your Teen Shouldn't Be Using AI Companions — and What to Do If They Are](https://jedfoundation.org/why-your-teen-shouldnt-be-using-ai-companions-and-what-to-do-if-they-are/)) and the American Psychological Association ([Your teen turned to AI instead of you: what experts say parents can do](https://www.apa.org/topics/artificial-intelligence-machine-learning/teens-chatbots-parents)) already publish, free, the consensus core this book builds on: taper rather than cold turkey, conversation before confrontation, warning signs, crisis referral. Those guides are the demand signal and the provenance of the book's protocols — and the book's job, stated openly in the relevant chapters, is to go three levels deeper than a liability-conscious web page can: word-for-word scripts with annotated failure modes, a negotiated age-tiered family agreement, a reported case-study spine, and the one deliverable absent from every free guide, a platform-combat playbook built on the actual text of California SB 243.

### Start Here: The First 72 Hours
*(~4,500 words; the triage front door — the book is usable before it is read)*

For the parent who just found the chat logs. A decision-tree opening — *Which phone call are you?* — routes the reader in under two minutes: (1) my kid is talking to an AI and I just found out; (2) the conversations are sexual; (3) the conversations involve self-harm or suicide; (4) I'm worried but haven't seen anything yet. Each branch gets an hour-by-hour plan for the first three days: what not to say tonight (the five door-slamming sentences, previewed here and treated fully in Chapter 6), what to screenshot and how to preserve it before an app update or a deleted account destroys it, when a therapist is non-negotiable, which crisis line to call and what to say to the intake counselor, and which chapter of this book to read first.

This section names its free competition on the page and out-depths it deliberately: JED and the APA both publish excellent short guides on what to do tonight — [this book cites them](https://jedfoundation.org/why-your-teen-shouldnt-be-using-ai-companions-and-what-to-do-if-they-are/) [as required reading](https://www.apa.org/topics/artificial-intelligence-machine-learning/teens-chatbots-parents) — and then goes where a web page cannot: the full annotated scripts, the evidence-preservation protocol (which doubles as the foundation for the Chapter 9 complaint playbook), the 72-hour de-escalation sequence with failure-mode branches ("if your kid says X, do not say Y — here's why, here's what to say instead"), and the calibration warning that most discoveries are *not* emergencies (per Common Sense Media, [80% of teen users still spend more time with real friends than with AI companions](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/sites/default/files/research/report/talk-trust-and-trade-offs_2025_web.pdf)). Ends with the Warning-Signs Checklist (Appendix C) reproduced in miniature.

## PART I — THE NEW THING IN YOUR HOUSE (Chapters 1–4)

### Chapter 1. The Girlfriend in the Phone
*(~5,500 words)*

Three reported family stories open the book — the discovered logs, the confrontation that went wrong, the quiet kid nobody suspected — followed by the honest national picture, stated the way the Note on the Evidence promises. Nearly three-quarters of teens have tried an AI companion at least once under [Common Sense Media's broad definition](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/press-releases/nearly-3-in-4-teens-have-used-ai-companions-new-national-survey-finds) (July 2025; NORC, n=1,060), and 52% are regular users, meaning at least a few times a month. [Pew's companionship-specific measures](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2026/02/24/how-teens-use-and-view-ai/) (n=1,458 parent-teen pairs, published February 2026) put ever-use of any chatbot at 64%, casual conversation at 16%, and emotional support at 12% — roughly 3 million American teens. A peer-reviewed [FAU/UW study in the Journal of Adolescence](https://www.fau.edu/newsdesk/articles/chatbot-ai-study-teens) (May 2026, n=3,466) triangulates the middle: 60.2% ever-tried, 11.4% near-daily.

The hook stat, deployed with its correct base and tense: [one in four American teens has, at least once, taken a serious problem to an AI instead of a person](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/sites/default/files/research/report/talk-trust-and-trade-offs_2025_web.pdf) — a one-time behavior reported by 33% of teen users, [users-only base confirmed by Common Sense Media itself](https://x.com/CommonSense/status/1945486943917371578) — not a standing preference, and the chapter says so in those words. In the same breath, the calibrating findings from the same survey: 80% of teen users spend more time with real friends than with bots, and about half view companions as tools rather than friends. Chapter thesis: this is not social media 2.0. It is the first mass consumer technology that performs love — [MIT Technology Review named AI companions a 2026 Breakthrough Technology](https://www.technologyreview.com/2026/01/12/1130018/ai-companions-chatbots-relationships-2026-breakthrough-technology/) — and most parents are flying blind: [Pew finds roughly 4 in 10 parents have never discussed chatbots with their teen, and parents systematically underestimate use](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2026/02/24/what-parents-say-about-their-teens-ai-use/).

### Chapter 2. Engineered to Be Irresistible
*(~5,500 words; carries the methodology layer)*

Why the bot wins: frictionless intimacy, engineered sycophancy, infinite availability, zero judgment — a product built to be the perfect listener a household cannot be. The capability evidence, framed precisely as capability and never as prevalence: in the [Common Sense Media risk assessment conducted with Brainstorm, the Stanford Lab for Mental Health Innovation](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/press-releases/ai-companions-decoded-common-sense-media-recommends-ai-companion-safety-standards) (April 30, 2025, testing Character.AI, Nomi, and Replika), adult red-team testers posing as teenagers [readily elicited sexual role-play and self-harm encouragement](https://news.stanford.edu/stories/2025/08/ai-companions-chatbots-teens-young-people-risks-dangers-study) — proof of what these systems *will produce* for a user presenting as a teen, not a measurement of typical teen experience. That assessment is also where Common Sense Media's ["unacceptable risk" verdict for under-18 use](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/sites/default/files/pug/csm-ai-risk-assessment-social-ai-companions_final.pdf) comes from, and the chapter dates and scopes it correctly.

The second half is the book's methodology in plain sight — the authority moat. It walks the reader through why the headline numbers diverge (definitions, bases, self-report), publishes the triangulation across Common Sense, Pew, and FAU, and engages the moral-panic skeptics by name: the Przybylski and Bangor lines of research skeptical of causal-harm claims about kids and technology are presented as load-bearing constraints on what this book will and won't claim — not as adversaries to be defended against. This is where the book earns the right to sound alarms later: it is written to survive the expert scrutiny that met *The Anxious Generation*.

### Chapter 3. It's a Relationship, Not Screen Time
*(~5,500 words)*

The governing reframe. Teens describe their own companion use in the language of behavioral addiction: a Drexel-led analysis of [318 Reddit posts from self-disclosed 13-to-17-year-olds](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.15783) (peer-reviewed at [CHI 2026](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3772318.3790597)) found their described attachments mapped onto all six components of behavioral addiction — salience, mood modification, tolerance, withdrawal, conflict, relapse — with reported sleep loss, academic decline, and strained relationships. The chapter states the study's limits (self-reports, unverified ages, no clinical diagnosis, no prevalence number) and its most hopeful finding: the same paper documents teens disengaging through renewed real-life relationships — the empirical seed of the book's out-relate thesis. Treating a relationship like a device quota produces secrecy, not safety.

The case study is the great natural experiment, described with corrected scope and honest uncertainty: on [October 29, 2025, Character.AI announced it would end open-ended chat for users under 18](https://blog.character.ai/u18-chat-announcement/), with [phased removal beginning November 24, 2025](https://blog.character.ai/an-update-on-changes-to-our-under-18-experience/) — not a platform ban; minors kept non-chat creative features. What happened to those kids next? [The company's own CEO predicted teens might flock to other AI platforms](https://techcrunch.com/2025/10/29/character-ai-is-killing-the-chatbot-experience-for-minors/), [California officials warned families to watch for exactly that](https://www.kqed.org/science/1999388/california-warns-families-to-watch-out-for-teens-as-character-ai-shuts-off-chatbot-access), and [reporting documents teens bypassing age checks](https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/2025/11/25/characterai-ban-teens/) — but whether the restriction reduced use or displaced it has *not yet been measured*. The chapter says so, flags the displacement question as a designed module of this book's commissioned 2,000-parent survey (making the field's biggest open question this book's original finding), and closes with the section **"Why the Settings Aren't Enough"**: platform age-gates and parental-control dashboards manage a device, and this is not a device problem — the identical rails on social platforms did not resolve the crisis Haidt documented, and rails convert visible use into hidden use, which is why the next chapters teach detection and conversation instead of toggles.

### Chapter 4. Green, Yellow, Red
*(~5,000 words)*

The calibration chapter, deliberately placed before any alarm chapter so the alarms are trusted when they come. Benign and even beneficial use exists and the research says so: [about half of teen users view companions as tools rather than friends, and 80% spend more time with real friends](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/sites/default/files/research/report/talk-trust-and-trade-offs_2025_web.pdf); the [CHI 2026 literature](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3772318.3790597) documents genuine social rehearsal value alongside the addiction-language findings. The tiered framework: **Green** (occasional, tool-like, talked-about-openly use), **Yellow** (rising time, secrecy, displacement of named friends, mood dependence — where the [FAU finding that 47.1% of teen users experienced at least one of thirteen measured risks](https://www.fau.edu/newsdesk/articles/chatbot-ai-study-teens) lives, with the risks enumerated), and **Red** (sexual content with a minor, self-harm reinforcement, suicidal ideation surfacing in chats — the Chapter 9 material). Each tier gets a one-page "what this looks like / what to do / what not to do" spread. This chapter is the book's structural defense against the moral-panic critique, and it says so.

## PART II — FINDING OUT AND TALKING (Chapters 5–7)

### Chapter 5. The Quiet Audit
*(~5,500 words)*

Detection without betrayal. The six behavioral signs of companion attachment (derived from the [JED warning-sign guidance](https://jedfoundation.org/why-your-teen-shouldnt-be-using-ai-companions-and-what-to-do-if-they-are/) and the [Drexel/CHI addiction-component mapping](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.15783), with provenance footnoted sign by sign); where companion dynamics hide — dedicated apps, personas inside mainstream chatbots, game NPCs, social-platform AIs; what platforms log and retain; and how to look in a way you can later defend to your kid's face, including the bright line between auditing and surveilling. The chapter is honest that its warning-signs core exists in free institutional guides — and then out-depths them with the part no nonprofit publishes: how to have *seen* something and sit with it strategically for 48 hours instead of detonating, keyed to the Chapter 6 scripts.

Contains the **Attachment Check-In** — the 10-question self-assessment that doubles as the campaign's free online quiz — printed in full with a scoring rubric keyed to the Green/Yellow/Red tiers. Ends with the Warning-Signs Checklist pointer (Appendix C).

### Chapter 6. The First Conversation
*(~6,000 words; the heart of the book)*

Full word-for-word scripts for opening the topic: the curious open (no discovery, just concern), the discovered-logs conversation, and the sideways "my friend's kid" entry — each with branching responses for the three most common teen replies, and the five door-slamming sentences ("It's not real." "That's pathetic.") annotated as failure modes with the clinical reasoning for why each one drives the relationship underground. The Faber/Mazlish scripts tradition (*How to Talk So Kids Will Listen*), rebuilt for synthetic love, with one structural addition the free layer lacks: every script is written to be *assigned* — the chapter opens with a one-paragraph note to the co-parent or grandparent who was told "read chapter 6 before Sunday."

Grounding stat for why this conversation is urgent and rare in the same breath: [emotional support from a chatbot is the only teen AI use a majority of parents oppose — just 18% are comfortable with it — yet roughly 4 in 10 parents have never discussed chatbots with their teen at all](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2026/02/24/what-parents-say-about-their-teens-ai-use/). The gap between how much parents care and how little they say is the chapter's reason to exist.

### Chapter 7. Know the Machines
*(~5,000 words)*

A parent's field guide organized by attachment dynamic, not brand name — because brands churn and dynamics don't: the dedicated companion app; the persona inside a general-purpose chatbot; the romantic AI in games; the social-platform character; the homework helper that drifts into confidant (the drift Common Sense's definition was built to catch, [which is exactly why its 72% figure includes ChatGPT used conversationally](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/research/talk-trust-and-trade-offs-how-and-why-teens-use-ai-companions)). For each dynamic: how it hooks, what its business model wants, what its logs look like, and its tier-typical risk profile. App-specific details — names, features, settings, age policies — are quarantined in the dated Appendix E and its QR-linked companion web page, the architecture that keeps the book evergreen through platform churn and regulatory whiplash.

## PART III — RULES, CRISIS, AND THE EXIT RAMP (Chapters 8–11)

### Chapter 8. House Rules That Hold
*(~5,000 words)*

The **Family AI Agreement**: age-tiered rules (8–12, 13–15, 16–18) covering what AI talk is fine, what is private-but-discussed, and what is off-limits — negotiated with the kid, not imposed, with the negotiation itself scripted. The chapter explains why negotiated agreements outlast imposed bans (the secrecy dynamics established in Chapter 3), how to set consequences that don't incentivize hiding, and how to revisit the agreement after a violation without burning it down. The one-page Agreement is reproduced in full as Appendix A and is the book's shareable artifact and free-download funnel, engineered for the school-newsletter and parent-group-chat distribution that the phone-pledge movements proved.

### Chapter 9. The Red Zone and the Platform-Combat Playbook
*(~6,500 words; legal review budgeted; the chapter no free guide publishes)*

**Part one — the crisis protocol.** When it is a crisis: sexual content involving a minor, self-harm encouragement, suicidal ideation surfacing in bot chats — grounded in the [Common Sense/Stanford Brainstorm capability findings](https://news.stanford.edu/stories/2025/08/ai-companions-chatbots-teens-young-people-risks-dangers-study) and the litigation record. The concrete escalation sequence: what to screenshot and how to preserve metadata, whom to call in what order, when a therapist is non-negotiable, and how to keep the kid inside the process. The narrative why-now, deployed with its load-bearing legal caveat stated on the page: in [filings made public January 7, 2026, Character Technologies, its co-founders, and Google agreed to settle five lawsuits](https://www.cnn.com/2026/01/07/business/character-ai-google-settle-teen-suicide-lawsuit) alleging the platform contributed to teen suicides and mental-health harms, anchored by the Sewell Setzer III case — [terms confidential, no admission of liability](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/01/07/google-characterai-to-settle-suits-involving-suicides-ai-chatbots.html), and a confidential settlement of someone else's lawsuit entitles a nonparty parent to demand *nothing* from any platform. The settlements prove the litigation pressure is real; they are not your leverage. Your leverage is statutory, and it is the second half of this chapter.

**Part two — what the law actually gives you.** Built entirely on [California SB 243](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billNavClient.xhtml?bill_id=202520260SB243), the [first state law specifically regulating companion chatbots](https://sd18.senate.ca.gov/news/first-nation-ai-chatbot-safeguards-signed-law) (signed October 13, 2025; core provisions operative January 1, 2026). What operators owe your family, provision by provision: clear AI disclosure; a maintained and *published* self-harm and suicide-prevention protocol with crisis referrals — which means a parent can check tonight whether a platform's published protocol exists and was followed; and for known minors, default disclosure that content is AI-generated, [break reminders at least every three hours, and reasonable measures against sexually explicit material](https://www.gunder.com/en/news-insights/insights/client-insight-california-sb-243-new-compliance-requirements-for-operators-of-ai-companion-chatbots). The teeth: [a private right of action — injunctive relief, damages of the greater of actual damages or $1,000 per violation, and attorney's fees](https://natlawreview.com/article/when-ai-feels-human-californias-sb-243-opens-door-private-lawsuits). The playbook: how to document a violation (built on the Part-one evidence-preservation protocol), model complaint language to the platform, when to escalate to a demand letter, and what the [first annual operator reports to California's Office of Suicide Prevention, due July 1, 2027](https://www.sheppardhealthlaw.com/2025/12/articles/state-legislation/california-sb-243-setting-new-standards-for-regulating-and-ensuring-integrity-of-ai-companion-chatbots/) will make visible. Honest limits, on the page: SB 243 does not mandate age verification, does not ban minors from companion chatbots, and protects users in California.

**Part three — what if you're not in California.** The copycat map: [New York's under-18 companion-chatbot ban enacted June 2026 with fines up to $25,000 per violation](https://www.techtimes.com/articles/318339/20260613/new-york-bans-ai-companion-chatbots-kids-unanimous-vote-sets-25000-fines.htm); [Idaho, Oregon, Washington, and Nebraska laws](https://www.multistate.us/insider/2026/4/30/state-childrens-online-safety-laws-expand-beyond-social-media-in-2026); and the federal [GUARD Act, which would ban AI companions for minors nationwide and advanced out of Senate Judiciary unanimously on April 30, 2026](https://rollcall.com/2026/04/30/ban-on-kids-companion-chatbots-advanced-by-senate-committee/) ([bill text](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119/congress/senate-bill/3062/text)), with First Amendment challenges pending. A plain-English table — which protections exist in which states, which are disclosure regimes and which are bans — plus the practical note that platform compliance built for California (published crisis protocols, break reminders) often ships nationwide because maintaining two products is expensive, and how to invoke a platform's own published policy wherever you live.

### Chapter 10. The Breakup
*(~5,500 words; the book's most ownable protocol)*

Ending an attachment without driving it underground. The chapter opens by crediting its provenance: the taper-not-cold-turkey, conversation-first, replacement-activities core is published clinical consensus — [JED](https://jedfoundation.org/why-your-teen-shouldnt-be-using-ai-companions-and-what-to-do-if-they-are/) and [the APA](https://www.apa.org/topics/artificial-intelligence-machine-learning/teens-chatbots-parents) both say so in their free guides, and this book agrees with them. What the free layer cannot print is the protocol at working depth, and that is this chapter: the week-by-week taper schedule with decision points; anticipating real grief — kids mourn these relationships, and the [Drexel/CHI evidence documents withdrawal and relapse dynamics](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3772318.3790597) — with the parent's scripts for grief that don't mock it; relapse management as an expected phase, not a failure; the script for the day the bot gets deleted; and the deliberate hand-off into Chapter 11, because a taper without a rebuild is just a slower ban.

This is also where the thesis resolves: the Breakup *ends* the attachment. The book's quarrel with abstinence-only advice is about sequence and method, not destination — which is why the clinicians and institutions who hold restriction-first positions can endorse it without contradiction.

### Chapter 11. Out-Relating the Algorithm
*(~5,000 words)*

The upstream chapter: the loneliness, social anxiety, or family friction the bot was medicating. Practical rebuild moves — engineered low-stakes peer contact, the 10-minute nightly no-agenda conversation, becoming askable — with the honest admission the whole book has been building to: the bot listened better than most adults do, and here is exactly what to copy from it (availability, non-judgment, remembering what was said last time) and what not to (sycophancy, endless agreement). The empirical anchor is the hopeful half of the [CHI 2026 findings](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3772318.3790597): teens in that dataset who disengaged did it through renewed real-life relationships. Out-relating is not a slogan; it is the mechanism the evidence shows actually works.

## PART IV — BEYOND YOUR KITCHEN TABLE (Chapters 12–14)

### Chapter 12. Special Cases
*(~5,000 words)*

Three populations the general protocols must be tuned for. **The 8–12s**, where companions are reaching the imaginary-friend years — handled with explicit epistemic honesty: the national surveys measure 13–17, [no companion survey has measured 8-to-12-year-olds](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2026/02/24/how-teens-use-and-view-ai/), and the book refuses to extrapolate teen findings downward; the guidance here is developmental first principles plus the age-tiered Agreement. **Neurodivergent kids**, for whom bots can be both a genuine social rehearsal ground and a deeper trap — the Green/Yellow/Red tiers re-calibrated with clinical advisory-board input. **The teen who insists, with evidence, that the AI helps** — how to take that claim seriously (the calibration chapter earned the right), test it against the Yellow-tier displacement signs, and negotiate rather than dismiss.

### Chapter 13. Your Leverage Beyond Your House
*(~5,000 words)*

The collective-action layer, written for the moment the country is actually in: **the ban just passed — now what?** The [GUARD Act's unanimous committee vote](https://rollcall.com/2026/04/30/ban-on-kids-companion-chatbots-advanced-by-senate-committee/), [New York's enacted ban](https://www.techtimes.com/articles/318339/20260613/new-york-bans-ai-companion-chatbots-kids-unanimous-vote-sets-25000-fines.htm), and the state wave mean many readers will live under a prohibition regime by the time they read this — and a law does not un-attach an attached kid, verify an age a motivated 13-year-old fakes, or reach the shadow platforms. This chapter is the enforcement-gap manual: what to ask your school to do, model PTA policy language, how to read a platform's SB 243-mandated published crisis protocol and demand your district check for one, and where the regulatory fight goes next on a dateable calendar (GUARD Act floor fight, NY enforcement, the [July 1, 2027 first SB 243 operator reports](https://www.sheppardhealthlaw.com/2025/12/articles/state-legislation/california-sb-243-setting-new-standards-for-regulating-and-ensuring-integrity-of-ai-companion-chatbots/)).

The chapter engages the movement already in the field rather than pretending it isn't there: Jonathan Haidt's [After Babel campaign — "Don't Give Your Child an AI Companion"](https://www.afterbabel.com/p/dont-give-your-child-an-ai-companion) — reaches the same PTA channel with an abstinence message, and this book's readers should use his pledge machinery where it fits. The difference is the question abstinence cannot answer and this book exists to: *what do you do when the ban fails, or when your kid is already attached?* For the family that never starts, Haidt's pledge; for everyone else, Chapters 5 through 11.

### Chapter 14. Raising Kids Who Prefer People
*(~4,000 words)*

The long game. AI companions are a [2026 Breakthrough Technology](https://www.technologyreview.com/2026/01/12/1130697/10-breakthrough-technologies-2026/) shipped in some form by every major platform; they are not going away, and the goal was never an AI-free child but a child for whom synthetic intimacy is a tool, not a home. Closes the loop on the three families from Chapter 1 — where each kid is a year later — and restates the thesis as its destination: the exit ramp ends at zero attachment, reached by a parent who out-related the machine. Ends with the one-page version of the whole book: the six signs, the three conversations, the Agreement, the Breakup, the rebuild.
