## Part 1 — The Author-Platform Plan

### 1.1 Why the author is the decisive variable

The completeness critic was right to call the missing author the decisive variable, and the finalized spec agrees: securing a platform co-author is listed as a **build precondition**, not a nice-to-have ([`phaseB_finalize.json`, risk 4](research/phaseB_finalize.json)). The logic is simple and is drawn straight from the comp set:

- *The Anxious Generation* moved into the millions of copies on a **pre-existing author platform** (a tenured NYU social psychologist with a large newsletter and an established franchise). Its numbers are not evidence that the *topic* sells cold — they are evidence that the *topic plus the platform* sells.
- Sherry Turkle's prior books making the *identical* synthetic-intimacy argument (*Alone Together*, *Reclaiming Conversation*) sold at respected-bestseller, not phenomenon, scale — and Turkle is a 40-year MIT authority. That is the honest ceiling for the argument *without* a mass platform, and the reason this book's P&L is underwritten to **75k–250k units** (§3), not 2M.

The audience is real; the question the acquisition turns on is who hands it to them. So the author is not the last hire — it is the **first acquisition decision**, and everything downstream (advance size, pub timing against Turkle's 29 Sep 2026 date, print run) is contingent on it.

### 1.2 The ideal author profile

The book needs a voice that can carry three registers the outline demands at once: the **reporting spine** of Part I (how the products were engineered, built entirely from public record), the **developmental argument** of Part II (attachment, precautionary and honest about thin science), and the **parent playbook** of Part III (assessment, scripts, triage, reconnection). No single person reliably holds all three. The realistic answer is a **two-hander**.

### 1.3 Recommended structure: the two-hander (expert + writer)

| Role | What they supply | What they cannot supply alone |
|---|---|---|
| **Platform expert / lead author** (name on spine, face of the book) | Credibility, an existing audience to transfer (newsletter, clinical practice, media rounds, speaking), and authority for Part III's advice | Reporting bandwidth; narrative-nonfiction craft; time |
| **Reporter / writer (co-author or "with")** | The public-record investigative spine of Part I; narrative propulsion; the discipline to keep claims sourced and precautionary | The platform and the standing to issue parenting guidance |

This is the standard architecture behind most "expert-driven, narratively-told" trade nonfiction, and it directly de-risks the two things a single author most often gets wrong here: an expert with no audience (no transfer) or a journalist with no standing to write Part III (no authority). Pairing solves both. The expert's platform is the transfer engine; the writer guarantees the reporting spine and the responsible-reporting handling that the minors'-suicide material requires ([`phaseB_finalize.json`, legal/sensitivity risk](research/phaseB_finalize.json)).

### 1.4 Author archetypes (real, plausible *kinds* — no specific person is attached)

The following are **archetypes**, not claims that any named individual has been approached or signed. Each is described by the *kind* of platform it brings and the *transfer risk* it carries.

1. **The developmental / clinical psychologist with a large parenting audience.** A child- or adolescent-development expert who already publishes to hundreds of thousands of parents (newsletter, podcast, or bestselling backlist) and treats or studies adolescents clinically. *Strength:* maximum authority for Part III and native credibility on attachment; strongest transfer profile. *Risk:* may resist the precautionary-not-proven framing Part II requires, or lack reporting range — pair with a strong writer.

2. **The pediatric / adolescent-medicine or child-psychiatry clinician-communicator.** A physician who has become a trusted public explainer of adolescent mental health. *Strength:* "your child's doctor is worried" is the most persuasive possible frame for the anxious-parent buyer; excellent for the triage chapters (Ch. 12, "Normal, Worrying, Emergency"). *Risk:* smaller consumer platform than a category psychologist; must avoid over-medicalizing a behavior that is a minority phenomenon.

3. **The investigative technology journalist who has covered the AI-companion story.** A reporter who has covered the Character.AI/Meta/OpenAI litigation, the FTC 6(b) inquiry, the Reuters Meta-policy leak, or the TBIJ Character.AI investigation. *Strength:* owns the Part I reporting spine outright; deep public-record command. *Risk:* no parenting platform and no standing for Part III — this archetype is almost always the *writer* half of the two-hander, not the lead.

4. **The child-safety-technologist or former trust-and-safety insider.** Someone who built or audited youth-safety systems at a major platform, or leads a child-online-safety research organization. *Strength:* unmatched "how it was engineered" authority; media-friendly; institutionally credible. *Risk:* perceived conflict or advocacy framing; needs a writer to keep the narrative from reading as a policy white paper.

5. **The education / school-counseling or crisis-clinician voice.** A school counselor, crisis-line clinician, or educator with a public platform who sees the behavior in real kids daily. *Strength:* the ground-truth texture and the reconnection scripts (Ch. 11, 13) that no diagnosis book delivers. *Risk:* platform size is usually the constraint; strongest as a co-author or major source rather than lead.

6. **The "anxious-parent-of-a-teen" narrative-nonfiction author.** An established narrative journalist who has written a previous well-received book on childhood, technology, or mental health and can front a topic on craft and reputation. *Strength:* proven ability to sell a single-topic idea book; owns the "narrative nonfiction with a playbook" register that dodges the cooling pure-self-help category (`04-market-evidence.md`, §7 risk 7). *Risk:* borrowed rather than native authority on adolescent development; pair with a clinical expert as named contributor.

**The likely winning combination:** archetype 1 or 2 (platform + authority, on the spine) *with* archetype 3 or 4 (reporting spine, as "and" / "with"). That pairing supplies transfer, authority, and the public-record engine simultaneously.

### 1.5 The "author-search-first" acquisition path

A publisher or packager should treat author identification as the **first** workstream, running *before* a full proposal is locked, because the author determines the advance the house can justify and the pub date it must hit.

1. **Build the target list against the archetypes (weeks 0–4).** Screen candidates on three hard filters: (a) a *demonstrable, ownable* audience (newsletter subscribers, verified sales history, podcast reach, or clinical/institutional standing — not follower counts alone); (b) willingness to hold the **precautionary, not proven** line (a candidate who wants to claim settled neurological harm is disqualifying — it is the fastest way a reviewer guts the book, per `04`, §7 risk 3); and (c) availability to publish **close behind** Turkle's 29 Sep 2026 date, never trailing into a me-too 2027 slot.

2. **Lead with the expert, then bolt on the writer.** Secure the platform/authority half first, because that is the transfer engine and the harder "yes." Present the two-hander structure explicitly so the expert understands the reporting load is covered.

3. **Pressure-test transfer before betting the advance — de-risk the platform bet (weeks 4–8).** The platform-transfer bet is the whole risk, so *measure* it rather than assume it:
   - Ask for **audience evidence**, not audience claims: open/click rates and subscriber counts on the newsletter, actual BookScan history on any backlist, verified download numbers.
   - Run a **live demand probe**: a newsletter post, podcast episode, or op-ed on the exact thesis, and watch the engagement. A platform that spikes on "your kid and AI companions" is a transferring platform.
   - Check **audience-topic fit**: a large audience for unrelated content transfers poorly. The book needs an audience of *anxious parents of 10–17s*, which is precisely the *Anxious Generation* buyer.
   - Structure the deal to share the risk: **escalators and a bonus structure** tied to sales rather than a single large guaranteed advance, so the house is not fully underwriting an unproven transfer (see §3.4).

4. **Lock the writer and the pub-date plan (weeks 8–12).** Attach the reporting half, confirm the two can hit a manuscript date that lands the book within roughly two publishing seasons of Turkle, and only then finalize the proposal and advance.

5. **Pursue the Turkle-adjacent positioning asset.** A Turkle blurb or an explicit "the parent's field guide to the diagnosis Turkle names" framing is worth actively chasing during author search, because it converts the highest competitive risk (pre-emption) into a positioning advantage.

**Bottom line for the acquirer:** do not buy this concept and *then* look for an author. The concept's value is *conditional on* the author. Securing a transferring platform co-author is the build precondition — if it cannot be secured, the honest move is to underwrite at the conservative end of §3 or pass, not to bet the advance on the topic carrying itself.

## Part 2 — Market Size (TAM / SAM / SOM)

> **Method.** `04-market-evidence.md` sized the audience two ways — proven comp demand and survey-verified behavioral penetration — but deliberately withheld a population headcount rather than dress it with a false citation. This section supplies that headcount from primary sources and builds it into a funnel. Two structural honesty rules are carried through: (a) the intimacy behavior is a **~12–16% minority** of teens, so the funnel sells to the whole anxious-parent cohort on **anticipatory anxiety**, not to current-sufferers only; and (b) every step is either cited or labeled **[ESTIMATE]**.

### 2.1 Population base (sourced)

- **U.S. population under 18 (2024):** ~**73.1 million** ([U.S. Census Bureau, Vintage 2024 Population Estimates](https://www.census.gov/newsroom/press-kits/2025/2024-population-estimates-characteristics.html)).
- **Children by age band (most recent single-year federal table, 2022):** ages **6–11 = 24.2 million**; ages **12–17 = 25.8 million** ([Federal Interagency Forum on Child and Family Statistics, *America's Children*, Table POP1](https://www.childstats.gov/americaschildren/tables/pop1.asp)).

**Deriving the 10–17 target cohort [ESTIMATE].** The book's operational focus is ages 10–17. Using the cited bands: the 12–17 group is 25.8M outright; ages 10–11 are estimated by pro-rating the 6–11 band (24.2M ÷ 6 single-year cohorts × 2 cohorts ≈ 8.1M), assuming roughly even distribution across that band.
- **Ages 10–17 ≈ 25.8M + 8.1M ≈ ~33–34 million children.** *[ESTIMATE — sum of one cited band plus a pro-rated slice of a second cited band; even-distribution assumption; 2022 base, so directional for 2024.]*
- For the survey-matched **13–17** teen frame used by Pew and Common Sense: 12–17 (25.8M ÷ 6 × 5 cohorts) ≈ **~21–22 million teens**. *[ESTIMATE, same method.]*

### 2.2 From children to the buyer: households / parents

The buyer is the *parent*, not the child. Translating the child cohort into buying units:

- **Parent-buyer households with a 10–17-year-old ≈ ~24–27 million households.** *[ESTIMATE.]* Assumptions: ~33–34M children ages 10–17, at an assumed **~1.3–1.4 children in this age band per household** (siblings within the 10–17 window collapse into one buying household), yielding roughly 24–26M households; call it **~25 million** as a central figure. This is a buying-unit count, not a person count, and a household may buy once regardless of how many teens it contains. Directionally consistent with Census reporting that a large share of U.S. family households include children under 18 ([U.S. Census Bureau, families and living arrangements, 2024](https://www.census.gov/newsroom/press-releases/2024/families-and-living-arrangements.html)).

### 2.3 TAM — the total addressable market

**TAM = U.S. parents/households of a 10–17-year-old who are anxious about technology's effect on their child.**

- Household base: **~25 million** households with a 10–17-year-old *[ESTIMATE, §2.2]*.
- Anxious-and-engaged filter: not every such parent is a candidate book-buyer. The comp evidence gives the willingness-to-pay proof — the *Anxious Generation* buyer exists at phenomenon scale (#1 *NYT*, 73 weeks / #1 eleven times as of Aug 2025, [author, X](https://x.com/JonHaidt/status/1958598653540446370)) — while the *anticipatory* anxiety is now pointed squarely at AI: only **18% of parents are comfortable** with their teen getting emotional support from a chatbot ([Pew, Feb 2026](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2026/02/24/what-parents-say-about-their-teens-ai-use/)), i.e., **~80% are uncomfortable** with the exact use-case the book addresses.
- **Applied filter [ESTIMATE]:** assume **~25–40%** of the ~25M households are "engaged anxious parents" who buy books/guides on child-tech topics (a bracket informed by, not derived from, the scale of *Anxious Generation* penetration). → **TAM ≈ ~6–10 million U.S. parent-buyer households.**

*This is the ceiling the concept references while explicitly underwriting to a fraction of it.*

### 2.4 SAM — the serviceable available market

**SAM = anxious parents whose child is already exposed to AI chatbots** (the book is not abstract for them). Apply verified penetration to the target cohort:

| Layer | Verified rate | Applied to ~21–22M teens (13–17) | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Teens who have **ever used** an AI chatbot | **64%** | ~13.5–14M teens | [Pew, Dec 2025](https://www.pewresearch.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/20/2025/12/PI_2025.12.09_Teens-Social-Media-AI_REPORT.pdf) |
| Teens using AI chatbots **daily** | **~28–30%** | ~6–6.6M teens | [Pew, Dec 2025](https://www.pewresearch.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/20/2025/12/PI_2025.12.09_Teens-Social-Media-AI_REPORT.pdf); [NBC News](https://www.nbcnews.com/tech/social-media/28-us-teens-say-use-ai-chatbots-daily-poll-says-rcna248133) |
| Teens using chatbots for **emotional support/advice** | **12%** | ~2.6M teens | [Pew, Dec 2025](https://www.pewresearch.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/20/2025/12/PI_2025.12.09_Teens-Social-Media-AI_REPORT.pdf) |
| Teens using chatbots for **casual conversation** | **16%** | ~3.4M teens | [Pew, Dec 2025](https://www.pewresearch.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/20/2025/12/PI_2025.12.09_Teens-Social-Media-AI_REPORT.pdf) |

**The layered read (this is the honest part).** The "exposed ring" is large — roughly two-thirds of teens use chatbots at all — and *that ring's parents* are the SAM, sold on **anticipatory anxiety** ("your kid could fall in"). Inside it sits the smaller **intimacy core**: **~12–16% of all teens** on Pew's clean measure (up to ~⅓ of "companion users" on Common Sense's softest self-report, whose "AI companion" definition folds in general-purpose ChatGPT/Claude — [Common Sense Media, Jul 2025](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/research/talk-trust-and-trade-offs-how-and-why-teens-use-ai-companions)). The book sells to the *exposed ring's* parents on anticipation and serves the *intimacy core's* parents on triage. **The 10–12 preteen slice inside the target is empirically unmeasured for emotional/companion use; no quantitative claim is made about it.**

- **SAM (exposed-ring parents) ≈ ~10–13 million households** *[ESTIMATE — parent-households mapped to the ~64% ever-exposed teen population, collapsed to household buying units].*
- **SAM (intimacy-core parents) ≈ ~2–3 million households** *[ESTIMATE — mapped from the 12–16% Pew intimacy-use rate].*

### 2.5 SOM — the realistic addressable-reader / unit range

**SOM = the units this specific book can realistically sell.** Book-market conversion of even a large anxious audience is a low single-digit fraction, disciplined by three facts from `04`: a forthcoming frontlist competitor (Turkle), a possibly-cooling self-help category, and — decisively — **the transfer question in Part 1**. The realistic unit outcome is therefore a *function of the author secured*, not of the TAM.

Anchoring to the comp-derived underwriting band (**Turkle-scale 75k–250k**, per `04` §7 risk 4 and [`phaseB_finalize.json`](research/phaseB_finalize.json)) and cross-checking against the SAM:

| Scenario | Implied conversion of ~10–13M exposed-ring SAM | **Net unit range (life-of-title)** | What it assumes about the author |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Conservative** | ~0.4–0.6% | **~50,000–75,000** | Modest platform; weak/partial transfer; competes into a cool category |
| **Base** | ~0.9–1.3% | **~100,000–150,000** | A genuine platform co-author transfers; ships close behind Turkle; solid reviews |
| **Optimistic** | ~1.8–2.5% | **~200,000–250,000+** | Strong transferring platform + Turkle-adjacent positioning + sustained news cycle |

*[All three columns are ESTIMATES. The percentages are illustrative conversion rates applied to the §2.4 SAM, bounded by the comp-anchored 75k–250k band; they are not forecasts.]* The book is explicitly **not** underwritten to *Anxious Generation* scale, whose "2M+ copies" is a publisher marketing figure, not audited BookScan ([PRH](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/729231/the-anxious-generation-by-jonathan-haidt/)), and which ran on a pre-existing mass platform this book does not yet have.

### 2.6 Category context (for scale, not as the funnel's basis)

The U.S. book market is large and grew in 2024: total publishing revenue **$32.5 billion**, with **Trade (consumer) books at $21.2 billion**, up 4.4% ([AAP StatShot Annual Report, 2024](https://publishers.org/news/aap-statshot-annual-report-publishing-revenues-totaled-32-5-billion-for-calendar-year-2024/); [Publishing Perspectives](https://publishingperspectives.com/2025/08/association-of-american-publishers/)). This confirms a healthy trade market but is **scene-setting only** — it is not used to inflate the funnel. The narrower "self-help/parenting" category figures ($813M, 15,000 titles/yr) remain **unconfirmed SEO-aggregator numbers** and are not relied on here, consistent with `04` §2c; note also the **volatile, possibly cooling** self-help signal (self-help units down 26.3% YoY in Q1 2026 per secondary Circana reporting, `04` §2c) — a reason to position as narrative-nonfiction-with-a-playbook, not pure self-help.

### 3.4 Advance range — benchmarked to Turkle scale (NOT Haidt scale)

Advances are conventionally set at roughly **one-half to full projected life-of-title royalty earnings**, with the house wanting the book to earn out near its expected sales. Mapping the §3.2 earnings:

| If underwritten to… | Projected royalty earnings | **Illustrative advance range [ESTIMATE]** |
|---|---|---|
| **Conservative (50–75k units)** | ~$190k–$205k | **~$100,000–$175,000** |
| **Base (100–150k units)** | ~$385k–$415k | **~$200,000–$325,000** |
| **Optimistic (200–250k units)** | ~$700k–$740k | **~$400,000–$550,000** |

- **Recommended structure:** a **base advance near the conservative-to-base band with sales escalators/bonuses** rather than a single large guarantee, precisely because the platform-transfer bet (Part 1) is unproven until the author is secured. This shares risk between house and author and lets the deal scale *up* if a strong transferring platform is signed.
- **Explicitly rejected:** a *Haidt*-scale advance (which would imply a 2M-copy underwriting). `04` §7 and [`phaseB_finalize.json`](research/phaseB_finalize.json) both direct underwriting to Turkle-scale 75k–250k; the "2M+" comp figure is publisher marketing, not audited, and rode a pre-existing mass platform.

### 3.5 What the model says

At the **base** scenario with a **base-band advance (~$250k–$300k)**, the title earns out near expected sales and contributes positively before overhead — a healthy, non-heroic trade-nonfiction outcome. The model breaks in two ways, both foreseeable: (a) a large advance paid on an *unproven* platform that then fails to transfer (the Part 1 risk — mitigated by escalators over guarantees), or (b) net units landing in the conservative band against a base-band advance. Neither is a topic problem; both are **author/transfer** problems, which is why Part 1 is the gating decision.

## Part 4 — How to read these numbers

Treat everything in Parts 2 and 3 as a **transparent, reactable model, not a forecast or a promise.** The population and book-market anchors are sourced and solid (Census, the federal *America's Children* table, AAP); the funnel *conversions*, the household mappings, the unit ranges, and the entire P&L are **labeled estimates built on stated assumptions**, and they will move — sometimes a lot — when those assumptions change. Two cautions dominate all others. First, the intimacy behavior at the book's emotional center is a **minority (~12–16% of teens)**, so the market case rests on **anticipatory anxiety across the whole exposed-parent cohort**, not on a mass current-crisis; any version of this model that sells to "most kids already have an AI boyfriend" is dishonest and a reviewer will gut it. Second, the unit and advance ranges are **conditional on the author** — the same manuscript sells at the conservative or the optimistic end depending almost entirely on whether a genuinely transferring platform co-author is secured, which is why that is the build precondition and the first acquisition decision, not the last. Underwrite to the conservative-to-base band, structure the advance with escalators, ship close behind Turkle, and let a strong author — if one is signed — pull the outcome up toward the optimistic column.
