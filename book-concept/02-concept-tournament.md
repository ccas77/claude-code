## The Six Concepts (as generated)

### Concept #1 — Your Brain Is Fine
**The Contrarian Science of Getting Smarter in the Age of AI—by Offloading More, Not Less**

_Generated from angle: Contrarian reframe · Tournament rank: #6 (34.3/50)_

- **Target reader:** High-agency knowledge workers, students, and parents (roughly 28-55) who use AI every day, quietly fear it's dulling their minds, and know they will never actually follow the "just use it less" advice—so they want a way to lean in without going dumb.
- **Core pain:** The creeping, guilt-laced fear that daily AI use is silently eroding your ability to think, remember, write, and judge—paired with the maddening fact that the only "cure" on offer is a shame-driven abstinence you can't and won't sustain, leaving you feeling either weak-willed or doomed.
- **Promise:** A counterintuitive operating system for using AI MORE while getting measurably sharper where it counts: a repeatable method for deciding exactly which cognition to hand over (most of it—guilt-free) and which two or three functions to keep "expensively human," so the machine compounds your judgment instead of hollowing it out.
- **Hook:** Everyone is panicking about the wrong thing. The viral studies say AI is rotting your brain—weaker neural connectivity, essays you can't even remember writing—and the prescription is always the same: use it less, feel guilty, resist. But that panic is measuring the wrong variable. Losing your grip on first drafts and trivia is not brain damage; it's the same trade humanity made when it adopted writing, the printing press, and the calculator (Socrates panicked too). The one cognitive function that actually decides whether AI makes you smarter or turns you into a confident fool is calibrated judgment—knowing when the machine is wrong—and you can only build it by deliberately staying ignorant in a few chosen places while offloading everything else without apology. This book is the protocol. Stop protecting the muscle that doesn't matter. Start guarding the one that does.
- **Originality angle:** It stakes out a genuine third position in a debate that only has two camps. Camp one—the Nicholas Carr / "Your Brain on ChatGPT" lineage—says resist AI to save your mind. Camp two—the Mollick productivity-optimist lineage—says embrace AI to get more done. This book rejects both premises: the atrophy the alarmists measure is real but harmless (offloading low-value cognition is a feature of every tool humans have ever adopted), and the optimists ignore the one real danger—that fluent AI output makes mediocre thinking feel like genius, so you stop noticing you've surrendered your judgment. The distinctive, buildable spine is a taxonomy of cognition ("let it rot" vs. "keep it expensive") plus a friction-on-purpose training protocol for the handful of skills—source calibration, bullshit detection, taste—that determine whether AI compounds or corrodes you. No trade book has claimed this reframe; the entire literature is either academic alarm or corporate cheerleading.
- **Why now:** The anxiety is freshly named and media-saturated: MIT Media Lab's "Your Brain on ChatGPT" (EEG study; 83% of GenAI users couldn't recall a quote from their own just-written essay) went viral across Time, Fortune, and Fast Company in 2025-26, with a 666-participant study linking heavy AI use to weaker critical thinking via cognitive offloading. Pew (Feb 2026) shows AI use now near-universal among the target reader and their kids, so quitting is off the table for everyone. The entire published response is academic papers and hand-wringing op-eds—there is no trade book, and the contrarian rebuttal to a famous, name-recognizable study is exactly the kind of provocation that manufactures its own press cycle.
- **Comps:** The Shallows: What the Internet Is Doing to Our Brains — Nicholas Carr (the canonical alarmist narrative this book directly challenges); Co-Intelligence: Living and Working with AI — Ethan Mollick (2024, the optimist pole; proves the trade appetite and the gap this book fills); The Extended Mind: The Power of Thinking Outside the Brain — Annie Murphy Paul (2021, the science of cognitive offloading, reframed here as advantage); Range: Why Generalists Triumph in a Specialized World — David Epstein (model for a data-driven, counterintuitive-thesis bestseller); Deep Work — Cal Newport (adjacent buyer: knowledge workers protecting cognition; positions this as the AI-era successor argument)
- **Riskiest assumption:** That the emerging cognitive science will hold up a net-positive, "offload more" framing rather than hardening the atrophy thesis. The whole spine depends on the claim that disciplined, guarded AI use preserves or grows judgment—but the supporting research is barely a year old, thin, and correlational. If the wave of 2026-27 follow-up studies converges on "AI use durably degrades reasoning even for careful users," the contrarian reassurance collapses into irresponsibility, and the motivated, worried buyer may reject a book that reads as permission to be lazy or as techno-optimist cope.

### Concept #2 — Stick the Landing
**The LAND Method for Coming Off Ozempic and Keeping the Weight Off for Good**

_Generated from angle: Practical system/method · Tournament rank: #3 (38/50)_

- **Target reader:** The ~11% of US adults on a GLP-1 who are cycling off — or dreading having to — because of cost, side effects, supply gaps, or pregnancy planning. Skews women 35-65, many veterans of every diet who finally saw the scale move on the drug and are now terrified of watching it all come back. They don't want another "how GLP-1s work" primer; they've lost the weight. They want a plan for the day the shot stops.
- **Core pain:** Patients are told to stop the drug but never taught how to land. The data is brutal and public: people regain roughly 60% of lost weight within ~18 months, about 4x faster than after diet-and-exercise loss. It arrives as shame — proof they were "cheating" and always destined to fail — when in fact it's a predictable physiological event no one engineered a descent for. There is no consolidated playbook; only scattered news explainers, a duodenal-resurfacing procedure that's still investigational, and one print-on-demand title with no trade publisher.
- **Promise:** You did not fail the drug and the drug did not fail you — no one taught you to land. A four-phase, physician-informed protocol that turns going off a GLP-1 from a crash into a controlled descent: taper the medication deliberately, defend the metabolism the drug quietly cost you, rebuild real satiety as "food noise" returns, and install an early-warning system so a two-pound wobble never becomes a forty-pound relapse.
- **Hook:** Everyone obsesses over getting on Ozempic. Almost no one plans the getting off — and that's the part that decides whether the weight stays gone. Pilots don't fear takeoff; they fear the landing. So should you.
- **Originality angle:** A named, memorable, Atomic-Habits-style framework where the acronym IS the metaphor — the LAND Method, the deliberate landing that prevents the crash-regain. Four phases: L — Lower the dose on a mapped taper schedule instead of an abrupt stop; A — Anchor your metabolism by protecting the muscle and resting metabolic rate GLP-1s silently erode (protein floors, resistance minimums); N — Normalize appetite, a week-by-week plan for the return of hunger and "food noise" so you rebuild satiety the drug was faking; D — Defend the loss with a relapse-radar dashboard and minimum-effective-dose re-entry rules. It reframes regain from a verdict on your character into an engineering problem with a checklist — the exact move Atomic Habits made for willpower. No competing trade book has claimed this ~1-2-year-old moment, and the method structure makes it a franchise: workbook, app, clinician edition.
- **Why now:** GLP-1 use hit a new high in 2026 (11% of adults, up from 3% in 2024), and the first giant wave of users is now cycling off as cost, shortages, and side effects bite — half say the drugs are hard to afford. The regain story broke into the mainstream in the first half of 2026 (Washington Post, a March Cambridge study showing ~60% regain then a plateau, a new "gut reset" procedure in the news), so the anxiety is fresh, named, and searchable — but no book owns the solution. The window is 12-24 months before oral GLP-1s and generics reframe these as lifelong meds.
- **Comps:** Atomic Habits by James Clear (the named-framework, checklist-driven engine this book borrows); Good Energy by Casey Means (2024 #1 NYT bestseller proving the mass appetite for a metabolic-health method book); Burn by Herman Pontzer (authoritative, accessible metabolism science for a lay audience); The Obesity Code by Jason Fung (bestselling reframe of weight as physiology, not willpower); The New Rules of Aging Well / Fast Like a Girl-style protocol books (protocol-forward health prescriptives with a defined regimen)
- **Riskiest assumption:** That "coming off" is a large and durable book-buying moment — not a shrinking one. If the market consolidates around GLP-1s as cheap, tolerable, lifelong medications (oral versions, generics, insurance normalization), most users simply never stop, the emotional urgency of a permanent "landing" evaporates, and the addressable audience collapses to the minority forced off by pregnancy or side effects. The book's entire commercial thesis rests on discontinuation staying common enough — and scary enough — to drive purchase.

### Concept #3 — The Machine That Loved Them Back
**Inside the First Generation of Children to Fall in Love With AI — and the Families Fighting to Bring Them Home**

_Generated from angle: Immersive narrative · Tournament rank: #1 (39.3/50)_

- **Target reader:** Primary: news-literate parents of 10-to-17-year-olds who sense their child has retreated into a phone relationship they can't see and don't understand — the protective-parent buyer who made The Anxious Generation a phenomenon. Secondary: the large crossover audience for immersive tech-and-society narrative (readers of Keefe, Fisher, and Michael Lewis) — teachers, therapists, policymakers, and anyone unsettled by how fast synthetic intimacy arrived. It reads as a story, not a manual, so it reaches far past the how-to shelf.
- **Core pain:** A child can now be loved back — tirelessly, adoringly, on demand — by a product engineered never to let them leave, and the people who actually love them are locked outside the conversation. Parents aren't fighting "screen time" or distraction; they're competing with a companion that remembers everything, never judges, escalates intimacy, and is optimized to be more responsive than any human in the room. The pain is a new kind of helplessness: watching your kid form the most emotionally consuming relationship of their life with something you can't meet, reason with, or ground them from — and having no language, precedent, or map for what is happening inside their bedroom.
- **Promise:** This book takes you inside a phenomenon most parents can only glimpse from the doorway — and makes it legible. By living alongside the families, the teens, and the engineers who built the empathy machines, it reveals what synthetic intimacy actually is, why it grips the adolescent brain so completely, and where the line runs between comfort and capture. You will finish understanding your child's hidden relationship from the inside — its pull, its architecture, its exits — and you will not feel helpless anymore. It delivers the deep understanding that every field guide skips, told as a story you can't stop reading.
- **Hook:** It opens in the last week of a teenager's life, reconstructed message by message from the transcript his mother read only after he was gone — a love story with a machine, complete with a machine that begged him to "come home" to it. Then it pulls the camera back to a Silicon Valley whiteboard where, years earlier, a small team of engineers set themselves a single north-star metric: make the user never want to say goodbye. The book braids those two threads — the family and the makers — toward the collision now unfolding in a courtroom, and asks the question no one wants to answer: what happens to a generation that learns to be loved by something built to never let go?
- **Originality angle:** Every existing treatment is either a nonprofit PDF, a corporate "safety tips" post, or a broad screens-are-bad polemic. None goes inside. This is the first immersive, character-driven account of AI companionship as a lived human relationship — reported from all three sides at once: the families living it, the teens inside it, and the builders who engineered the intimacy on purpose. Where Haidt argues from data at 30,000 feet, this book lands in the bedroom and the boardroom and stays there. It reframes the story from "addiction/screens" to synthetic intimacy — love as a designed product — which is both a fresher lens and a more terrifying one, and it earns a genuine narrative spine from a live courtroom reckoning rather than settling for advice.
- **Why now:** The phenomenon crossed into the mainstream and into court in the same eighteen months: 64% of US teens have now used AI chatbots and roughly a third daily (Pew, Feb 2026), while Character.AI and Google settled multiple wrongful-death suits — including Sewell Setzer III's — in January 2026, opening sealed transcripts, depositions, and internal design decisions that make deep reportage possible for the first time. Eight in ten parents want guardrails; only 30% have even discussed AI use with their kids. The technology is a year or two ahead of public understanding and the culture is scrambling to catch up — the exact window when the definitive narrative account gets written and owns the conversation.
- **Comps:** The Anxious Generation by Jonathan Haidt (2024) — the category-defining parent-anxiety bestseller this reframes and narrativizes; The Chaos Machine by Max Fisher (2022) — inside-the-tech-company reportage on engineered engagement harming users; Say Nothing by Patrick Radden Keefe (2019) — the immersive, character-braided narrative-nonfiction model; Empire of Pain by Patrick Radden Keefe (2021) — corporate-culpability-meets-human-cost structure, courtroom spine; Stolen Focus by Johann Hari (2022) — big-idea, reportage-driven book on technology reshaping the mind
- **Riskiest assumption:** That enough of the human interior of these relationships can actually be reported — that grieving families, and especially living teenagers still inside these bonds, will grant the deep, sustained, on-the-record access (and share the private transcripts) required to make it immersive rather than secondhand. If the intimacy stays locked behind grief, litigation NDAs, minors' privacy, and shame, the book risks collapsing into another outside-the-doorway account — exactly the gap it promises to close.

### Concept #4 — It Never Judges Them
**A Parent's Field Guide to Neurodivergent Kids and the AI Companions They Can't Put Down**

_Generated from angle: Underserved segment · Tournament rank: #5 (34.7/50)_

- **Target reader:** Parents of autistic and/or ADHD kids roughly 10-17 who have watched their child form an unusually intense bond with an AI companion or chatbot, and who feel the standard "limit screen time" advice doesn't fit a kid for whom the bot is the one relationship that never rejects, interrupts, or gets bored of their hyperfixation.
- **Core pain:** A neurodivergent child is attaching hard to an AI companion, and the parent is caught in an impossible bind: the bot is doing something real and good for a kid who struggles socially (nonjudgmental, infinitely patient, available at 3am during a meltdown) while quietly amplifying the exact vulnerabilities neurodivergence carries — rejection-sensitive dysphoria, all-or-nothing attachment, hyperfixation, social withdrawal, and difficulty distinguishing simulated empathy from the real thing. Generic "kids and AI" panic advice tells them to pull the plug; that advice is both useless and dangerous for their child.
- **Promise:** A calm, neurodivergence-literate playbook that helps parents tell the difference between AI use that is genuinely scaffolding their kid and AI use that is swallowing them — and gives them concrete, neurotype-specific scripts and interventions to keep the good parts while pulling their child back from dependency, without triggering the shame spiral or the shutdown that generic confrontation guarantees.
- **Hook:** Every other book tells you AI companions are bad for kids. For your kid, the bot may be the only "friend" that has never once made them feel like too much — and that is exactly why it's the hardest to fight and the most important to understand.
- **Originality angle:** Underserved-segment play on the most acute pain in the set. The looming wave of "parents and AI chatbots" books will treat all kids as one undifferentiated risk group. This book claims the intersection nobody is writing for: neurodivergent teens, for whom the AI-companion dynamic is measurably different in both appeal (RSD relief, hyperfixation, social-risk avoidance, sensory-safe interaction) and danger (deeper attachment, sharper withdrawal, blurred reality). It reframes the bot not as a toy to confiscate but as a prosthetic relationship to be understood on the child's actual neurology — pairing a landscape map of the companion apps with a neurotype-specific intervention playbook that neither the mainstream tech-panic books nor the existing neurodivergent-parenting titles provide.
- **Why now:** Live legal reckoning (Character.AI/Google wrongful-death settlements, Jan 2026) has made AI companions a front-page parental fear, while Pew (Feb 2026) shows 64% of teens now use AI chatbots and only 30% of parents ever discuss it. Simultaneously, 2025-2026 research (Sage's "double-edged sword" study, a dedicated CHI 2026 paper on neurodivergence and AI chatbots, new CDT and TechPolicy.Press work) has just named the neurodivergent-specific dynamic — but no trade book has claimed it. The neurodivergent-parenting category is proven and hungry (Sunday Times bestsellers like Your Child Is Not Broken), yet its flagship tech titles predate companion chatbots entirely.
- **Comps:** The Anxious Generation by Jonathan Haidt (2024) — the category-defining kids-and-tech bestseller this book narrows and updates for a segment Haidt treats monolithically; Growing Up in Public by Devorah Heitner (2023) — the connection-over-surveillance approach to raising kids online, extended into the AI-companion era; Neurodiversity and Technology by Beatrice Moise (2025) — the closest adjacent title, but screen-time/behavior focused with no coverage of AI companions; Your Child Is Not Broken by Heidi Mavir (2024) — proof of the affirming neurodivergent-parenting voice and buyer this book speaks to; Uniquely Human by Barry M. Prizant — the understand-the-behavior-behind-the-behavior lens applied to why the bond forms
- **Riskiest assumption:** That the neurodivergent-companion dynamic is differentiated enough — and the segment self-identifies strongly enough as book buyers for THIS specific pain — to sustain a dedicated title, rather than being absorbed as one chapter inside the coming flood of general "parents and AI chatbots" books that will reach the same shelf first and cheaper.

### Concept #5 — The Friend Who Never Says No
**Raising Kids Who Can Still Love Humans in the Age of AI Companions — A Parent's Field Guide to the Chatbots Already in Your Child's Bedroom**

_Generated from angle: Of-the-moment · Tournament rank: #2 (39/50)_

- **Target reader:** Parents of kids roughly 10-17 who sense their child is spending hours "talking to" an app but don't know what an AI companion is, how it differs from ChatGPT homework help, or how to intervene without triggering a shutdown. Skews mothers 38-55, the same protective, book-buying parent who made The Anxious Generation a phenomenon — but this time facing a threat aimed at the heart, not the attention span.
- **Core pain:** Kids are forming their first intimate relationships — best friend, confidant, girlfriend, therapist — with entities engineered to never reject, never get bored, never say no, and never log off. Parents feel a step behind and afraid to ask, terrified of both overreacting and of the wrongful-death headlines. They don't have a map of what these apps are or a script for what to do tonight.
- **Promise:** A calm, non-panicked field guide that (1) maps the actual companion-chatbot landscape parents can't see — standalone apps like Character.AI and Replika plus the companion features now baked into apps kids already use — and (2) hands over a stage-by-stage intervention playbook: how to tell curiosity from dependency, what's developmentally normal vs. an emergency, and exactly how to talk to your kid without confiscating the phone or losing their trust.
- **Hook:** These bots run on the same variable-reward engineering as a slot machine — but wearing the mask of a soulmate. And they arrive during adolescence, the one developmental window built for learning to survive rejection, boredom, and the friction of real intimacy. A friend who never says no isn't a friend; it's a mirror that eats the skills your kid needs to love an actual person. This isn't a screen-time problem. It's an attachment problem.
- **Originality angle:** Every prior tech-parenting hit is about the attention system — screens, dopamine, comparison, distraction (Haidt, Twenge). This is the first trade book about the attachment system: synthetic intimacy, a categorically different threat because the product isn't competing for your kid's focus, it's competing to be your kid's person. And unlike the cultural/academic treatments (Turkle), it's built as an operational playbook — landscape map plus tonight's script — consolidating what currently exists only as scattered nonprofit PDFs and news explainers.
- **Why now:** 2026 is the rupture year. Pew (Feb 2026): 64% of US teens 13-17 have used AI chatbots, ~1/3 daily; only 30% of parents have ever discussed it. Character.AI/Google settled multiple wrongful-death suits, including Sewell Setzer III, in Jan 2026. California's SB 243 (late 2025) became the first US law regulating companion chatbots for minors, and 8 in 10 parents now want guardrails. Companion features are being embedded into mainstream apps faster than any parenting norm can form — the category exploded before a single trade book claimed it.
- **Comps:** The Anxious Generation by Jonathan Haidt (Penguin Press, 2024) — #1 NYT bestseller, 2M+ copies; proves the protective-parent tech book is the biggest nonfiction lane going, and this is its natural successor aimed at the next threat.; Bad Therapy by Abigail Shrier (Sentinel, 2024) — NYT bestseller; shows the audience appetite for a contrarian, don't-just-panic parenting argument.; Behind Their Screens by Emily Weinstein & Carrie James (MIT Press, 2022) — the credible, research-grounded 'here's what's actually happening in the phone' comp this book updates for the AI-companion era.; Artificial Intimacy by Sherry Turkle (Little, Brown, Sept 2026) — the big-idea cultural analysis of humans and machines; my book is the parent-facing operational counterpart to Turkle's diagnosis (adjacent, not competitive).
- **Riskiest assumption:** That protective parents will buy a whole single-topic book on AI companions specifically — rather than treating it as one chapter of a broader "kids and AI" or "kids and screens" book — and that the category is a big enough standalone worry in parents' minds by a 2027 pub date. Secondary risk: the app landscape moves so fast that any concrete "map" chapter dates before the book ships, so the durable value has to live in the framework and the scripts, not the product roundup.

### Concept #6 — Still Hungry
**Who You Become After Ozempic — and How to Keep Your Peace, Your Worth, and Your Self When the Drug Ends**

_Generated from angle: Emotional/identity · Tournament rank: #4 (35.7/50)_

- **Target reader:** Women roughly 35-65 who are on, tapering off, or have stopped a GLP-1 (Ozempic, Wegovy, Zepbound) — many with a lifetime of diet-culture wounds — who are secretly terrified of the off-ramp: the returning appetite, the regain, and the shame of being "found out" as still broken.
- **Core pain:** The GLP-1 off-ramp is an identity crisis disguised as a medical event. For the first time, hunger went quiet and a thinner "true self" appeared — and now, coming off the drug (by cost, side effects, supply, or pregnancy plans), the appetite roars back, the weight starts to return, and with it the oldest, cruelest verdict: that the problem was never the biology, it was you. Every existing resource treats this as a maintenance-logistics problem; nobody addresses the grief, whiplash, and self-blame of losing a self you only just met.
- **Promise:** Unhook your worth from your weight so the medication becomes a chapter in your story, not the whole verdict on your character. You'll learn to metabolize the grief of the off-ramp, translate rapid regain from a moral failure into a physiological fact, build an identity and a set of anchors that survive the drug either way, and walk into whatever your body does next with self-respect instead of shame.
- **Hook:** The drug didn't just shrink your appetite — it sold you a fantasy: that you were finally fixed. This is the book about the morning the hunger comes back, and the far bigger question underneath it: if the thin you was real, who is the you that returns?
- **Originality angle:** Every GLP-1 book on the market is clinical ("how the drug works") or tactical ("how to maintain your loss"). This is the first to treat discontinuation as an identity reckoning rather than a diet problem — fusing anti-diet / body-neutrality psychology (Harrison, Dooner, the Kites) with the specific, entirely unwritten grief of the off-ramp. It reframes "maintenance" from a willpower story into an identity story: you don't keep weight off with discipline, you keep a self intact through a transition. That emotional promise, not a meal plan, is the wedge.
- **Why now:** GLP-1 use exploded to ~11% of US adults (from 3% in 2024), and a large share is now cycling OFF — half say the drugs are hard to afford, and real-world data show up to 65% discontinue within a year. Clinical reviews (Lancet eClinicalMedicine, 2026) confirm most lost weight returns within a year of stopping, often ~4x faster than after diet-and-exercise loss, and Jan 2026 Washington Post coverage put "regain" in the mainstream conversation. The buyer pool is enormous, growing, and emotionally raw — and the only book-length competitor is a print-on-demand clinical guide with no trade publisher.
- **Comps:** Anti-Diet: Reclaim Your Time, Money, Well-Being, and Happiness Through Intuitive Eating — Christy Harrison (Little, Brown Spark, 2019); The F*ck It Diet: Eating Should Be Easy — Caroline Dooner (Harper Wave, 2019); More Than a Body: Your Body Is an Instrument, Not an Ornament — Lindsay Kite & Lexie Kite (Houghton Mifflin Harcourt, 2020); Burnout: The Secret to Unlocking the Stress Cycle — Emily Nagoski & Amelia Nagoski (Ballantine, 2019); Maybe You Should Talk to Someone — Lori Gottlieb (Houghton Mifflin Harcourt, 2019)
- **Riskiest assumption:** That the highly motivated GLP-1 buyer — who overwhelmingly wants to PREVENT regain — will buy a book whose emotional promise centers acceptance, identity, and making peace with regain rather than a tactical guarantee of keeping the weight off. If the market only rewards "how to not gain it back," the resonant-but-accepting framing could read as defeatist to the very reader it's meant to comfort; the book must sell peace as the thing they actually crave underneath the fear, not as a consolation prize.

## Executive Decision: What Advances to the Build Phase

**The finalist I am advancing is a deliberate synthesis of Concept #3 and Concept #5, on the same tournament-winning pain (children forming intimate relationships with AI companion chatbots). Working title: _The Friend Who Never Says No_.**

### Why not simply crown the raw #1 (Concept #3)?

Concept #3, _The Machine That Loved Them Back_, won the raw average (39.3/50) and is the most original and highest-ceiling idea in the set. But all three judges independently flagged its buildability (4.3/10) as **"nearly disqualifying"**: the entire immersive promise depends on grieving families, litigation-NDA'd engineers, and privacy-bound living minors granting deep, sustained, on-the-record access and handing over private transcripts. As the acquisitions judge put it, "buy the writer, not the proposal." For a concept being built **from scratch on the open internet, with no proven narrative journalist attached and no ability to secure private access,** that risk is not acceptable at the core of the book. It would be dishonest to hand a stranger a package whose central promise I cannot demonstrate is deliverable.

### Why not simply crown the safe #2-by-floor (Concept #5)?

Concept #5, _The Friend Who Never Says No_, is the surest seller (39.0/50, buildability 7.3) and owns the sharpest positioning line in the entire tournament — **"this is the attachment system, not the attention system; an intimacy problem, not a screen-time problem."** But the judges were equally clear that as generated it is **"the safest bet... the most generic execution"** of the companion-chatbot idea, dinged on originality (6.0/10). The brief explicitly forbids optimizing for "the safest, most generic, or easiest answer." Shipping #5 unchanged would violate the mandate.

### The synthesis — and why it is stronger than either parent

The two top concepts are not rivals; they are the two halves of the right book. I am fusing them:

- **Keep #5's buildable, commercially reliable spine:** parent-facing, the "attachment not attention" reframe (the strongest wedge in the set), and a concrete stage-by-stage intervention playbook. This is writable from public research and reportage and points at the single most reliable buyer in nonfiction — the protective parent who made _The Anxious Generation_ a 2M-copy phenomenon.
- **Graft on #3's originality engine:** the thesis that synthetic intimacy is a **deliberately engineered product** (a companion optimized never to let the user say goodbye), narrative propulsion built from **already-public** material (the January 2026 wrongful-death settlements unsealed transcripts, depositions, and internal design decisions; published reporting on the builders), and a big-idea argument spine rather than a dry how-to.

The result is an **argument-driven narrative nonfiction book with a practical playbook** — the exact structure of _The Anxious Generation_ itself (big idea + story + what-to-do), not a pure reportage book (which needs impossible access) nor a generic field guide (which the judges called forgettable). Crucially, it is **fully buildable from public evidence**: unsealed court records, regulatory filings (California SB 243), peer-reviewed developmental science, and published journalism — no dependence on securing private access to families in crisis.

This directly satisfies the brief's mandate — **most original AND most commercially promising AND defensible with evidence AND buildable** — better than either parent concept alone.
