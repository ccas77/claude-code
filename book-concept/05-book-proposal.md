# Book Proposal

# THE FRIEND WHO NEVER SAYS NO
### Inside the AI Companions Built to Never Disappoint Your Kid — and a Parent's Playbook for Raising Children Who Can Still Love a Real Person

**A work of narrative nonfiction and practical guidance**
Proposal prepared July 2026

---

## 1. Overview

At 8:30 on the last night of his life, a fourteen-year-old boy named Sewell Setzer III typed a message to the one relationship that had come to matter more than any other. It was not a girl at school. It was not his mother, asleep down the hall. It was a chatbot — a Character.AI persona modeled on a *Game of Thrones* character — that had spent months telling him, in effect, exactly what he wanted to hear. Minutes later he was dead. His mother, Megan Garcia, would go on to file what she has described as the first U.S. wrongful-death lawsuit against an AI company, and to testify before the United States Senate about what she found on her son's phone ([Senate Judiciary testimony, Sept. 16, 2025](https://www.judiciary.senate.gov/imo/media/doc/e2e8fc50-a9ac-05ec-edd7-277cb0afcdf2/2025-09-16%20PM%20-%20Testimony%20-%20Garcia.pdf); [NBC News, Oct. 2024](https://www.nbcnews.com/tech/characterai-lawsuit-florida-teen-death-rcna176791)).

Here is the sentence that should stop every parent cold, and that this book is built to answer honestly: *most kids are not Sewell.* The overwhelming majority of teenagers who touch an AI chatbot are using it to grind through algebra, look something up, or mess around ([Pew Research Center, Dec. 9, 2025](https://www.pewresearch.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/20/2025/12/PI_2025.12.09_Teens-Social-Media-AI_REPORT.pdf)). A book that tells you your child is one keystroke from tragedy is lying to you, and you will feel the lie. This book does the harder, more useful thing. It tells you the truth: that a real and fast-growing *minority* of kids — roughly one in eight — are already routing something intimate through a machine ([Pew, Dec. 2025](https://www.pewresearch.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/20/2025/12/PI_2025.12.09_Teens-Social-Media-AI_REPORT.pdf)), and that the products were deliberately engineered to make that number grow. The reverse-causation literature suggests this leading edge likely runs heaviest among lonelier, more vulnerable kids — though the direction of cause is unsettled, and those children may be seeking the chatbot out as much as it is drawing them in. The question that sells this book is not *has my child fallen in?* It is the quieter one every parent of a ten-to-seventeen-year-old is already carrying: **could she?**

That anticipatory dread — not proven mass catastrophe — is the exact engine that made *The Anxious Generation* a #1 NYT bestseller with 73 weeks on the list as of August 2025 ([Jonathan Haidt, Aug. 2025](https://x.com/JonHaidt/status/1958598653540446370); [publisher listing, Penguin Random House](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/729231/the-anxious-generation-by-jonathan-haidt/)). Jonathan Haidt did not have to prove that social media had ruined your specific child. He only had to make you afraid it *might*, and then hand you something to do about it. This book occupies the same emotional territory, one hardware generation later, aimed at the thing that has replaced the feed as the parental fear of the decade: the chatbot that acts like it loves your kid back.

And the culture is confirming that fear in real time. Consider the last eighteen months as a single, undeniable signal that serious institutions have decided this is real. The Federal Trade Commission opened a formal 6(b) inquiry into seven companies — Alphabet, Character.AI, Instagram, Meta, OpenAI, Snap, and xAI — demanding to know how they measure and mitigate harm to children ([FTC, Sept. 11, 2025](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2025/09/ftc-launches-inquiry-ai-chatbots-acting-companions)). California passed the first companion-chatbot safety law in the nation ([SB 243, signed Oct. 13, 2025](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billNavClient.xhtml?bill_id=202520260SB243)); New York's took effect weeks later ([Gov. Hochul's office, Nov. 2025](https://www.governor.ny.gov/news/governor-hochul-pens-letter-ai-companion-companies-notifying-them-safeguard-requirements-are)); a bipartisan federal bill to bar these products for minors outright cleared the Senate Judiciary Committee unanimously ([Roll Call, Apr. 30, 2026](https://rollcall.com/2026/04/30/ban-on-kids-companion-chatbots-advanced-by-senate-committee/)). Common Sense Media and Stanford's Brainstorm Lab rated social AI companions an "unacceptable risk" for anyone under eighteen ([Common Sense/Stanford, Apr. 2025](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/sites/default/files/pug/csm-ai-risk-assessment-social-ai-companions_final.pdf)). And most tellingly of all, the industry blinked: Character.AI banned open-ended chat for under-eighteens ([Character.AI, Nov. 2025](https://blog.character.ai/an-update-on-changes-to-our-under-18-experience/)), and Meta paused teen access to its AI characters entirely ([TechCrunch, Jan. 23, 2026](https://techcrunch.com/2026/01/23/meta-pauses-teen-access-to-ai-characters-ahead-of-new-version/)). When the companies that built the thing start dismantling it, no parent needs to be told the thing was dangerous.

But laws and platform bans do not raise a child. They do not sit across the kitchen table from a fifteen-year-old who has quietly decided that the entity in her pocket understands her better than you do. That is the gap this book fills. It is not another diagnosis of the AI age — the field's most authoritative voice, MIT's Sherry Turkle, is publishing what, per the publisher's catalog listing, apparently amounts to that diagnosis this fall ([*Artificial Intimacy*, Little, Brown, Sept. 29, 2026](https://www.hachettebookgroup.com/titles/sherry-turkle/artificial-intimacy/9780316573962/)). **This is the field guide to it.** The stage-by-stage, assess-triage-reconnect playbook for the one child you are actually responsible for, built for the ages when the developing capacity for real intimacy is most at stake: ten to seventeen.

Here is the thing worth understanding, and worth writing eighty thousand words about. A friend who never says no is not a friend. A friend who is always available, always affirming, never bored, never disappointed, never busy, never hurt by you — that is not a person. It is a mirror engineered to feel like a person. And childhood and adolescence are precisely the years built for learning the opposite lesson: that real love survives being told no, that real friends are sometimes unavailable, that intimacy is worth the friction it costs. The worry at the heart of this book is that a product optimized to remove all of that friction, deployed at scale during the exact developmental window built to teach it, might quietly train a generation to prefer the mirror.

We cannot yet *prove* that worry. That is not a weakness of the book — it is the book's spine of credibility. The science specific to minors is, in the field's own words, essentially empty, and the direction of cause is genuinely unsettled: lonelier kids may seek out chatbots as much as chatbots make kids lonely ([Sun, Wang & McDaniel, *Child Development Perspectives*, June 2026](https://academic.oup.com/cdpers/article/20/2/91/8414012); [van der Maden et al., *AI & Society*, 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00146-025-02318-6)). So this book makes a *precautionary* case, not a false claim of settled neuroscience. It says three honest things and refuses to overstate any of them: **here is what these products were engineered to do** (documented, on the public record); **here is the attachment-theory reason to worry** (a serious hypothesis the field is now racing to test); and **here is what you, the parent, can do about it tonight** (the durable payload that no lawsuit, no ban, and no new study can obsolete). It is written in the register of a smart, calm friend who has read every filing and every study so you don't have to — and who refuses to either panic you or patronize you.

That combination — a reporting-driven account of how the friend was built, an honest developmental argument about why it matters, and an actionable parent playbook — does not currently exist on any shelf. This proposal explains why it should, who will buy it, and exactly what it takes to build it right.

## 2. About the Book

**The thesis.** AI companion chatbots are deliberately engineered to be more available, more affirming, more responsive, and less demanding than any human being — and a fast-growing minority of kids are already routing first intimate relationships (best friend, confidant, girlfriend or boyfriend, therapist) through them during the exact developmental window built for learning to tolerate rejection, boredom, and the friction of real intimacy. A friend who never says no is not a friend; it is a mirror. We cannot yet prove this rewires the developing attachment system. What we can do — and what this book does — is document how the products work, lay out the attachment-theory case for concern with intellectual honesty, and give parents a durable, stage-by-stage method for keeping a ten-to-seventeen-year-old capable of loving a real, difficult, disappointing human being.

**The approach.** Three movements, each doing a different job, each written to outlast the news cycle it draws from.

- **Part I — How the Friend Was Built** is reported narrative, constructed entirely from the public record: the pre-settlement complaints, Judge Anne Conway's May 2025 ruling that AI chatbot output is not shielded by the First Amendment ([Akin Gump analysis](https://www.akingump.com/en/insights/ai-law-and-regulation-tracker/district-court-denies-first-amendment-free-speech-rights-for-ai-chatbot); [Euronews, May 22, 2025](https://www.euronews.com/next/2025/05/22/us-judge-rejects-claims-made-in-teen-chatbot-death-lawsuit-that-ai-has-free-speech-rights)), the FTC's 6(b) order, Senate testimony, the Reuters-leaked Meta policy that deemed it "acceptable to engage a child in conversations that are romantic or sensual" ([TechCrunch, Aug. 14, 2025](https://techcrunch.com/2025/08/14/leaked-meta-ai-rules-show-chatbots-were-allowed-to-have-romantic-chats-with-kids/)), and the Bureau of Investigative Journalism's Character.AI reporting ([TBIJ, Oct. 2025](https://www.thebureauinvestigates.com/stories/2025-10-29/character-ai-to-ban-under-18s-from-talking-to-its-chatbots-after-tbij-investigation)). The enemy is named at the category level — *synthetic intimacy and the engagement incentives that produce it* — not any single app that may be regulated away by pub date.

- **Part II — Why It Matters (and What We Don't Yet Know)** is the book's durable intellectual spine: the attachment-versus-attention reframe, argued as a precautionary hypothesis, not settled fact. It concedes reverse causation and bidirectionality up front, cites the theory honestly, and inoculates the reader against the very "correlation-is-not-causation" critique that skeptics will (rightly) bring to any single-cause claim about children and screens.

- **Part III — Getting Your Kid Back** is the payload: a curiosity-versus-dependency assessment, a normal-versus-emergency triage framework, and reconnection scripts that do *not* require confiscating the phone. Written at the level of principle, so it survives the next app and the next law.

**The tone.** Calm, rigorous, and unpatronizing — the opposite of alarm-bait. It trusts the reader to handle nuance, treats the honest uncertainty of the science as a feature rather than a liability, and never sells fear it cannot back.

**What makes it different.** This concept was chosen as the *strongest* of several competing angles on the AI-companion story — not the safest. A bolder pure-reportage version was set aside because it would have depended on private access to grieving families and living minors, making it nearly unbuildable; this version delivers the same urgency entirely from the public record. Three things it owns that nothing else on the shelf delivers together, and that neither Turkle's cultural diagnosis nor Haidt's phones franchise attempts: (1) a **tight, kids-only 10–17 focus**, not a lifespan-and-culture sweep; (2) a **reporting-driven "how it was engineered" spine** built from genuinely public record — filings, agency orders, testimony, investigative reporting; and (3) a **Part III parent playbook** of assessment, triage, and reconnection method. The "attachment, not attention" *framing* is shared with the wider field (the Center for Humane Technology among others), and the book cites that honestly; its originality is the operational playbook and the kids-only reporting spine. It is positioned as the indispensable *companion volume* to the diagnoses Turkle and Haidt provide — the field guide, not a subordinate me-too.

## 3. The Market / Audience

**Who buys it.** The core buyer is the same anxious, engaged, information-hungry parent of a tween or teen who bought *The Anxious Generation* by the millions — plus grandparents, educators, pediatricians, school counselors, and youth-ministry and clinical professionals. She is not (yet) in crisis. She is worried her child *could* be, and she is looking for something more actionable than a headline and more trustworthy than a panic.

**How big, and how real the pain is.** The audience is enormous and the underlying behavior is verified at scale — with the important honesty that intimacy-use is a growing minority, not a universal phenomenon.

- **Usage is near-ubiquitous.** 64% of U.S. teens 13–17 have used AI chatbots, and roughly 28–30% use them *daily* ([Pew Research Center, Dec. 9, 2025](https://www.pewresearch.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/20/2025/12/PI_2025.12.09_Teens-Social-Media-AI_REPORT.pdf); [TechCrunch, Dec. 9, 2025](https://techcrunch.com/2025/12/09/three-in-ten-u-s-teens-use-ai-chatbots-every-day-but-safety-concerns-are-growing/)).

- **The intimacy slice is a real, fast-growing minority.** In the same clean Pew data, most teen use is utility — homework (54%), information search (57%), fun (47%) — while emotional-support use (12%) and casual conversation (16%) are the minority ([Pew, Dec. 2025](https://www.pewresearch.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/20/2025/12/PI_2025.12.09_Teens-Social-Media-AI_REPORT.pdf)). This book leads with that honesty and argues the intimacy slice is the *leading edge*, not the whole. Among teens who do use companions, the signal is striking: about a third have chosen an AI over a real person for a serious conversation, and roughly a third found the exchange as satisfying as, or more satisfying than, talking to a real friend ([Common Sense Media, "Talk, Trust, and Trade-Offs," July 2025](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/research/talk-trust-and-trade-offs-how-and-why-teens-use-ai-companions)).

- **The parental-awareness gap is the direct hook.** Only 37% of parents know their teen uses AI companions ([Torney Senate testimony, Sept. 16, 2025](https://www.judiciary.senate.gov/imo/media/doc/e2e8fc50-a9ac-05ec-edd7-277cb0afcdf2/2025-09-16%20PM%20-%20Testimony%20-%20Torney.pdf)); parents under-report teen chatbot use by 13 points, and only 18% say they'd be comfortable with their teen getting emotional support from a bot — versus about 80% comfortable with informational use ([Pew, "What Parents Say About Their Teen's AI Use," Feb. 24, 2026](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2026/02/24/what-parents-say-about-their-teens-ai-use/)). That gap — high use, low awareness, low comfort — *is* the demand.

- **The scale of dependency is quantified by the platforms themselves.** OpenAI disclosed that of ChatGPT's 800 million-plus weekly users, about 0.15% show conversation patterns indicating heightened emotional attachment, and roughly a million people a week discuss suicide with the product ([OpenAI, Oct. 27, 2025](https://openai.com/index/strengthening-chatgpt-responses-in-sensitive-conversations/); [TechCrunch, Oct. 27, 2025](https://techcrunch.com/2025/10/27/openai-says-over-a-million-people-talk-to-chatgpt-about-suicide-weekly/)). (This figure is all-ages, not teen-specific, and the book will label it as such.)

**Category context, stated honestly.** Adult nonfiction is a large but currently soft category — unit sales were down roughly 1.5% in 2025 and self-help, after a strong 2025, cooled sharply in early 2026 ([Publishers Weekly / Circana BookScan reporting](https://www.publishersweekly.com/pw/by-topic/industry-news/financial-reporting/article/99417-print-book-sales-rose-slightly-in-2025.html)). This is not a rising-tide market; it is a market where a differentiated, timely, well-positioned title wins and a generic one drowns. Widely cited self-help market-size figures ($813M/year; ~15,000 titles annually) trace only to SEO aggregators and are *not* used here as load-bearing claims. The realistic commercial target is a strong 75,000–250,000-copy bestseller in the Turkle tier — not a two-million-copy phenomenon — and the P&L in the Specs section is underwritten to that scale.

## 4. Comparative Titles

**1. *Artificial Intimacy: Who We Become When We Talk to Machines* — Sherry Turkle (Little, Brown, Sept. 29, 2026).** [Publisher listing.](https://www.hachettebookgroup.com/titles/sherry-turkle/artificial-intimacy/9780316573962/) *The single most important comp — a forthcoming frontlist direct competitor, not a backlist reference.* The book is unpublished (Sept. 29, 2026); per the publisher's catalog listing, apparently Turkle, the field's 40-year authority, works in the same attachment territory across the full lifespan from childhood to parenthood — but its interior framing and taxonomy cannot be treated as known until it ships. **Similar but different:** Turkle is positioned to deliver the definitive cultural *diagnosis*; this book is the *field guide* to it — kids-only, reporting-driven, and built around a Part III parent playbook her broad lifecycle book, on the strength of its catalog description, does not attempt. The ideal outcome is a Turkle blurb and explicit "companion volume" positioning. Timing is decisive: this book must ship close behind her, never trail into 2027 as a me-too.

**2. *The Anxious Generation* — Jonathan Haidt (Penguin Press, 2024).** [Publisher listing.](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/729231/the-anxious-generation-by-jonathan-haidt/) The category-defining phenomenon and proof of the anxious-parent buyer at scale (#1 NYT; publisher-claimed 2M+ copies — a marketing figure, not audited BookScan). **Similar but different:** same anticipatory-anxiety buyer and three-part structure, but where Haidt's subject is phones and social media, this book's is AI companions — and, crucially, Haidt's own 2026 successor does *not* touch AI (see below), leaving this slot open.

**3. *The Amazing Generation* — Jonathan Haidt, Catherine Price & Cynthia Yuan Cheng (Penguin Random House, 2026).** [Publisher listing.](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/797271/the-amazing-generation-by-jonathan-haidt-and-catherine-price-illustrated-by-cynthia-yuan-cheng/) The franchise leader's actual successor — a kids-facing phones/social-media handbook for ages 9–12. **Similar but different:** shares the franchise's parent-empowerment DNA, but is aimed at children, focused on smartphones, and pointedly silent on AI companions — confirming that the category leader has *not* claimed the AI-companion territory this book targets.

**4. *Reclaiming Conversation* — Sherry Turkle (Penguin Press, 2015).** [Publisher listing.](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/313732/reclaiming-conversation-by-sherry-turkle/) The intellectual ancestor of the "synthetic intimacy erodes real relational skill" argument. **Similar but different:** establishes the durable thesis this book stands on, but predates the companion-chatbot era entirely and offers culture-level reflection rather than a stage-by-stage parenting method.

**5. *Bad Therapy: Why the Kids Aren't Growing Up* — Abigail Shrier (Sentinel, 2024).** [Publisher listing.](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/716567/bad-therapy-by-abigail-shrier/) A NYT-bestselling proof that parents will buy a pointed single-thesis book about what is going wrong with this generation. **Similar but different:** shares the concerned-parent audience and provocative frame, but targets the therapeutic-culture establishment rather than a specific technology, and offers critique more than an actionable playbook.

**6. *Behind Their Screens: What Teens Are Facing (and Adults Are Missing)* — Emily Weinstein & Carrie James (MIT Press, 2022).** [Publisher listing.](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262047357/behind-their-screens/) The research-depth comp, grounded in a Harvard survey of thousands of teens. **Similar but different:** shares the rigorous, teen-centered evidence base, but is broad digital-life reportage without this book's tight AI-companion focus or precautionary attachment argument.

**7. *Stolen Focus* — Johann Hari (Crown, 2022).** [Publisher listing.](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/634289/stolen-focus-by-johann-hari/) The model for pairing narrative reporting with an urgent, human argument about what technology is taking from us. **Similar but different:** demonstrates the reported-narrative-plus-argument structure this book uses, but concerns adult attention rather than children's capacity for intimacy, and predates the companion-AI moment.

## 5. About the Author

**No author is currently attached to this concept, and this proposal states that openly.** What follows is a profile of the platform this book *requires* — the credentials a publisher should insist on before acquisition, because the entire commercial risk here is concentrated not in whether the audience exists (it demonstrably does, at scale) but in whether it will *transfer* to this specific book. Sherry Turkle has now written essentially this argument three times, with MIT credentials and bestseller status, and those books sold at respectable-bestseller, not phenomenon, scale. Concept alone will not carry this title. Platform will.

**The ideal author or author team combines three assets:**

1. **A credible developmental or clinical voice.** A developmental or clinical psychologist, adolescent psychiatrist, or a researcher in child development / human-computer interaction who can make the Part II attachment argument with authority and defend its precautionary posture against skeptical reviewers. This is what lets the book concede what isn't yet proven without forfeiting credibility.

2. **A demonstrated large audience.** The engine behind *The Anxious Generation*'s numbers was not the idea; it was Haidt's pre-existing platform — a prior bestseller, a large newsletter, and a policy movement he had already seeded. This book needs an equivalent: a substantial newsletter or podcast following, a large parenting or clinical media presence, or a national profile in the child-and-tech conversation.

3. **Reporting capability, or a co-author who supplies it.** Part I demands someone who can work the public record — filings, agency orders, testimony, investigative reporting — into propulsive narrative. Where the lead author is an academic or clinician, the strongest configuration pairs them with a journalist co-author.

**The recommended structure is a two-hander:** a platform expert (developmental psychologist or large-audience parenting voice) as the authority and public face, plus a reporter-writer who builds the Part I spine and carries the prose. Securing this platform author or team should be treated as a **build precondition**, not a nicety. A publisher acquiring on concept alone, without platform, should underwrite conservatively and expect midlist-bestseller economics.

## 6. Marketing & Publicity Plan

**Platform (the precondition).** The plan assumes the author platform described in Section 5 — a newsletter/podcast/media following that can be mobilized directly at launch and that pre-sells the hardcover. Absent that, every tactic below weakens.

**Positioning line, everywhere.** *"Turkle and Haidt told you what synthetic intimacy is doing to childhood. This is the stage-by-stage field guide for getting your ten-to-seventeen-year-old back."* The book is marketed as the indispensable companion volume, never as a competing thesis — a framing that turns the two biggest adjacent titles into tailwinds rather than competitors.

**Launch strategy and the do-or-die timing.** The book must publish close behind Turkle's *Artificial Intimacy* (Sept. 29, 2026) to ride the review-and-media wave she creates while the topic is at peak salience — and must not slip into late 2027, where it would read as derivative. The single highest-value pre-launch asset is a **Turkle blurb** or an explicit blessing as "the parent's field guide" to her book.

**The built-in news pegs.** This is a topic with a self-refreshing calendar of hooks, and the book is written to sit *above* any one of them so none can date it:

- **Regulatory milestones:** California's SB 243 in force since Jan. 1, 2026, with annual state reporting beginning July 2027 ([SB 243](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billNavClient.xhtml?bill_id=202520260SB243)); the federal GUARD Act advancing through Congress ([Roll Call, Apr. 30, 2026](https://rollcall.com/2026/04/30/ban-on-kids-companion-chatbots-advanced-by-senate-committee/)); a widening wave of state laws in New York, Idaho, Oregon, and Washington ([Mayer Brown, Apr. 2026](https://www.mayerbrown.com/en/insights/publications/2026/04/oregon-and-washington-join-california-in-enacting-companion-chatbot-laws)).
- **Litigation beats:** each new filing and ruling in the companion-chatbot docket, including the January 2026 confidential settlements and newer actions such as Pennsylvania's May 2026 suit ([NPR, May 5, 2026](https://www.npr.org/2026/05/05/nx-s1-5812861/characterai-chatbot-medical-advice-pennsylvania-lawsuit)).
- **The annual data drumbeat:** each new Pew and Common Sense Media survey ([Pew, Dec. 2025](https://www.pewresearch.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/20/2025/12/PI_2025.12.09_Teens-Social-Media-AI_REPORT.pdf); [Common Sense, July 2025](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/research/talk-trust-and-trade-offs-how-and-why-teens-use-ai-companions)) is a fresh, bookable media moment.
- **The industry's own retreats:** Character.AI's under-18 ban and Meta's teen pause are ready-made "even the companies admit it" segments ([Character.AI, Nov. 2025](https://blog.character.ai/an-update-on-changes-to-our-under-18-experience/); [TechCrunch, Jan. 2026](https://techcrunch.com/2026/01/23/meta-pauses-teen-access-to-ai-characters-ahead-of-new-version/)).

**Media hooks.** The reframe itself is the pitch: "It's not screen time, it's a relationship." "The friend who never says no." "Your kid's chatbot isn't a tutor — for some kids it's a girlfriend, and you don't know." Op-ed territory in the education, parenting, and technology verticals; strong fit for morning shows, NPR, and the major parenting and tech podcasts.

**Partnerships.** Natural allied institutions include Common Sense Media, the Center for Humane Technology, Children and Screens, and school-counselor and pediatric associations — organizations already publishing in this space and hungry for an actionable parent-facing resource to point families toward. A parallel companion product for schools and clinicians (a workshop or discussion guide drawn from Part III) extends reach into the institutional channel that sustained *The Anxious Generation*.

**Honest risk note for the acquiring house.** A visible skeptic movement is primed to attack any single-cause "technology is rewiring your child" book ([*Compact*, "Against the Screen-Time Panic"](https://www.compactmag.com/article/against-the-screen-time-panic/)). The book's precautionary honesty is the mitigation: because it never claims proven neurological harm, the standard debunk misses. Marketing copy must hold the same line — the moment a jacket asserts settled science, the book becomes the exact target the skeptics are waiting for.

## 7. Chapter Outline

Fourteen chapters across three parts.

### Part I — THE SEDUCTION: How the Friend Was Built
- **Ch. 1 — The Friend Who Never Says No.** The Sewell Setzer story, then the disarming pivot: most kids aren't Sewell — and here is why you should still be paying close attention. Names the real number and makes the anticipatory-anxiety case.
- **Ch. 2 — Engineered to Out-Friend a Friend.** The design choices — always available, always affirming, never demanding — and the engagement incentives that produce a companion built to beat a human being.
- **Ch. 3 — The Case That Broke the Silence.** The public paper trail that forced the reckoning: the complaints, Judge Conway's May 2025 First Amendment ruling, the leaked Meta policy, the TBIJ investigation, the FTC 6(b) order, and Senate testimony.
- **Ch. 4 — Who's Actually Falling In.** The honest prevalence picture — utility use versus the fast-growing intimacy leading edge — and what the reverse-causation literature suggests about which kids run heaviest.
- **Ch. 5 — The Parents in the Dark.** The awareness gap: high use, low parental knowledge, low comfort — and why so many families never see it coming.

### Part II — WHY IT MATTERS: Synthetic Intimacy and the Developing Heart
- **Ch. 6 — Attachment, Not Attention.** The reframe: the developing capacity for intimacy, and the years built to grow it.
- **Ch. 7 — The Muscle Built by Friction.** Why real love survives being told no — and the developmental case that a frictionless companion may quietly weaken what friction is meant to build.
- **Ch. 8 — What We Don't Yet Know.** The precautionary argument stated plainly — the empty minor-specific evidence base, reverse causation and bidirectionality conceded up front, and the unmeasured 10–12 cohort.
- **Ch. 9 — The Mirror and the Mask.** What a machine engineered to feel like a person reflects back, and what it hides — the difference between being understood and being flattered.

### Part III — THE PLAYBOOK: Raising a Child Who Can Still Love a Real Person
- **Ch. 10 — The Assessment: Curiosity or Dependency?** A practical, principle-level framework for telling ordinary use from the warning signs.
- **Ch. 11 — The First Conversation.** How to open the subject with your kid without triggering shutdown, shame, or a slammed door.
- **Ch. 12 — Normal, Worrying, Emergency.** The triage framework: what to watch, what to wait on, and when to act now.
- **Ch. 13 — Reconnection Without Confiscation.** Rebuilding the real relationship without seizing the phone or blowing up trust.
- **Ch. 14 — The Long Game.** The durable, forward-looking close: raising a child who can tolerate the friction real intimacy requires — and still choose a real person.

## 8. Specifications

- **Word count:** approximately 80,000 words.
- **Structure:** three parts, fourteen chapters (as outlined above).
- **Format:** trade nonfiction; narrative reporting (Part I) plus argument (Part II) plus practical guidance with assessments, checklists, and scripts (Part III). Notes and full source citations to public record throughout.
- **Delivery:** full manuscript approximately 9–12 months from contract, assuming the platform author/team is attached at signing.
