# Market Evidence Report

### *The Friend Who Never Says No: Inside the AI Companions Built to Never Disappoint Your Kid — and a Parent's Playbook for Raising Children Who Can Still Love a Real Person*

**Validation dossier • Prepared 9 July 2026**

> **Purpose.** This document assembles the public evidence that a market exists for a standalone, kids-only (ages 10–17) parent field guide to the AI-companion era. It is written to survive an informed skeptic. Every statistic is cited inline to a real source. Where the evidence is soft, unconfirmed, or points the other way, it is labeled as such — the market thesis is stronger for conceding what it cannot prove.
>
> **One-line thesis under test.** The anxious-parent buyer who made *The Anxious Generation* a phenomenon exists at scale and is now looking at AI companions; the intimacy behavior at the book's center is a fast-growing *minority* leading edge, not a mass crisis; and the practical-playbook lane sits open beneath the diagnosis books (Turkle, Haidt) that are about to define the category.

## 1. Executive summary

- **The buyer is verified and large.** The direct comp, *The Anxious Generation*, is a confirmed #1 *New York Times* bestseller that spent 73+ weeks on the list and hit #1 eleven times as of August 2025 ([Haidt, X/Twitter, Aug 2025](https://x.com/JonHaidt/status/1958598653540446370)), with a publisher-claimed — not independently audited — "over two million copies sold" ([Penguin Random House](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/729231/the-anxious-generation-by-jonathan-haidt/)). The anxious-parent-of-a-tween/teen audience is demonstrably not fatigued at the cash register.
- **The pain is real, high-frequency, and hidden from parents.** 64% of U.S. teens 13–17 have used AI chatbots and ~28–30% use them daily ([Pew, Dec 2025](https://www.pewresearch.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/20/2025/12/PI_2025.12.09_Teens-Social-Media-AI_REPORT.pdf)). Only 37% of parents know their teen uses AI companions ([Torney Senate testimony, Sep 2025](https://www.judiciary.senate.gov/imo/media/doc/e2e8fc50-a9ac-05ec-edd7-277cb0afcdf2/2025-09-16%20PM%20-%20Testimony%20-%20Torney.pdf)), and only 18% of parents are comfortable with their teen using a chatbot for emotional support ([Pew, Feb 2026](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2026/02/24/what-parents-say-about-their-teens-ai-use/)). That awareness-and-anxiety gap is the book's demand engine.
- **The intimacy behavior is a minority — and the report says so plainly.** Emotional-support use is 12% and casual-conversation use 16% of all teens ([Pew, Dec 2025](https://www.pewresearch.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/20/2025/12/PI_2025.12.09_Teens-Social-Media-AI_REPORT.pdf)). The scary "72% use AI companions" headline ([Common Sense Media, Jul 2025](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/research/talk-trust-and-trade-offs-how-and-why-teens-use-ai-companions)) is real but definitionally broad — it counts homework-ChatGPT. The honest positioning is *anticipatory anxiety* ("your kid could fall in"), not proven mass harm.
- **The white space is narrow but genuine.** No major-publisher head-to-head kids-only AI-companion *playbook* is confirmed forthcoming. But the attachment *diagnosis* is about to be owned by the field's 40-year authority — Sherry Turkle's *Artificial Intimacy* (Little, Brown, 29 Sep 2026) ([Hachette](https://www.hachettebookgroup.com/titles/sherry-turkle/artificial-intimacy/9780316573962/)) — which forces this book to be the *field guide to the diagnosis*, not a competing thesis.
- **The risks are concentrated in "transfer," not "audience."** The audience exists; whether it transfers to *this* book without a platform author is the central commercial risk. Section 8 treats this without flinching.

## 2. Addressable audience (TAM / SAM)

> **Sourcing caveat, stated up front.** There is no clean, primary market-size figure for the "U.S. parenting/AI nonfiction" niche. The commonly circulated self-help numbers trace only to SEO aggregators (see below) and are treated here as unconfirmed. This section therefore sizes the audience two defensible ways — **(a) proven comp demand** and **(b) survey-verified penetration of the target behavior** — rather than resting on a fabricated category dollar figure.

### 2a. Total addressable market (TAM): the anxious-parent-of-a-10-to-17-year-old

The relevant TAM is U.S. parents of children roughly 10–17 who are worried about technology's effect on their child. The cleanest available *proof of the size and willingness-to-pay of this exact buyer* is comp performance, not a population count:

- *The Anxious Generation* — confirmed #1 *NYT* bestseller; 73 weeks on the list and #1 eleven times as of Aug 2025 ([Haidt, X](https://x.com/JonHaidt/status/1958598653540446370)); publisher-claimed "over two million copies sold" ([PRH](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/729231/the-anxious-generation-by-jonathan-haidt/), [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/Anxious-Generation-Rewiring-Childhood-Epidemic/dp/0593655036)). **The 2M figure is a publisher marketing claim, not audited BookScan — treat it as directional evidence of scale, not a hard unit count.** A frequently cited "111 consecutive weeks on the *NYT* list" ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Anxious_Generation)) **could not be confirmed against a primary *NYT* archive and is presented as unconfirmed.**

The takeaway: a single-topic, anxiety-framed book about children and technology has, within the last two years, moved into the millions of copies. That is the TAM ceiling this concept references — while explicitly *underwriting* to a fraction of it (see §8).

*(Population denominator: a Census QuickFacts pull of the U.S. 10–17 population would give a clean TAM headcount. That figure is not included in this verified evidence base and is therefore deliberately not asserted here rather than dressed with a false citation. It should be added at proposal stage from [census.gov](https://www.census.gov/) directly.)*

**How to read the two prevalence numbers together.** The 72% (Common Sense) and the 12–16% (Pew) are *not* contradictory — they measure different things. Common Sense counts any teen who has ever used a chatbot in a companion-like way, folding in general-purpose ChatGPT/Claude; Pew isolates declared *use-cases* and finds intimacy use is a minority. **The honest SAM is therefore layered:** a very large "exposed" ring (roughly two-thirds of teens use chatbots at all), inside which sits a smaller but fast-growing "intimacy" core (roughly **12–16% of all teens** on Pew's clean measure, up to ~⅓ of companion users on the softest self-report). The book sells to the *exposed* ring's parents on anticipatory anxiety and serves the *intimacy* core's parents on triage. **The 10–12 preteen segment inside the target is empirically unmeasured for emotional/companion use and no quantitative claim is made about it.**

## 3. Demand signals

### 3a. Usage is real and high-frequency

- **64% of teens 13–17 have used AI chatbots; ~28–30% use them daily** — the clean, defensible usage anchor ([Pew, Dec 2025](https://www.pewresearch.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/20/2025/12/PI_2025.12.09_Teens-Social-Media-AI_REPORT.pdf), n=1,458 teen-parent dyads, fielded 25 Sep–9 Oct 2025, MOE ±3.3pp; [CNN](https://www.cnn.com/2025/12/09/tech/teens-ai-chatbot-use-study)).
- **72% have ever used an "AI companion"; ~52% are regular users; 21% a few times a week** ([Common Sense Media, Jul 2025](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/research/talk-trust-and-trade-offs-how-and-why-teens-use-ai-companions), n=1,060 teens, fielded Apr–May 2025; [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2025/07/21/72-of-u-s-teens-have-used-ai-companions-study-finds/)). **Definition disclosed:** includes general tools used companion-style.

### 3b. The intimacy signal — the fast-growing minority core

From the same Common Sense survey, among teen companion users:

- **33%** have chosen an AI companion over a real person for a serious or important conversation;
- **31%** found companion conversations **as satisfying or more satisfying** than talking with real friends;
- **34%** felt uncomfortable with something a companion said or did;
- **~24–25%** have shared personal/private information with a companion.

Sources: [Common Sense Media](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/research/talk-trust-and-trade-offs-how-and-why-teens-use-ai-companions); [Common Sense (official)](https://x.com/CommonSense/status/1945486943917371578); [CBS News](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/ai-digital-friendship-with-teens-common-sense-media-study/).

**The countervailing evidence, stated honestly (the book must not bury it):** roughly two-thirds of teens still find AI conversations *less* satisfying than human ones, ~80% report spending more time with real friends, and about half distrust AI advice ([Fast Company](https://www.fastcompany.com/91369689/teens-using-ai-companions-and-some-prefer-them-to-people), [CBS News](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/ai-digital-friendship-with-teens-common-sense-media-study/)). This is why the thesis is **precautionary and leading-edge, not "most kids already have an AI boyfriend."**

### 3c. Emotional dependency — the single most authoritative primary quantification

OpenAI's own disclosure, of ChatGPT's **800M+ weekly users** ([OpenAI, 27 Oct 2025](https://openai.com/index/strengthening-chatgpt-responses-in-sensitive-conversations/)):

- **~0.15%** show conversation patterns indicating **heightened emotional attachment/reliance**;
- **~0.15%** show explicit **suicidal-ideation** indicators (**~1M+ people per week** discuss suicide with ChatGPT — [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2025/10/27/openai-says-over-a-million-people-talk-to-chatgpt-about-suicide-weekly/));
- **~0.07%** show possible **psychosis/mania** signals.

**Label clearly: this is all-ages, platform-wide, not teen-specific.** It is nonetheless the strongest primary evidence that AI emotional dependency is a measurable, large-N phenomenon.

### 3d. Mental-health help-seeking (partially confirmed)

Nearly **1 in 5** Americans aged 12–21 report using an AI chatbot for **mental-health advice** (mid-2026 press framing), up from about **1 in 8** a year earlier; ~⅔ kept it secret from parents/friends/doctors; >90% rated the advice at least somewhat helpful ([RAND, Nov 2025](https://www.rand.org/news/press/2025/11/one-in-eight-adolescents-and-young-adults-use-ai-chatbots.html); [Brown SPH](https://sph.brown.edu/news/2025-11-18/teens-ai-chatbots); [MedicalXpress, Jun 2026](https://medicalxpress.com/news/2026-06-teens-ai-chatbots-mental-health.html)). **Flagged partial:** RAND's own release states "one in eight"; the "nearly 1 in 5" figure appears in later press cycles. Treat the 1-in-8 → nearly-1-in-5 shift as unreconciled pending the JAMA Pediatrics paper.

### 3e. Parental awareness gap — the direct hook

- Only **37%** of parents know their teen uses AI companions, against ~70%+ actual teen use ([Torney Senate testimony, 16 Sep 2025](https://www.judiciary.senate.gov/imo/media/doc/e2e8fc50-a9ac-05ec-edd7-277cb0afcdf2/2025-09-16%20PM%20-%20Testimony%20-%20Torney.pdf)). *Confirmed via Congressional testimony relaying Common Sense polling, not an independently published topline — noted accordingly.*
- Parents **under-report** teen chatbot use by 13 points: only **51%** of parents say their teen uses chatbots vs **64%** of teens; ~3 in 10 parents are "unsure" ([Pew, Feb 2026](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2026/02/24/what-parents-say-about-their-teens-ai-use/); [The Hill](https://thehill.com/policy/healthcare/5753210-parents-teens-ai-chatbot-use-pew-survey/)).
- Only **18%** of parents are comfortable with their teen getting emotional support from a chatbot, versus ~80% comfortable with informational use ([Pew, Feb 2026](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2026/02/24/what-parents-say-about-their-teens-ai-use/); [CBS News](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/ai-teens-parents-pew-survey/)).

**This trio is the demand thesis in three numbers:** parents are unaware their kids are exposed, they under-count that exposure, and they are deeply uncomfortable with the exact use-case (emotional support) the book addresses.

### 3f. Expert/institutional demand signal

Common Sense Media with **Stanford Medicine's Brainstorm Lab** rated social AI companions **"unacceptable risk" for anyone under 18** (Apr 2025), documenting easily circumvented age gates, steerable sexual role-play with test accounts, and potential to deepen compulsive attachment and worsen existing conditions ([Common Sense/Stanford report PDF](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/sites/default/files/pug/csm-ai-risk-assessment-social-ai-companions_final.pdf); [press release](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/press-releases/ai-companions-decoded-common-sense-media-recommends-ai-companion-safety-standards); [Stanford Medicine](https://news.stanford.edu/stories/2025/08/ai-companions-chatbots-teens-young-people-risks-dangers-study)). When the field's leading child-media authority tells parents "no one under 18," it validates a book that tells them what to do about it.

## 6. Competitive landscape & white-space assessment

### What already exists (diagnosis / culture)
- **Turkle's forthcoming *Artificial Intimacy*** (29 Sep 2026) will own the attachment *diagnosis* from the field's 40-year authority, with the identical childhood-to-parenthood scope and best-friend/lover/therapist taxonomy ([Hachette](https://www.hachettebookgroup.com/titles/sherry-turkle/artificial-intimacy/9780316573962/)). **This is a frontlist direct competitor, not backlist.**
- **Turkle's backlist** (*Alone Together*, *Reclaiming Conversation*) already made this argument twice at midlist-bestseller scale ([sherryturkle.com](https://www.sherryturkle.com/reclaiming-conversation)).
- **Adult-relationships adjacencies:** Muldoon's *Love Machines* (Faber, 2026); De Kai's *Raising AI* (MIT Press, 2025, [MIT Press](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262049764/raising-ai/)).

### What exists in the broad tech-parenting lane
- Market leaders fold AI companions into general digital-childhood books: Haidt's *The Amazing Generation* (phones/social media, ages 9–12, **not AI**) ([PRH](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/797271/the-amazing-generation-by-jonathan-haidt-and-catherine-price-illustrated-by-cynthia-yuan-cheng/)); Weinstein & James's *Behind Their Screens* ([MIT Press](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262047357/behind-their-screens/)).

### What exists for free (the toolkit tier)
- Common Sense Media, APA, Children and Screens, and various parent-safety orgs publish free, faster-refreshing AI-companion guidance ([Children and Screens](https://www.childrenandscreens.org/learn-explore/research/ai-social-companions-and-youth-what-parents-need-to-know/)). These own the SEO and update faster than any book.

### The gap
No **confirmed** major-publisher, **kids-only (10–17), reporting-driven, actionable *playbook*** exists or is announced for 2025–2027 ([Publishers Marketplace scan](https://lunch.publishersmarketplace.com/category/ai/)). The three unclaimed differentiators:

1. **Tight kids-only 10–17 focus** — Turkle's lifecycle book and Haidt's ages-9–12 phones book both dilute or omit it.
2. **A "how it was engineered" reporting spine** built entirely from public record (complaints, the May 2025 ruling, FTC 6(b), Senate testimony, the Reuters Meta leak, TBIJ, statute text) — no reliance on the confidential settlements.
3. **A Part III assess/triage/reconnect parent playbook** — the one thing a diagnosis book and a free PDF do not deliver at book depth.

**The silver lining in the competitive picture:** the franchise leader (Haidt) has explicitly *not* claimed the AI-companion slot ([After Babel](https://www.afterbabel.com/p/dont-give-your-child-an-ai-companion), [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/09/26/jonathan-haidt-how-parents-can-limit-their-kids-use-of-ai-chatbots.html)), leaving it open for a companion-*volume* positioned beneath Turkle's diagnosis.

**Honest caveat:** the "no head-to-head playbook" finding is a search-verified *absence*, which could reflect an unannounced/embargoed deal rather than true white space ([Publishers Weekly deal reports](https://www.publishersweekly.com/pw/by-topic/industry-news/book-deals/article/100472-book-deals-week-of-may-25-2026.html)). Treat as a strong signal, not a certainty.

## 7. Risks to the market thesis (clear-eyed)

**1. Turkle pre-emption (highest risk).** *Artificial Intimacy* (29 Sep 2026) will own the attachment diagnosis first, from a bigger name and imprint ([Hachette](https://www.hachettebookgroup.com/titles/sherry-turkle/artificial-intimacy/9780316573962/)). *Mitigation:* position as the *playbook to* Turkle's diagnosis, not a competing thesis; ship close behind (never trail into 2027 as a me-too); pursue a Turkle blurb / "companion volume" framing; lean on kids-only focus + Part III scripts she does not deliver.

**2. The prevalence is a minority, not a mass crisis.** Emotional/companion use is **12–16% of all teens** ([Pew, Dec 2025](https://www.pewresearch.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/20/2025/12/PI_2025.12.09_Teens-Social-Media-AI_REPORT.pdf)), and Common Sense's own data show most teens still prefer real friends ([CBS News](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/ai-digital-friendship-with-teens-common-sense-media-study/)). A reviewer can gut an overclaimed thesis in one paragraph. *Mitigation:* sell on **anticipatory anxiety** ("your kid *could* fall in"), lead with 30% daily use and the 1-in-3-of-users satisfaction figure, and pre-empt the "it's mostly homework" rebuttal in the introduction. Do not cite "72%" as a companion-intimacy or Pew figure.

**3. The science is thin — the "attachment harm" claim is not settled.** Peer-reviewed reviews state there is essentially **no empirical evidence** on companion AI's impact on minors; the strongest source (Sun/Wang/McDaniel, *Child Development Perspectives*, June 2026) is a **bidirectional theory review**, and the Danish "My AI" finding supports reverse causation (lonelier kids seek chatbots) ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00146-025-02318-6), [Oxford/CDP](https://academic.oup.com/cdpers/article/20/2/91/8414012), [The Conversation](https://theconversation.com/teens-are-increasingly-turning-to-ai-companions-and-it-could-be-harming-them-261955)). The Odgers/Etchells "correlation is not causation" critique that dogged Haidt is pre-loaded for this genre. *Mitigation:* adopt an openly **precautionary** posture; concede reverse causation and bidirectionality in Part II; never let marketing assert proven neurological harm.

**4. Commercial transfer risk (not audience risk).** The audience exists at scale, but Turkle's own prior books — the same argument — sold at midlist-bestseller, not phenomenon, scale ([sherryturkle.com](https://www.sherryturkle.com/reclaiming-conversation)). *The Anxious Generation*'s numbers ran on a pre-existing author platform. *Mitigation:* underwrite the P&L to **Turkle scale (75k–250k)**, not Haidt's publisher-claimed 2M ([PRH](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/729231/the-anxious-generation-by-jonathan-haidt/)); make a **platform co-author** a build precondition.

**5. Obsolescence.** Trade books take 12–18 months; the lead antagonist already banned under-18 open chat ([Character.AI](https://blog.character.ai/u18-chat-announcement/)), Meta paused teen AI characters ([TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/01/23/meta-pauses-teen-access-to-ai-characters-ahead-of-new-version/)), settlements are confidential ([CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/01/07/google-characterai-to-settle-suits-involving-suicides-ai-chatbots.html)), and stats refresh yearly. *Mitigation:* durable spine in Part II at the principle level ("synthetic intimacy and the developing attachment system"); time-stamp all products/dockets/numbers as illustrative.

**6. "Already a chapter" + free-toolkit glut.** Broad tech-parenting books absorb AI companions as a subsection, and free, faster-refreshing toolkits own the SEO ([Children and Screens](https://www.childrenandscreens.org/learn-explore/research/ai-social-companions-and-youth-what-parents-need-to-know/)). *Mitigation:* justify standalone length via the genuine book-sized attachment reframe (Part II) and triage/reconnection depth no PDF delivers; keep Part III principle-based.

**7. A cooling self-help category.** Directional Circana data show self-help units **down 26.3% YoY in Q1 2026** after a strong 2025 ([Publishers Weekly](https://www.publishersweekly.com/pw/by-topic/industry-news/financial-reporting/article/99417-print-book-sales-rose-slightly-in-2025.html)) — **partial confidence**. *Mitigation:* position as narrative-nonfiction-with-a-playbook (Hari/Haidt register), not pure self-help, and do not rely on category tailwinds.

**8. A primed science-skepticism counter-movement.** Outlets are already publishing against "screen-time panic" ([Compact](https://www.compactmag.com/article/against-the-screen-time-panic/), [Mad in America](https://www.madinamerica.com/2026/03/screen-time/)). *Mitigation:* the precautionary posture and public-record reporting spine are the direct inoculation.

## 8. Bottom line

The market thesis survives scrutiny **with the repositioning locked in**. The buyer is verified and phenomenon-scale (the anxious parent of a 10–17-year-old); the pain is real, daily, and hidden from parents; the culture is treating AI-companions-and-minors as a live legal, regulatory, and public-health story; and there is a genuine, if narrow, unclaimed lane for a kids-only *playbook* beneath the Turkle/Haidt diagnoses. The thesis does **not** survive if the book overclaims prevalence ("72% / most kids have an AI boyfriend"), overclaims science ("the science proves attachment harm"), or is underwritten to Haidt-scale economics. Sold honestly — anticipatory anxiety, precautionary science, Turkle-scale P&L, platform co-author, category-level durable spine — the evidence supports a build.
