# The Book Concept

**Document 3 of the concept package — full positioning, post-adjudication**

---

## 1. Title

**Working title:** *Your Kid's AI Best Friend*
**Working subtitle:** *What to Do When the Phone Loves Them Back — Scripts, House Rules, and the Way Out*

**The title decision, honestly.** The concept was developed under the title *The Perfect Listener*, which lost in evaluation for a concrete reason: at Amazon-thumbnail distance it reads as a communication or leadership book. *Your Kid's AI Best Friend* names the buyer, the phenomenon, and the shelf in five words, and it is the current front-runner — but it is a paper hypothesis, untested against the core demographic (mothers 35–55), and a title that reads flip rather than urgent would depress the duty-purchase and gift mechanics the sales model depends on. *The Perfect Listener* survives inside the book as the named diagnosis ("the perfect-listener problem") and as the lead tested alternate. Formal title testing against the target demographic is committed before catalog lock, and the subtitle has been revised in this document to retire the earlier "out-relate the machine" phrasing as the headline promise: the book's corrected thesis (see §6) is that restriction alone doesn't end an attachment — the book is the working exit ramp — and the subtitle must never be readable as "learn to live with your kid's AI girlfriend." One earlier marketing hook — "the parenting expert who says DON'T ban your kid's AI girlfriend" — has been killed outright, for reasons explained in §10: it contradicted the public positions of every institution the author plan depends on.

## 2. One-Line Pitch

Nearly three-quarters of American teens have tried an AI companion at least once under [Common Sense Media's broad definition](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/research/talk-trust-and-trade-offs-how-and-why-teens-use-ai-companions), roughly [1 in 8 use a chatbot for emotional support](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2026/02/24/how-teens-use-and-view-ai/) — about three million kids — and [one in four American teens has, at least once, taken a serious problem to an AI instead of a person](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/sites/default/files/research/report/talk-trust-and-trade-offs_2025_web.pdf): this is the first clinician-authored protocol book — crisis triage, word-for-word scripts, age-tiered Family AI Agreements, a Breakup protocol, and a platform-accountability playbook built on California's SB 243 — for the parent who just found the chat logs and needs to know what to do Tuesday night.

## 3. Category, Format, Length, Price

- **Category:** Family & Relationships / Parenting — Teenagers (FAM039000). Prescriptive nonfiction with a reported case-study spine, in the *Good Inside* / *How to Talk So Kids Will Listen* clinician-protocol lane, opening with a crisis-triage front section structured like an ER manual.
- **Format:** The economics are deliberately hedged below a $28-hardcover-only bet. Primary plan: trade paperback original (or simultaneous hardcover/trade paper) at $19.99–$22.99 paper / $28.99 cloth, because the adjacent-title record says the Haidt-scale hardcover P&L is the wrong base rate for a follower title in this category (post-Haidt follower titles reviewed in this package's market research have underperformed the Haidt analogy, while *Good Inside*-lane clinician books have not). E-book and audio simultaneous; audio matters because the scripts chapters perform as performed dialogue.
- **Length:** 75,000–85,000 words. Fourteen chapters plus the "Start Here: The First 72 Hours" triage front door and a dated, QR-linked appendix of platform specifics engineered for revised-edition cycles.
- **P&L sizing:** to *Good Inside*-lane clinician-protocol comps, not *Anxious Generation* comps. *The Anxious Generation* — [over two million copies sold per its publisher](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/729231/the-anxious-generation-by-jonathan-haidt/), and as of June 2026 [111 consecutive weeks on the NYT nonfiction list, reaching #1 repeatedly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Anxious_Generation) — proves the buyer exists; it does not price this advance.

## 4. Target Reader

**Primary:** Mothers 35–55 of kids 10–17 — the demographic that powered Haidt's two-year run — served in two deliberately separate buyer states: (1) the **crisis buyer** who just found the logs, met by the 72-hour triage section that makes the book usable before it is read; and (2) the **anxious-but-unaware buyer**, met by the Attachment Check-In quiz that manufactures the discovery moment. The unaware state is the majority state: [Pew finds roughly 4 in 10 parents have never discussed chatbots with their teen, and parents systematically underestimate use](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2026/02/24/what-parents-say-about-their-teens-ai-use/) (64% of teens have used a chatbot versus about half of parents aware). The addressable base is parents of the [roughly 42 million US children aged 8–17](https://www.childstats.gov/americaschildren/tables/pop1.asp).

**Secondary:** Fathers and co-parents (the scripts and family-meeting chapters are written to be assigned — "read chapter 6 before Sunday" — making two-plus copies per household the natural unit of sale), plus therapists and pediatricians who need a handable resource.

**Institutional:** School counselors, PTAs, and districts — the bulk channel the Haidt playbook proved, reached through the Common Sense Media / Child Mind Institute co-brand strategy (§10) and the model-PTA-policy chapter. This concern is demonstrably mainstream and cross-partisan, not elite: [emotional support from a chatbot is the only teen AI use a majority of parents oppose — just 18% are comfortable with it](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2026/02/24/what-parents-say-about-their-teens-ai-use/) — and the Character.AI settlement story ran everywhere from [CBS](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/ai-teens-parents-pew-survey/) to [CNN](https://www.cnn.com/2026/01/07/business/character-ai-google-settle-teen-suicide-lawsuit) to conservative talk media.

## 5. Core Promise and Reader Transformation

In one weekend — or, for the parent in active crisis, in the first 72 hours via the triage section that opens the book — a frightened parent goes from *"I just found the chat logs and I have no idea what this is"* to a working plan:

1. **Calibration first:** a green/yellow/red framework, front-loaded so the reader trusts the alarms, that honestly separates benign and even beneficial use from warning signs and genuine crisis.
2. **Detection without betrayal:** the six behavioral signs of companion attachment and the Quiet Audit — how to look in a way you can later defend to your kid.
3. **The first conversation:** word-for-word scripts — the curious open, the discovered-logs conversation, the sideways entry — with the five door-slamming sentences ("It's not real," "That's pathetic") annotated as failure modes.
4. **House rules that hold:** the age-tiered (8–12 / 13–15 / 16–18) Family AI Agreement, negotiated with the kid rather than imposed.
5. **The red zone:** a concrete escalation protocol for sexual content and self-harm reinforcement — what to screenshot, whom to call, when a therapist is non-negotiable — plus the platform-accountability playbook grounded in [SB 243's actual provisions](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billNavClient.xhtml?bill_id=202520260SB243) (§7).
6. **The Breakup:** ending an attachment without driving it underground — harm-reduction taper versus cold turkey, anticipating real grief, managing relapse.
7. **The upstream rebuild:** out-relating the algorithm by rebuilding the human connection the bot was substituting for — with the honest admission that the bot listened better than most adults do, and what to copy from it.

The governing reframe: **this is not a screen-time problem; it is a relationship in your kid's life.** And the transformation the book sells is not coexistence with the bot — it is the working exit ramp: restriction alone does not end an attachment, so the book supplies what a rule cannot, and its endpoint is a kid who prefers people.

## 6. The Book's Spine and Voice

**The lane is clinician-protocol, and the lane is the moat.** The voice is a credentialed adolescent clinician who has sat with these families — calm, specific, script-level, anti-panic — the Damour/Kennedy register, not the exposé register and not the jeremiad register.

**An honest accounting of what's free.** The best free guidance is genuinely good, and this book says so on the jacket rather than pretending otherwise. [The JED Foundation's parent guide](https://jedfoundation.org/why-your-teen-shouldnt-be-using-ai-companions-and-what-to-do-if-they-are/) and [the APA's guidance for parents](https://www.apa.org/topics/artificial-intelligence-machine-learning/teens-chatbots-parents) already publish the consensus core — taper rather than cold turkey, conversation before confiscation, warning signs, crisis referral. The book cites those guides as its provenance and its demand signal: they are recognizable one-page sketches of a problem parents need four hundred pages and a protocol for. What no free guide provides, and what constitutes the moat:

- **Script-level depth** — the Faber/Mazlish DNA: not "open a nonjudgmental conversation" but the actual sentences, the actual failure modes, the actual comebacks, rehearsed across dozens of family scenarios. Parents have paid for exactly this, for decades, at the *How to Talk So Kids Will Listen* register.
- **The reported case-study spine** — real families, followed through discovery, confrontation, breakup, and rebuild, which a liability-averse nonprofit web page will never carry.
- **The age-tiered, negotiated Family AI Agreement** — a developmental framework, not a tip list, and the book's shareable artifact and download funnel.
- **The platform-accountability playbook** — the one deliverable genuinely absent from every free guide, built on [California SB 243](https://sd18.senate.ca.gov/news/first-nation-ai-chatbot-safeguards-signed-law): the first US law regulating companion chatbots, operative January 1, 2026, with a private right of action (the greater of actual damages or $1,000 per violation, injunctive relief, attorney's fees), mandated and published self-harm crisis protocols, AI-disclosure duties, and three-hourly break reminders for known minors — with [model complaint language and the first operator safety reports due to California's Office of Suicide Prevention July 1, 2027](https://natlawreview.com/article/when-ai-feels-human-californias-sb-243-opens-door-private-lawsuits). The chapter includes an honest "what if you're not in California" section covering the state copycats ([New York's June 2026 under-18 ban with fines up to $25,000 per violation](https://www.techtimes.com/articles/318339/20260613/new-york-bans-ai-companion-chatbots-kids-unanimous-vote-sets-25000-fines.htm), plus [Idaho, Oregon, Washington, and Nebraska](https://www.multistate.us/insider/2026/4/30/state-childrens-online-safety-laws-expand-beyond-social-media-in-2026)) and pending federal law. A crucial correction is baked in: the [Character.AI/Google settlements (filed January 7, 2026)](https://www.cnn.com/2026/01/07/business/character-ai-google-settle-teen-suicide-lawsuit) are confidential, no-admission agreements that entitle a nonparty parent to demand nothing from any platform — the book cites them only as narrative evidence that litigation pressure is real, and the proposal budgets a legal reviewer for this chapter.

**The named protocols** — the Quiet Audit, the Attachment Check-In, the Family AI Agreement, the Breakup — are flag-planted as named assets a fast-follower must awkwardly rename. They are framed, deliberately, as **consensus translation, not clinical invention**: no validated intervention for adolescent AI-companion de-attachment yet exists, so every protocol carries its provenance (APA advisory, JED guidance, the adolescent-attachment literature) in plain sight, a named external clinical advisory board stands behind the book in the proposal, and no "trademark-able" or validation claims are made. That humility is not a concession; it is the defense against the expert backlash that hit Haidt.

**The migration question is handled as a question.** The book does not claim the Character.AI restriction proved that bans displace use. What's citable: on October 29, 2025, [Character.AI announced it would remove open-ended chat for under-18 users](https://blog.character.ai/u18-chat-announcement/), with [phased removal beginning November 24, 2025](https://techcrunch.com/2025/10/29/character-ai-is-killing-the-chatbot-experience-for-minors/) — ending open-ended chat for minors, not banning them from the platform. The company's own CEO [predicted teens might flock to other AI platforms](https://techcrunch.com/2025/10/29/character-ai-is-killing-the-chatbot-experience-for-minors/), [California officials warned families to watch for exactly that](https://www.kqed.org/science/1999388/california-warns-families-to-watch-out-for-teens-as-character-ai-shuts-off-chatbot-access), and [reporting documents teens bypassing age checks](https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/2025/11/25/characterai-ban-teens/) — but no published study measures where minor users actually went. The book says so, and the displacement question is a designed module of the package's commissioned 2,000-parent survey, converting the concept's weakest factual claim into its ownable original finding. The anti-restriction-alone argument therefore rests where it doesn't need migration data at all: on the secrecy and relationship dynamics — treating a relationship like a device quota produces hiding, not safety.

## 7. Why Now — the Corrected Evidence

The why-now case survives on honest numbers, and the honest numbers are the authority moat, because deploying inflated ones would falsify the book's premise about itself.

**The prevalence story is told in two numbers, always together.** In [Common Sense Media's *Talk, Trust, and Trade-Offs*](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/research/talk-trust-and-trade-offs-how-and-why-teens-use-ai-companions) (July 16, 2025; nationally representative NORC survey, n=1,060 teens 13–17), 72% of teens said they had used an AI companion at least once — under a broad, usage-based definition that counts general-purpose chatbots like ChatGPT used for personal conversation. Companionship-specific measures run far lower: [Pew Research](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2026/02/24/how-teens-use-and-view-ai/) (n=1,458 parent-teen pairs, published February 2026) finds 64% of teens have ever used any AI chatbot, 16% use one for casual conversation, and 12% — roughly 1 in 8, about three million kids — for emotional support or advice. A [peer-reviewed FAU/UW study in the *Journal of Adolescence*](https://www.fau.edu/newsdesk/articles/chatbot-ai-study-teens) (May 2026, n=3,466) triangulates the middle: 60.2% have tried an AI companion, 11.4% are near-daily users, and 47.1% of users experienced at least one of thirteen measured risks. The book never cites 72% as companion-app prevalence, and the marketing hook is the bulletproof wording: [**one in four American teens has, at least once, taken a serious problem to an AI instead of a person**](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/sites/default/files/research/report/talk-trust-and-trade-offs_2025_web.pdf) — the corrected version of the old "33%" claim, whose true base is [users, not all teens](https://x.com/CommonSense/status/1945486943917371578), and whose tense is "has at least once," not "prefers."

**The harm evidence is capability evidence, labeled as such.** The [Common Sense Media risk assessment conducted with Brainstorm, the Stanford Lab for Mental Health Innovation (April 30, 2025)](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/press-releases/ai-companions-decoded-common-sense-media-recommends-ai-companion-safety-standards) found adult testers posing as teens on Character.AI, Nomi, and Replika could readily elicit sexual role-play and self-harm encouragement — proof of what these systems will produce for a user presenting as a teen, never framed as what typically happens. A [Drexel-led study, peer-reviewed at CHI 2026](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.15783) ([ACM DOI](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3772318.3790597)), analyzed 318 Reddit posts by self-disclosed 13–17-year-olds and found their described attachments mapped onto all six components of behavioral addiction — salience, mood modification, tolerance, withdrawal, conflict, relapse — presented as an analysis of teen self-reports, with no prevalence number attached.

**The catalysts are stacked and dated.** The [settlements of five teen-harm lawsuits including the Setzer case (January 7, 2026)](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/01/07/google-characterai-to-settle-suits-involving-suicides-ai-chatbots.html); [SB 243 in force since January 1, 2026](https://fpf.org/blog/understanding-the-new-wave-of-chatbot-legislation-california-sb-243-and-beyond/); [MIT Technology Review naming AI companions a 2026 Breakthrough Technology](https://www.technologyreview.com/2026/01/12/1130018/ai-companions-chatbots-relationships-2026-breakthrough-technology/); and the regulatory wave now cresting: the [federal GUARD Act advanced out of Senate Judiciary unanimously on April 30, 2026](https://rollcall.com/2026/04/30/ban-on-kids-companion-chatbots-advanced-by-senate-committee/), and [New York enacted its under-18 ban in June 2026](https://www.techtimes.com/articles/318339/20260613/new-york-bans-ai-companion-chatbots-kids-unanimous-vote-sets-25000-fines.htm).

**The primary frame is therefore inverted from the original concept: not "you can't ban it" but "the ban just passed — now what?"** The book may ship into a country that has legally restricted the thing it addresses, and that is the sales case, not the threat: the book is the manual for the enforcement gap — age-check evasion, shadow platforms, the 13-year-old with a fake birthdate, and above all the already-attached kid whom no statute un-attaches. The precedent runs in the book's favor: legislation historically amplifies parent-book demand, and *The Anxious Generation* sold through the entire state phone-ban wave. Forward pegs are on the calendar through the pub window: the GUARD Act floor fight, New York enforcement, the first SB 243 operator safety reports (July 1, 2027), the expanding wrongful-death litigation wave against OpenAI, Common Sense Media's now-annual teen-AI census, and every platform parental-dashboard launch — each dashboard manufacturing exactly the discovery moments this book monetizes. An early "Why the Settings Aren't Enough" section addresses OpenAI age prediction and Meta parental controls directly, and the bill-signing op-ed is pre-drafted.

## 9. The Anti-Panic Calibration Stance

This book is structurally incapable of being shelved with the moral-panic literature, because calibration is load-bearing, not decorative. The green/yellow/red framework sits at chapter 4 — before any alarm chapter — and the book leads with the field's own reassuring findings: in Common Sense Media's survey, [80% of teen companion users spend more time with real friends than with their bots, and about half view companions as tools, not friends](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/sites/default/files/research/report/talk-trust-and-trade-offs_2025_web.pdf) ([CBS coverage](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/ai-digital-friendship-with-teens-common-sense-media-study/)). The benefits literature gets the same honest treatment: the [peer-reviewed CHI 2026 study](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3772318.3790597) that documented addiction-pattern language in teen self-reports *also* documents teens disengaging through renewed real-life relationships — direct evidence for the book's rebuild thesis — and the neurodivergent-kids chapter takes seriously that bots can be a genuine social practice ground as well as a deeper trap. The methodology chapter cites the moral-panic skeptics in the academic literature by name as load-bearing sources, not opponents to be defended against; publishes every headline number with its definition, base, and range; and the package's commissioned 2,000-parent survey is designed to own the *conservative* numbers. The book's rigor is its differentiation: in a field where the loudest statistics deflate on air, the author who volunteers the two-number framing is the one the bookers can trust.

## 10. Author Requirements and the Two-Track Strategy

**The requirement:** a licensed clinical psychologist or adolescent psychiatrist with an active teen practice, fluency in the primary research, media capability, and no moral-panic track record — optionally paired with a tech-accountability journalist for the reported spine. The structure is deliberately load-bearing — named protocols, scripts, the Agreement, the triage section — so the book works with a very good author rather than only a celebrity one.

**The thesis and the bench are now aligned.** The original "don't ban" counterprogramming hook contradicted the on-record positions of every named institutional candidate: Stanford Brainstorm and Common Sense Media jointly rated social AI companions an ["unacceptable risk" for anyone under 18 (April 30, 2025)](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/press-releases/ai-companions-decoded-common-sense-media-recommends-ai-companion-safety-standards), and JED and the APA hold restriction-first positions. That hook is dead. The corrected thesis — **bans alone backfire; this book is the working exit ramp to zero** — ends the attachment rather than accommodating it, which means Track A clinicians can sign without repudiating their institutions, and the Common Sense Media / Child Mind Institute co-brand becomes closeable instead of self-contradicting. Closing that co-brand before circulation converts the largest free "rival" into the foreword and the school bulk channel.

**The realistic plan, dated honestly from July 2026.** The original Labor-Day-2026 proposal gate is arithmetically dead and is abandoned.

- **Track B is now primary:** a working adolescent clinician paired with a tech-accountability journalist co-author — a findable package, not a unicorn hunt.
- **Track A names are reclassified as endorsers and foreword candidates,** not acquisition targets: the Damour-class figures are visibly servicing their own franchises and platforms.
- **The 60-day proposal build runs in parallel with the search,** and the two demand-proof assets are funded now, before circulation: the Attachment Check-In quiz funnel (reporting real email-capture and preorder-intent conversion as measured, proprietary proof of the trade-up bet) and the 2,000-parent survey with its migration module. Monitoring vendors of the Bark/Aura type — where the category's dollars currently flow — are treated as bundling and affiliate channels, not ignored.
- **Target: proposal out the day an author signs — realistically Q4 2026** — with Turkle's September 29, 2026 launch treated as the start of the me-too clock, and a crashed spring-2027 publication evaluated against the default back-to-school-2027/2028 window. Every month after Turkle lands, the "what do I actually DO" vacuum she creates is being filled by someone.
- **The hard gate stands: the proposal does not circulate without a signed author.** A failed search kills the deal, correctly — this concept is worth nothing without the clinician.

## 11. What This Concept Honestly Is

A category-defining protocol book in a proven lane (*Good Inside*, *How to Talk So Kids Will Listen*), aimed at the single question the entire announced AI-parenting wave — Turkle, Twenge, Suskind, the AAP, Susskind, Muldoon — describes and none answers: *my kid is attached to a machine that performs love; what do I do tonight, this week, and until it's over?* Its evidence base is the corrected, two-number, triangulated version that survives fact-checking on air. Its legal chapter is built on statutes that actually confer rights, reviewed by counsel. Its thesis is compatible with the institutions whose endorsement it needs. Its schedule is the honest one. And its remaining risks — an invisible rival deal, an author search that could fail, a trade-up bet supported by measured funnel data rather than proof — are priced, named, and carried on the cover sheet rather than hidden in the copy.
