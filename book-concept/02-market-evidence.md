# Market Evidence Dossier

## *Your Kid's AI Best Friend* — The Evidence Base, With Its Caveats Attached

**Prepared July 2026.** This dossier assembles every load-bearing market claim behind the concept, in the form each claim can actually survive: correct base, correct definition, correct date, correct legal scope. Where the evidence is thinner than the headline version that circulates in press coverage, the thinner version is stated here first, deliberately. A book whose differentiation is rigor cannot rest its own case on inflated numbers, and this proposal does not. Section 6 lists, plainly, what remains unverified or contested.

---

## 1. The Phenomenon: How Many Kids, Under Which Definition

The single most-quoted number in this category — "72% of teens use AI companions" — is real, but it is honest only when its definition travels with it. This dossier uses a two-number framing throughout, and the book will too.

**The broad number.** In Common Sense Media's [*Talk, Trust, and Trade-Offs*](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/research/talk-trust-and-trade-offs-how-and-why-teens-use-ai-companions) (released July 16, 2025; a nationally representative NORC survey of 1,060 US teens aged 13–17, fielded April–May 2025), [72% of teens said they had used an AI companion at least once](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/press-releases/nearly-3-in-4-teens-have-used-ai-companions-new-national-survey-finds) — under a usage-based definition ("digital friends or characters you can text or talk with") that explicitly counts general-purpose chatbots like ChatGPT or Claude when used for personal conversation, and excludes homework-helper use. The same survey found [52% are regular users](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/press-releases/nearly-3-in-4-teens-have-used-ai-companions-new-national-survey-finds), defined as at least a few times a month ([toplines](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/sites/default/files/research/talk-trust-and-trade-offs_2025_toplines.pdf)); a roughly 13% daily-use figure appears in secondary summaries and is less firmly pinned than the 52%. Coverage: [TechCrunch, July 21, 2025](https://techcrunch.com/2025/07/21/72-of-u-s-teens-have-used-ai-companions-study-finds/).

**The narrow number.** Companionship-specific measures run far lower. [Pew Research](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2026/02/24/how-teens-use-and-view-ai/) (n=1,458 parent–teen pairs, fielded September–October 2025, published February 2026) found 64% of teens have ever used any AI chatbot, [16% use one for casual conversation, and 12% for emotional support or advice](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2025/12/09/teens-social-media-and-ai-chatbots-2025/) — roughly 3 million US teens, or about 1 in 8. Note the definitional tell: Pew's ceiling for *any* chatbot use ever (64%) sits below Common Sense Media's 72% "companion" figure, which is only possible because the two surveys are measuring different things. The 72% must never be cited as dedicated companion-app prevalence or as teens "already using one."

**Peer-reviewed corroboration.** A Florida Atlantic University/University of Washington study ([*Journal of Adolescence*, May 2026, n=3,466](https://www.fau.edu/newsdesk/articles/chatbot-ai-study-teens)) found 60.2% of teens have ever tried an AI companion and 11.4% are near-daily users — independently landing in the same range as both surveys' respective definitions and confirming this is mass behavior, not a niche.

**The serious-conversation finding — corrected base.** The widely repeated "33% chose the bot over you" claim requires correction: among teens *who have used* AI companions, [33% have chosen to discuss important or serious matters with an AI companion instead of a real person at least once](https://x.com/CommonSense/status/1945486943917371578) (17% once or twice, 12% occasionally, 4% frequently) — roughly 24% of *all* US teens, and a one-time behavior, not a standing preference ([full report PDF](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/sites/default/files/research/report/talk-trust-and-trade-offs_2025_web.pdf); [CBS News coverage](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/ai-digital-friendship-with-teens-common-sense-media-study/)). The defensible marketing wording, used throughout this package: **"One in four American teens has, at least once, taken a serious problem to an AI instead of a person."** This is not to be conflated with the separate finding that 31% of users rate AI conversations as satisfying as, or more satisfying than, conversations with friends.

**The calibrating numbers, stated in the same breath.** The same Common Sense report found [80% of teen users spend more time with real friends than with bots, and about half view companions as tools rather than friends](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/sites/default/files/research/report/talk-trust-and-trade-offs_2025_web.pdf). These figures are not a weakness to hide; they are the anti-panic positioning the book's Green/Yellow/Red calibration framework is built on.

**Durability of the category.** [MIT Technology Review named AI companions one of its 10 Breakthrough Technologies for 2026](https://www.technologyreview.com/2026/01/12/1130018/ai-companions-chatbots-relationships-2026-breakthrough-technology/) (published January 12, 2026, citing Anthropic, Character.AI, OpenAI, and Replika) — this is a capability every major platform now ships, not a single app with a usage cliff.

## 2. The Harm and Concern Evidence

**The risk-assessment verdict.** On April 30, 2025, Common Sense Media — in a [risk assessment conducted with Brainstorm: The Stanford Lab for Mental Health Innovation](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/press-releases/ai-companions-decoded-common-sense-media-recommends-ai-companion-safety-standards) at Stanford School of Medicine — rated social AI companions an "unacceptable risk" for anyone under 18, after testing Character.AI, Nomi, and Replika ([full assessment PDF](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/sites/default/files/pug/csm-ai-risk-assessment-social-ai-companions_final.pdf); [The Markup coverage](https://themarkup.org/artificial-intelligence/2025/04/30/kids-should-avoid-ai-companion-bots-under-force-of-law-assessment-says)). Two scope notes the book must respect: the verdict covers social companion apps specifically, and the date is April 30, 2025 — July 16, 2025 was the separate usage survey.

**What the red-teaming showed — and what it can and cannot prove.** In that same assessment, testers posing as teenagers readily elicited sexual role-play, self-harm encouragement, and other harmful content from the tested platforms ([Stanford's August 2025 news article publicized the findings](https://news.stanford.edu/stories/2025/08/ai-companions-chatbots-teens-young-people-risks-dangers-study) ahead of California AB 1064 testimony; see also [KQED](https://www.kqed.org/news/12038154/kids-talking-ai-companion-chatbots-stanford-researchers-say-thats-bad-idea)). This is **capability evidence** — these systems will produce this content for a user presenting as a teen — not prevalence evidence. It was adversarial red-team testing, not observation of typical teen use, and it is not peer-reviewed. The dossier states this because a skeptical reviewer will, and the book's credibility depends on getting there first.

**Peer-reviewed risk exposure.** The [FAU/UW *Journal of Adolescence* study](https://www.fau.edu/newsdesk/articles/chatbot-ai-study-teens) (n=3,466) found 47.1% of teen users experienced at least one of 13 measured risks, with 13-year-olds the most exposed age group — the strongest peer-reviewed prevalence-of-risk figure currently available.

**The addiction-language study — with its peer-review status stated exactly.** A Drexel-led study ([arXiv:2507.15783](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.15783)), peer-reviewed and published in the Proceedings of CHI 2026 ([ACM DOI 10.1145/3772318.3790597](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3772318.3790597); [Drexel announcement](https://drexel.edu/news/archive/2026/April/teen-AI-chatbot-addiction)), qualitatively analyzed 318 Reddit posts from r/CharacterAI by users self-disclosing as 13–17 and found their described attachments mapped onto all six components of behavioral addiction — salience, mood modification, tolerance, withdrawal, conflict, and relapse — with reported sleep loss, academic decline, and strained relationships. It is independently corroborated by a [FAccT 2026 paper](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3805689.3812235). Citation rules this package observes: it is an analysis of teen *self-reports* (unverified ages, a self-selected venue, no clinical diagnosis); no prevalence number can be attached to it; and the paper also documents teens disengaging via renewed real-life relationships — a finding that supports the book's out-relate thesis rather than a panic thesis.

**The litigation record — with the legal caveat that governs Chapter 9.** In court filings made public January 7, 2026, Character Technologies, its co-founders, and Google [agreed to settle five lawsuits](https://www.cnn.com/2026/01/07/business/character-ai-google-settle-teen-suicide-lawsuit) alleging the platform contributed to teen suicides and mental-health harms, anchored by *Garcia v. Character Technologies* (the Sewell Setzer III case) ([CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/01/07/google-characterai-to-settle-suits-involving-suicides-ai-chatbots.html); [Washington Post](https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/2026/01/07/google-character-settle-lawsuits-suicide/); [Bloomberg Law](https://news.bloomberglaw.com/litigation/character-ai-google-agree-to-settle-teen-chatbot-harm-lawsuits)). The terms are **confidential, with no admission of liability and no disclosed injunctive relief**. A confidential settlement of someone else's lawsuit entitles a nonparty parent to demand nothing from any platform — which is why the book's platform-combat chapter is built on statute (Section 4), and cites these settlements only as evidence that litigation pressure on the industry is real. Broader litigation continues, including new suits against Character.AI and a parallel wave against OpenAI.

## 3. Parental Demand Signals

**Parental discomfort is mainstream and measured.** Pew's parent–teen pair survey (published February 2026) found that [emotional support or advice from a chatbot is the only teen AI use a majority of parents oppose — just 18% of parents are comfortable with it](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2026/02/24/what-parents-say-about-their-teens-ai-use/). The same research documents the awareness gap the book monetizes: roughly 4 in 10 parents have never discussed chatbots with their teen, and parents underestimate use — 64% of teens have used a chatbot versus about half of parents aware ([CBS News](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/ai-teens-parents-pew-survey/); [The Hill](https://thehill.com/policy/healthcare/5753210-parents-teens-ai-chatbot-use-pew-survey/)). The concern is not an elite or partisan phenomenon: settlement coverage ran across [CNN](https://www.cnn.com/2026/01/07/business/character-ai-google-settle-teen-suicide-lawsuit), [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/01/07/google-characterai-to-settle-suits-involving-suicides-ai-chatbots.html), and the [Washington Post](https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/2026/01/07/google-character-settle-lawsuits-suicide/), and — per this project's adversarial fact-check — extended through mass-market broadcast (CBS Mornings, ABC, Axios, local TV) and conservative talk media, making the audience demonstrably cross-partisan.

**The stopgap free-guide wave — a demand proxy, stated honestly.** Institutions are publishing web guides at a pace that signals a book-shaped hole: the [JED Foundation](https://jedfoundation.org/why-your-teen-shouldnt-be-using-ai-companions-and-what-to-do-if-they-are/) ("Why Your Teen Shouldn't Be Using AI Companions – and What to Do If They Are"), the [APA](https://www.apa.org/topics/artificial-intelligence-machine-learning/teens-chatbots-parents) ("Your teen turned to AI instead of you: what experts say parents can do"), the [Child Mind Institute](https://childmind.org/article/ai-chatbots-and-teens/), [Children and Screens](https://www.childrenandscreens.org/learn-explore/research/ai-social-companions-and-youth-what-parents-need-to-know/), and [PolitiFact's parent guide](https://www.politifact.com/article/2025/dec/19/AI-chatbots-companions-talk-to-kids-parent-guide/), among others. The honest reading, which this package adopts: these guides are simultaneously the demand signal *and* partial free competition. JED and the APA already publish the operative core of taper-not-cold-turkey, conversation-first, warning-signs, and crisis-referral guidance. The book's moat is therefore not that this content is unpublishable by nonprofits — it is script-level depth, a reported case-study spine, negotiated age-tiered Family AI Agreements, and the statutory platform-combat playbook genuinely absent from every free guide. The free guides are cited on the jacket as demand evidence, not denied.

**The comp precedent — with verified figures.** [*The Anxious Generation*](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/729231/the-anxious-generation-by-jonathan-haidt/) (Jonathan Haidt, Penguin Press, March 2024) has sold over two million copies per its publisher and, as of June 2026, had spent [111 consecutive weeks on the NYT nonfiction bestseller list, reaching #1 repeatedly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Anxious_Generation) (at least 11 separate weeks). The accurate formulation — "a #1 NYT bestseller that has spent over two years on the list" — proves that this parent demographic buys books about this fear at volume, and that legislation (the state phone-ban wave) amplified rather than cannibalized demand. Two honesty notes: Haidt is an **active incumbent, not a friendly predecessor** — he runs an explicit ["Don't Give Your Child Any AI Companions" campaign](https://www.afterbabel.com/p/dont-give-your-child-an-ai-companion) and has extended his franchise with [*The Amazing Generation* (January 2026)](https://www.npr.org/2026/01/27/nx-s1-5686531/anxious-generation-jonathan-haidt-screen-time-amazing-generation), meaning a substantial share of "the Haidt buyer" is a Haidt-brand buyer. This book is positioned as the answer to the question his abstinence position cannot answer: what to do when the ban fails, or when the kid is already attached.

**The adjacent publishing wave — demand evidence, and the competitive frame stated plainly.** The shelf next door is crowded and filling, and this package never claims otherwise: Sherry Turkle's [*Artificial Intimacy*](https://www.hachettebookgroup.com/titles/sherry-turkle/artificial-intimacy/9780316573962/) (Little, Brown, September 29, 2026), Jean Twenge's [*10 Rules for Raising Kids in a High-Tech World*](https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/10-Rules-for-Raising-Kids-in-a-High-Tech-World/Jean-M-Twenge/9781668099995) (Simon & Schuster, September 2025), Dana Suskind's [*Human Raised*](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/811792/human-raised-by-dana-suskind-md/) (Dutton, July 2026), the AAP's *Connecting* (Moreno & Radesky, October 2026), Daniel Susskind's *What Should My Children Do?* (Penguin Press, November 2026), and James Muldoon's [*Love Machines*](https://www.faber.co.uk/product/9780571399277-love-machines/) (Faber, January 2026) — a wave [Publishers Weekly covered on May 4, 2026](https://www.publishersweekly.com/pw/by-topic/new-titles/adult-announcements/article/100308-new-books-on-kids-and-technology.html). That six major houses are paying for books at this intersection is itself market evidence. The niche claim that survived three adversarial preemption searches (deal coverage, [Publishers Marketplace](https://lunch.publishersmarketplace.com/category/ai/), catalogs, and every named natural author) is deliberately narrow: **as of July 2026, no traditionally published, book-length, teen-focused, clinician-authored scripts-and-protocols guide dedicated to AI-companion attachment exists or is announced.** Every qualifier in that sentence is load-bearing, and unannounced deals remain invisible. Turkle tells parents what these machines are doing to their kid; this book tells them what to do Tuesday night.

## 4. The Regulatory Calendar

Regulation is not a risk to this book's premise; it is the premise's delivery mechanism. Each item below is a dated, verifiable peg that regenerates coverage into the publication window.

**California SB 243 — in force now, first reports due July 1, 2027.** [SB 243](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billNavClient.xhtml?bill_id=202520260SB243), signed October 13, 2025, is the [first US state law specifically regulating companion chatbots](https://sd18.senate.ca.gov/news/first-nation-ai-chatbot-safeguards-signed-law); its core provisions became operative January 1, 2026. Operators must provide clear AI disclosure; maintain and publish a self-harm/suicide-prevention protocol with crisis referrals; and, for known minors, default to AI-generated-content disclosure, deliver break reminders at least every three hours, and take reasonable measures against sexually explicit material ([Future of Privacy Forum analysis](https://fpf.org/blog/understanding-the-new-wave-of-chatbot-legislation-california-sb-243-and-beyond/); [Gunderson client alert](https://www.gunder.com/en/news-insights/insights/client-insight-california-sb-243-new-compliance-requirements-for-operators-of-ai-companion-chatbots)). Enforcement is a **private right of action**: injunctive relief, damages of the greater of actual damages or $1,000 per violation, and attorney's fees ([National Law Review](https://natlawreview.com/article/when-ai-feels-human-californias-sb-243-opens-door-private-lawsuits)). Annual operator reports to California's Office of Suicide Prevention begin **July 1, 2027** — a dated news event sitting inside the publication window. Caveats the book states rather than hides: SB 243 does not mandate age verification, does not ban minors from companion chatbots, and protects California users — hence the playbook's honest non-California section.

**The federal GUARD Act — live and moving.** The GUARD Act ([S. 3062](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119/congress/senate-bill/3062/text)), which would ban AI companions for minors nationwide and mandate age verification, [advanced out of Senate Judiciary unanimously on April 30, 2026](https://rollcall.com/2026/04/30/ban-on-kids-companion-chatbots-advanced-by-senate-committee/), with a bipartisan House companion (H.R. 8623) and pending First Amendment objections. The floor fight — and, if it passes, the signing and the enforcement gap that follows — are pre-drafted op-ed moments for the author.

**State copycats.** New York [enacted an under-18 companion-chatbot ban in June 2026 with fines up to $25,000 per violation](https://www.techtimes.com/articles/318339/20260613/new-york-bans-ai-companion-chatbots-kids-unanimous-vote-sets-25000-fines.htm); Idaho, Oregon, Washington, and Nebraska have [enacted their own laws](https://www.multistate.us/insider/2026/4/30/state-childrens-online-safety-laws-expand-beyond-social-media-in-2026) ([see also Sheppard Mullin's SB 243 analysis](https://www.sheppardhealthlaw.com/2025/12/articles/state-legislation/california-sb-243-setting-new-standards-for-regulating-and-ensuring-integrity-of-ai-companion-chatbots/)). The book ships into a country actively legislating this problem — which is the frame: *the ban just passed; now what?*

**The Character.AI policy change — exact dates and scope.** On **October 29, 2025**, Character.AI [announced it would remove open-ended chat for users under 18](https://blog.character.ai/u18-chat-announcement/); the [phased removal began November 24, 2025](https://blog.character.ai/an-update-on-changes-to-our-under-18-experience/), for US minors (a subset temporarily kept a one-hour-per-day capped experience), enforced via "age assurance" technology with third-party verification ([TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2025/10/29/character-ai-is-killing-the-chatbot-experience-for-minors/); [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/11/24/characterai-to-ban-teens-from-open-ended-chats-human-interaction-is-crucial-psychotherapist-says.html)). Precision matters: it restricted **open-ended chat specifically** — minors retained non-chat creative features — so it is accurately described as "ending open-ended chat for minors," not a platform ban. The restriction remains in place as of mid-2026. What happened to those users afterward is a hypothesis, not a finding; see Section 6.

**The forward peg calendar, verifiable today:** the GUARD Act floor fight; New York enforcement; first SB 243 operator safety reports (July 1, 2027); expanding wrongful-death litigation against OpenAI; Common Sense Media's now-annual teen-AI survey cycle; and each platform parental-dashboard launch, every one of which manufactures the discovery moments the book monetizes.

## 5. Audience Size and Buying Propensity

**The addressable base.** Approximately **42 million US children are aged 8–17** — a derived approximation from Census Bureau age-bucket data: [25.8M aged 12–17](https://www.childstats.gov/americaschildren/tables/pop1.asp) plus roughly two-thirds of the [24.2M aged 6–11](https://datacenter.aecf.org/data/tables/101-child-population-by-age-group). Cite as "~42 million" or "41–42 million." A discipline this package enforces on itself: the 13–17 survey findings are **not** extrapolated to 8–12s, whom no companion survey has measured.

**The engaged core.** Within that base, the honest concentric circles: [~3 million teens using a chatbot for emotional support or advice](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2025/12/09/teens-social-media-and-ai-chatbots-2025/) (Pew's 12%); [11.4% of teens near-daily companion users](https://www.fau.edu/newsdesk/articles/chatbot-ai-study-teens) (FAU); and [one in four teens who has at least once taken a serious problem to an AI instead of a person](https://x.com/CommonSense/status/1945486943917371578) (Common Sense Media). Every one of those circles has parents.

**The buyer.** The primary buyer is the parent — skewing mothers 35–55, the demographic whose purchasing power is demonstrated by *The Anxious Generation*'s [publisher-confirmed 2M+ copies and 111 consecutive weeks on the NYT list](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Anxious_Generation). Pew's finding that [only 18% of parents are comfortable with chatbot emotional support](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2026/02/24/what-parents-say-about-their-teens-ai-use/), combined with its finding that roughly 4 in 10 have never discussed chatbots with their teen, defines the two buyer states the book serves: the crisis buyer who just found the logs, and the anxious-but-unaware buyer for whom the discovery moment must be manufactured (the Attachment Check-In quiz funnel).

**Institutional channels.** The schools/PTA bulk channel is the Haidt-proven route to this demographic — the collective-action chapter is written to convert readers into it — and Haidt's own pledge machinery ([anxiousgeneration.com / After Babel](https://www.afterbabel.com/p/dont-give-your-child-an-ai-companion)) reaches the same PTA channel, which the plan treats as infrastructure to ride (guest essay, resource listing) rather than duplicate. The category's current dollars also flow to subscription monitoring vendors (Bark- and Aura-class services now market AI-chatbot detection), which the commercial plan treats as a bundling/affiliate channel rather than ignored competition.

**Buying-propensity evidence — stated at its true strength.** Direct proof that this topic converts to trade-book purchases is limited, and this dossier says so. The affirmative datapoints: the *Anxious Generation* precedent above (this parent fear, adjacent topic, historic volume, sales sustained *through* a legislative wave); the six-house adjacent-shelf acquisition wave in Section 3 (publishers' own money as a demand signal); and — per this project's adversarial demand review — one retail datapoint at the exact AI-parenting intersection, De Kai's [*Raising AI*](https://www.amazon.com/Raising-AI-Essential-Parenting-Future/dp/0262049767) (MIT Press, 2025), which earned bestseller-list appearances and a paperback pickup. The sobering counterweight, also from that review: post-Haidt follower titles have generally underperformed the lead title, which is why the P&L is sized to clinician-protocol comps (the *Good Inside* lane), not to *Anxious Generation* fantasy, with format hedged below hardcover-only economics. The remaining conversion question is addressed with money rather than assertion: the pre-proposal quiz funnel and 2,000-parent survey are designed to produce measured email-capture and preorder-intent conversion as proprietary proof of demand.

## 6. What Remains Unverified or Contested

A skeptical editor will probe here first. These are the claims this package does not treat as established, and how each is handled.

1. **The migration hypothesis.** No published data documents where Character.AI's minor users went after the November 2025 restriction. What is citable: [Character.AI's own CEO predicted teens "might flock to other AI platforms"](https://techcrunch.com/2025/10/29/character-ai-is-killing-the-chatbot-experience-for-minors/), [California officials warned families to watch for exactly that](https://www.kqed.org/science/1999388/california-warns-families-to-watch-out-for-teens-as-character-ai-shuts-off-chatbot-access), and [reporting documents teens bypassing age checks](https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/2025/11/25/characterai-ban-teens/) — but whether the restriction reduced use or displaced it to less-moderated platforms **has not been measured**. The book's anti-ban-alone argument therefore rests additionally on relationship-dynamics grounds that need no migration data, and the displacement question is a designed module of the commissioned 2,000-parent survey — converting the package's weakest factual claim into its ownable original finding.

2. **The single-survey dependency.** The headline 72/52/33 numbers all trace to one n=1,060 self-report survey ([Common Sense Media/NORC, July 2025](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/research/talk-trust-and-trade-offs-how-and-why-teens-use-ai-companions)). Triangulation exists — [Pew](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2026/02/24/how-teens-use-and-view-ai/) (n=1,458 pairs) and [FAU/UW](https://www.fau.edu/newsdesk/articles/chatbot-ai-study-teens) (n=3,466, peer-reviewed) — but the surveys use different definitions and produce materially different numbers (72% vs. 64% vs. 60.2% ever-use; 12–16% companionship-specific). The book's methodology chapter publishes this triangulation with stated ranges rather than picking the largest number; the ~13% daily-use figure is flagged as less firmly pinned than the 52% regular-use figure.

3. **The 8–12 age band is unmeasured.** No companion-usage survey covers children under 13. The "companions colonizing the imaginary-friend years" chapter rests on reporting (e.g., [Axios on AI as the new imaginary friend](https://www.axios.com/2025/12/21/ai-companions-new-imaginary-friend-children-teens)) and clinical observation, not prevalence data, and the book says so.

4. **Capability vs. prevalence in the harm evidence.** The most alarming findings — sexual content and self-harm encouragement elicited with teen personas — come from [adversarial red-team testing that is not peer-reviewed](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/sites/default/files/pug/csm-ai-risk-assessment-social-ai-companions_final.pdf). They establish what these systems *will* do, not how often they do it in typical teen use. The peer-reviewed layer (FAU's 47.1%-of-users-hit-at-least-one-risk; the CHI 2026 addiction-language study) carries the prevalence weight, within its own limits: the CHI study analyzes self-reports with unverified ages from a self-selected venue and supports no prevalence number.

5. **The trade-up bet.** There is no direct proof that parents served by free JED/APA/Child Mind guides convert to a $28 hardcover at scale. This is the package's honest commercial wager; the mitigations (measured funnel data before circulation, format hedging, institutional co-brand, comp-sizing to the follower-title base rate rather than the outlier) are described in Section 5.

6. **Contested framing in the academic literature.** A skeptical research literature (Przybylski and the Bangor-associated authors are the named exemplars in this project's adversarial review) targets ahead-of-evidence prescriptive confidence in exactly this category. The book's defense is structural: calibration before alarm (the Green/Yellow/Red chapter), protocols framed as consensus translation with [APA](https://www.apa.org/topics/artificial-intelligence-machine-learning/teens-chatbots-parents)/[JED](https://jedfoundation.org/why-your-teen-shouldnt-be-using-ai-companions-and-what-to-do-if-they-are/) provenance rather than clinical invention, a named external clinical advisory board, and citing the skeptics as load-bearing in the methodology chapter rather than defending against them.

7. **Pipeline invisibility.** Exhaustive preemption search cannot prove a negative: an unannounced, clinician-fronted rival deal could surface any month, and Turkle's September 29, 2026 launch is treated as the start of the me-too clock. The only defense is speed plus assets a fast-follower cannot cheaply replicate (the named-protocol suite and the proprietary survey), with [Publishers Marketplace deal flow](https://lunch.publishersmarketplace.com/category/ai/) monitored continuously.

8. **Settlement terms are, and will remain, unknown.** Because the [Character.AI/Google settlements are confidential with no admission of liability](https://news.bloomberglaw.com/litigation/character-ai-google-agree-to-settle-teen-chatbot-harm-lawsuits), no claim about their terms, their effect on platform behavior, or any entitlement they create for other families can ever be made in this book.
