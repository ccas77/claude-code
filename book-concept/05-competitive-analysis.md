## The Frame: Crowded Adjacent Shelf, Empty Niche

Let us begin with what this analysis will *not* claim, because the claim is checkably false and any acquiring editor who reads [Publishers Weekly's May 4, 2026 roundup of the AI-parenting wave](https://www.publishersweekly.com/pw/by-topic/new-titles/adult-announcements/article/100308-new-books-on-kids-and-technology.html) knows it: the shelf is not empty. Between September 2025 and November 2026, at least six traditionally published books about children, families, and artificial intelligence arrive from major houses — Twenge, Muldoon, Suskind, Turkle, the American Academy of Pediatrics, and Susskind — trailing behind Jonathan Haidt, who built the category, and De Kai, who proved a publisher would take the AI-parenting intersection to market. Publishers Weekly has covered this as a wave because it is one.

The defensible claim is narrower, and every qualifier in it is load-bearing: **as of July 2026, no traditionally published, book-length, teen-focused, clinician-authored scripts-and-protocols guide dedicated to AI-companion attachment exists or is announced** — a conclusion that survived exhaustive search of [Publishers Marketplace deal coverage](https://lunch.publishersmarketplace.com/category/ai/), publisher catalogs ([Hachette](https://www.hachettebookgroup.com/titles/sherry-turkle/artificial-intimacy/9780316573962/), [Simon & Schuster](https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/10-Rules-for-Raising-Kids-in-a-High-Tech-World/Jean-M-Twenge/9781668099995), [Penguin Random House](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/811792/human-raised-by-dana-suskind-md/)), the PW previews, and the publication plans of every natural author for such a book. Unannounced deals remain invisible — that residual risk is addressed in Section 5.

The wave is not a problem to be explained away. It is the marketing budget this book does not have to spend. Each of the titles below will put "teens and AI companions" on morning shows, op-ed pages, and school-newsletter agendas — and each of them, by design or by DNA, stops at the moment the parent asks the only question that converts anxiety into a purchase: *"Fine. It's Tuesday night, I just found the chat logs — what do I do?"* That question is the lane this book occupies alone.

## 1. The Named Adjacent Wave, Title by Title

### Jonathan Haidt — *The Anxious Generation* (Penguin Press, March 2024): the category-maker, and an active incumbent

[*The Anxious Generation*](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/729231/the-anxious-generation-by-jonathan-haidt/) created the modern parents-and-technology category: over two million copies sold per its publisher, and — [as of June 2026 — 111 consecutive weeks on the New York Times nonfiction bestseller list](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Anxious_Generation), reaching #1 repeatedly. It proved the buyer (mothers 35–55), the collective-action playbook (phone-free schools, pledge drives), and the PTA/district bulk channel this book's chapter 13 is built to inherit.

It would be convenient to describe Haidt as a friendly predecessor who "stays in the phones lane." He does not. Since late 2025 Haidt has run an explicit ["Don't Give Your Child Any AI Companions" campaign](https://www.afterbabel.com/p/dont-give-your-child-an-ai-companion) through After Babel and a sustained LinkedIn drumbeat, reaching the exact PTA channel this book targets — and preaching abstinence, the position this book's thesis complicates. He is also actively extending his franchise: [*The Amazing Generation*, his book for kids, arrived in January 2026](https://www.npr.org/2026/01/27/nx-s1-5686531/anxious-generation-jonathan-haidt-screen-time-amazing-generation), evidence that he absorbs category demand rather than referring it onward.

**Why he does not occupy the lane:** Haidt's position is a rule, not a protocol. "Don't give your child an AI companion" has no chapter for the majority case — the kid who already has one. Under Common Sense Media's broad definition, [nearly three-quarters of teens have tried an AI companion at least once](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/press-releases/nearly-3-in-4-teens-have-used-ai-companions-new-national-survey-finds); [Pew's companionship-specific measures find roughly one in eight teens using chatbots for emotional support or advice](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2026/02/24/how-teens-use-and-view-ai/) — about three million kids. For those families, abstinence advice arrives too late by definition. This book is positioned as the evidence-based answer to the question Haidt's position cannot answer — *what if the ban fails, or your kid is already attached* — with a de-attachment endpoint (the Breakup protocol) compatible enough with his that the strategy is to ride his pledge machinery, not fight it. His annexation risk is treated as the top preemption scenario in Section 5.

### James Muldoon — *Love Machines* (Faber, January 2026): the intimacy economy, examined

[*Love Machines*](https://www.faber.co.uk/product/9780571399277-love-machines/) is the first of the 2026 wave: a critical examination of AI intimacy — companion apps, romantic chatbots, the commercialization of synthetic affection — written for a general-interest reader.

**Why it does not occupy the lane:** it is an adult-facing analysis of the phenomenon, not a parenting book at all. No triage, no scripts, no house rules, no teen focus. Its value to this package is agenda-setting: it legitimizes "AI intimacy" as a serious trade subject twelve months before this book would ship.

### Jean Twenge — *10 Rules for Raising Kids in a High-Tech World* (Simon & Schuster, September 2025): one rule where a protocol is needed

[Twenge's book](https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/10-Rules-for-Raising-Kids-in-a-High-Tech-World/Jean-M-Twenge/9781668099995) is the closest thing to prescriptive competition on the current shelf: a house-rules format from a proven data-driven author, and it does address AI companions and the Character.AI case directly.

**Why it does not occupy the lane:** AI companions get a rule, not a book. The format is ten commandments across the whole technology landscape — the companion-bot problem is one stop on the tour. A rule tells a parent *whether*; it does not walk her through detection, the first conversation, a negotiated Family AI Agreement, a taper, grief, relapse, or a platform complaint under California SB 243. Twenge also writes as a researcher, not a clinician with an active teen practice; the authority register is population data, not the consult room. Her book primes the buyer and leaves the buyer's hardest case unserved.

### Dana Suskind — *Human Raised* (Dutton, July 14, 2026): the early-childhood flank

[*Human Raised*](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/811792/human-raised-by-dana-suskind-md/) brings a physician's authority to raising young children in the AI era, publishing this month.

**Why it does not occupy the lane:** the developmental window is wrong. Suskind's career and this book center on early childhood; the AI-companion attachment crisis is concentrated in 13–17-year-olds ([Pew](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2026/02/24/how-teens-use-and-view-ai/); [FAU/UW, *Journal of Adolescence*, n=3,466](https://www.fau.edu/newsdesk/articles/chatbot-ai-study-teens)). A parent holding a fifteen-year-old's chat logs will not find her situation in a book about toddlers and preschoolers. Adjacent, credentialed, and non-competing.

### Sherry Turkle — *Artificial Intimacy* (Little, Brown, September 29, 2026): the nearest neighbor, and the clock

[*Artificial Intimacy: Who We Become When We Talk to Machines*](https://www.hachettebookgroup.com/titles/sherry-turkle/artificial-intimacy/9780316573962/) is the most important title on this map. Turkle is clinically trained, is the most famous chronicler of humans and machine relationships alive, covers chatbot attachment beginning in childhood, and cites the same 70-plus-percent teen statistic this proposal uses. Her book will dominate the fall 2026 conversation on exactly this subject.

**Why it does not occupy the lane:** Turkle writes diagnosis, and has for four decades — *what these machines are doing to us and who we become*. She does not write triage sections, word-for-word scripts, age-tiered family agreements, breakup protocols, or platform-complaint language, and her publisher is not positioning *Artificial Intimacy* as a how-to. The positioning sentence for this package, usable in every pitch meeting: **Turkle tells you what these machines are doing to your kid; this book tells you what to do Tuesday night.** The category has run this two-book pattern before — Haidt's diagnosis did not crowd out Becky Kennedy's protocols; it fed them. Turkle's launch is therefore treated as both the single biggest demand event in this book's favor and the starter pistol for rival proposals (Section 5).

### AAP / Megan Moreno & Jenny Radesky — *Connecting* (October 2026): institutional breadth, institutional constraints

The American Academy of Pediatrics enters the wave with [*Connecting*, by Moreno and Radesky](https://www.publishersweekly.com/pw/by-topic/new-titles/adult-announcements/article/100308-new-books-on-kids-and-technology.html), from the two most prominent pediatric digital-media researchers in the country.

**Why it does not occupy the lane:** it is a general digital-parenting book from a medical association — the full landscape of screens, social media, gaming, and AI, delivered in consensus-guideline register. Institutional authorship is a genuine strength and a genuine ceiling: an AAP book is calibrated to what a professional body can endorse, which is guidance, not scripts, and certainly not a chapter teaching parents to build an evidence file and invoke a private right of action against a chatbot operator. It validates the space, occupies the pediatrician's-waiting-room slot, and leaves the crisis parent's playbook unwritten.

### Daniel Susskind — *What Should My Children Do?* (Penguin Press, November 2026): the futures flank

[Susskind's book](https://www.publishersweekly.com/pw/by-topic/new-titles/adult-announcements/article/100308-new-books-on-kids-and-technology.html) addresses the question AI poses to parenting at the horizon of work, education, and purpose — what to raise children *for* in an AI economy.

**Why it does not occupy the lane:** it is an economist's book about children's futures, not a clinician's book about a teenager's present attachment. No overlap with detection, conversation, house rules, or de-attachment. Shares a buyer's bookshelf, not a buyer's problem.

### De Kai — *Raising AI* (MIT Press, 2025): proof of publisher appetite, by contrast

[*Raising AI: An Essential Guide to Parenting Our Future*](https://www.amazon.com/Raising-AI-Essential-Parenting-Future/dp/0262049767) inverts the premise — humanity as the parent, AI as the child — and demonstrated that the AI-parenting intersection clears trade publication and earns media.

**Why it does not occupy the lane:** it is philosophy. A parent holding chat logs cannot act on it, and it does not try to help her. Its existence de-risks the category while defining the gap by everything it is not.

## 2. The Free-Content Layer: Competition and Demand Signal, Honestly Weighed

The most serious competitor to a $20–28 book is not another book. It is the layer of free institutional guidance that already exists, and this analysis will not pretend otherwise:

- **The JED Foundation** publishes ["Why Your Teen Shouldn't Be Using AI Companions – and What to Do If They Are"](https://jedfoundation.org/why-your-teen-shouldnt-be-using-ai-companions-and-what-to-do-if-they-are/).
- **The American Psychological Association** publishes ["Your teen turned to AI instead of you: what experts say parents can do"](https://www.apa.org/topics/artificial-intelligence-machine-learning/teens-chatbots-parents).
- **Common Sense Media** publishes both the field's defining [usage research](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/research/talk-trust-and-trade-offs-how-and-why-teens-use-ai-companions) and its [risk assessment, conducted with Stanford Brainstorm Lab clinicians (April 30, 2025)](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/press-releases/ai-companions-decoded-common-sense-media-recommends-ai-companion-safety-standards), which rated social AI companions an unacceptable risk for under-18s.
- **The Child Mind Institute** publishes parent guidance on [AI chatbots and teens](https://childmind.org/article/ai-chatbots-and-teens/), as do [Children and Screens](https://www.childrenandscreens.org/learn-explore/research/ai-social-companions-and-youth-what-parents-need-to-know/) and explainer journalism like [PolitiFact's parent guide](https://www.politifact.com/article/2025/dec/19/AI-chatbots-companions-talk-to-kids-parent-guide/).

**The honest overlap analysis.** These guides are good, and they are not thin. The JED and APA pieces contain the operative core of two of this book's central protocols: taper rather than cold turkey, open the conversation before imposing rules, watch for named warning signs, refer to crisis resources. A prior draft of this package claimed the book's key deliverables were things "a liability-averse nonprofit will never publish." That claim is false — the free layer publishes recognizable drafts of them — and it has been struck.

**What actually survives as the moat**, after crediting the free layer with everything it does:

1. **Script-level depth.** The free guides say "start a nonjudgmental conversation." They do not give a frightened parent the words — the curious open, the discovered-logs conversation, the five sentences that slam the door, annotated as failure modes. That is the *How to Talk So Kids Will Listen* DNA, and it is the difference between advice and a tool.
2. **The reported case-study spine.** Three families followed from discovery through breakup and rebuild — narrative infrastructure no tip sheet has and no institutional guide will commission.
3. **Age-tiered, negotiated Family AI Agreements.** The free layer says "set boundaries"; it does not provide the 8–12 / 13–15 / 16–18 negotiated household instrument.
4. **The platform-combat chapter — genuinely absent from every free guide.** Built on [California SB 243](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billNavClient.xhtml?bill_id=202520260SB243), the [first state law regulating companion chatbots](https://sd18.senate.ca.gov/news/first-nation-ai-chatbot-safeguards-signed-law): mandated and published self-harm crisis protocols, AI-disclosure duties, break reminders for known minors, and [a private right of action — the greater of actual damages or $1,000 per violation, plus injunctive relief and attorney's fees](https://natlawreview.com/article/when-ai-feels-human-californias-sb-243-opens-door-private-lawsuits) — with model complaint language, a what-to-screenshot evidence protocol, and an honest non-California section covering [the state copycat wave](https://www.multistate.us/insider/2026/4/30/state-childrens-online-safety-laws-expand-beyond-social-media-in-2026) and pending federal law. No nonprofit guide teaches a parent to fight the platform; this chapter is the deliverable the free layer structurally cannot ship.

**The free layer as demand signal.** The same institutions publishing stopgap guides are documenting the buyer: [Pew finds that emotional support from a chatbot is the only teen AI use a majority of parents oppose — just 18% are comfortable with it — while roughly four in ten parents have never discussed chatbots with their teen at all](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2026/02/24/what-parents-say-about-their-teens-ai-use/). A web guide serves the parent who already knows what to search for; it cannot reach the 40% who have never had the conversation, and it cannot sit on a nightstand through a six-week taper. The strategy, accordingly, is conversion rather than denial: cite the JED and APA guides on the jacket as consensus provenance for the protocols, and pursue the Common Sense Media / Child Mind co-brand that turns the largest free "rival" into the foreword and the school distribution channel.

## 3. The KDP Layer: What the Buyer Actually Finds Today

Search Amazon for help with this problem in July 2026 and the results page is instructive. Between De Kai's philosophy and Haidt's phones book sits a shelf of self-published, substantially AI-generated titles — [*AI Parents & Kids*](https://www.amazon.com/AI-Parents-Kids-Complete-Learning/dp/B0FSRTMH51), [*Parenting in the Age of AI*](https://www.amazon.com/Parenting-Age-Resilient-Emotionally-Intelligent/dp/B0DW845215) — keyword-squatting product with tell-tale catalog artifacts (research scouts documented listings bearing "author" names like "BLOCKBUSTER STORY" and "KITS FOR LIFE").

This layer matters in two ways. First, it is a discoverability obstacle: the mid-crisis parent searching at 11 p.m. must be reachable *around* the spam, which raises the value of every off-Amazon channel this package builds — the Attachment Check-In quiz funnel, school newsletters, the co-brand, earned media. Second, it is unambiguous evidence of unmet demand: KDP squatters colonize search terms people are typing into a void. A credible, clinician-authored entry does not fight this shelf so much as render it invisible — the first professionally published book in a spam-filled vertical typically owns the results page's only trustworthy slot.

## 4. Comp Titles and P&L Sizing: The Discipline

The adjudication of this concept ordered a specific commercial discipline, adopted here in full: **The Anxious Generation is the ceiling comp, not the sizing comp.** Sizing to Haidt is how P&Ls become fiction.

**The ceiling.** [*The Anxious Generation*](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/729231/the-anxious-generation-by-jonathan-haidt/): over two million copies per its publisher; [111 consecutive weeks on the NYT nonfiction list as of June 2026, hitting #1 repeatedly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Anxious_Generation). What Haidt proves for this P&L is not a sales projection but three structural facts: the buyer demographic exists at scale, the PTA/school bulk channel converts, and — critically for the regulatory environment this book ships into — **legislation amplifies rather than kills demand**: his book sold through the very state phone-ban wave it helped ignite.

**The realistic lane.** The sizing comps are clinician-with-scripts books — *Good Inside* (Becky Kennedy, Harper Wave, 2022), which demonstrated that a clinician selling protocols and a connection-over-control philosophy can lead the parenting category in the current market, and the decades-long backlist annuity of *How to Talk So Kids Will Listen & Listen So Kids Will Talk* (Faber & Mazlish, Scribner), which proves parents pay, for generations, for word-for-word dialogue guidance. The sober correction inside that lane: the post-Haidt *follower* record is humbling — *The Tech Exit*, the most direct Haidt-wake prescriptive title, achieved bestseller status only on Christian-retail (ECPA) lists while [Haidt extended his own franchise](https://www.npr.org/2026/01/27/nx-s1-5686531/anxious-generation-jonathan-haidt-screen-time-amazing-generation) — evidence that "the Haidt buyer" is substantially a Haidt-*brand* buyer. The P&L therefore assumes this book must manufacture its own discovery moments rather than inherit Haidt's.

**The sizing rules this package commits to:**

1. **Advance and print run modeled on the Good Inside lane, not the Haidt lane.** The Haidt outcome is priced as upside, at zero.
2. **Format hedged below $28-hardcover-only economics** — trade paperback or simultaneous paperback — because the category's competing dollars are subscription-priced: monitoring vendors such as Bark and Aura already sell AI-chatbot-risk detection to this exact parent as a monthly service, and are treated in this plan as bundling/affiliate channels rather than ignored.
3. **Proprietary demand proof before circulation, not analogy.** The Attachment Check-In quiz funnel and the commissioned 2,000-parent survey (including the post-restriction migration module — the question [no published data yet answers](https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/2025/11/25/characterai-ban-teens/)) are funded up front, so the proposal reports measured email-capture and preorder-intent conversion instead of a Haidt analogy.
4. **The addressable market stated conservatively and honestly**, in the two-number form: under Common Sense Media's broad definition, [nearly three-quarters of teens have tried an AI companion at least once, and 52% use one at least a few times a month](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/press-releases/nearly-3-in-4-teens-have-used-ai-companions-new-national-survey-finds); by [Pew's companionship-specific measures, roughly one in eight teens — about three million kids — uses a chatbot for emotional support or advice](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2026/02/24/how-teens-use-and-view-ai/). The sharpest defensible hook: [one in four American teens has, at least once, taken a serious problem to an AI instead of a person](https://x.com/CommonSense/status/1945486943917371578) (33% of the 72% who are users — a one-time behavior, not a standing preference). A book whose competitive premise is rigor cannot ship a P&L built on the inflated versions of its own numbers.

## 5. Preemption Watch: Scenarios and Tripwires

Search cannot prove a negative, and unannounced deals are invisible. The empty niche is a fact about July 2026, not a property of the market. Three scenarios, in descending order of damage:

### Scenario A — the clinician-fronted rival (the kill scenario)

A Damour-class adolescent clinician, or a Stanford Brainstorm / Common Sense–affiliated researcher, sells a scripts-and-protocols book on teen AI attachment. This is the one scenario the protocol-vs-diagnosis distinction does not defuse, because it is this book with a different name on it. **Tripwires:** continuous monitoring of [Publishers Marketplace AI deal flow](https://lunch.publishersmarketplace.com/category/ai/) and PW announcement coverage; the visible platform-building of natural rivals (Lisa Damour is actively developing her own teens-and-AI public presence and is therefore treated in this package as a foreword/endorser target, not an acquisition target). **Response:** speed is the only defense — proposal out the day an author signs (realistically Q4 2026), with the named-protocol suite and the pre-funded proprietary survey raising the cost of fast-following. A rival announcement triggers evaluation of a crashed spring-2027 pub against the default 2027–28 window.

### Scenario B — Turkle spillover (the clock)

[*Artificial Intimacy* lands September 29, 2026](https://www.hachettebookgroup.com/titles/sherry-turkle/artificial-intimacy/9780316573962/). Her launch does two things simultaneously: it creates the biggest "what do I actually *do*?" vacuum this category has ever had, and it shows every agent in New York that the subject clears a major house. Every month after her launch, that vacuum is being pitched into. **Tripwires:** her publicity cycle's parent-facing coverage (does she get asked the Tuesday-night question on air?); any Turkle announcement of a practical follow-up or workbook; the volume of post-launch deal announcements in the category. **Response:** the pre-drafted rapid-response op-ed — *she told you what it's doing to your kid; here's what to do tonight* — converts her cycle into this book's earned media, on the documented Anxious Generation → Good Inside coexistence pattern.

### Scenario C — Haidt annexes the category (the incumbent move)

Haidt folds AI companions into his franchise: a revised *Anxious Generation* edition, an After Babel–branded companion-bot book, or simple campaign dominance of the PTA channel via the ["Don't Give Your Child Any AI Companions" machinery](https://www.afterbabel.com/p/dont-give-your-child-an-ai-companion). His demonstrated pattern — [franchise extension rather than referral](https://www.npr.org/2026/01/27/nx-s1-5686531/anxious-generation-jonathan-haidt-screen-time-amazing-generation) — makes this live. **Tripwires:** After Babel publication cadence on companion bots; any Haidt book announcement touching AI; anxiousgeneration.com resource expansions. **Response:** coexistence, engineered in advance — pursue the After Babel guest essay and resource listing now; the book's fixed thesis ("bans alone backfire — this is the working exit ramp to zero") is compatible with his endpoint, and this book owns the question his abstinence position cannot answer: the already-attached kid, and the enforcement gap that [the GUARD Act's unanimous advance out of Senate Judiciary (April 30, 2026)](https://rollcall.com/2026/04/30/ban-on-kids-companion-chatbots-advanced-by-senate-committee/) and [New York's June 2026 under-18 ban](https://www.techtimes.com/articles/318339/20260613/new-york-bans-ai-companion-chatbots-kids-unanimous-vote-sets-25000-fines.htm) are about to make every family's problem. A Haidt companion-bot edition is the designated trigger for the crash-pub decision.

**Standing watch items:** Laurie Segall's *Mostly Human* (adjacent journalist-diagnosis territory); each platform safety announcement in the Character.AI pattern — [the October 29, 2025 announcement, phased from November 24, 2025, ending open-ended chat for minors while retaining non-chat features](https://blog.character.ai/u18-chat-announcement/) — since every such change manufactures the discovery moments this book monetizes; and the annual research calendar ([Common Sense usage research](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/research/talk-trust-and-trade-offs-how-and-why-teens-use-ai-companions), [Pew's parent-teen studies](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2026/02/24/how-teens-use-and-view-ai/), and the first [SB 243 operator safety reports due to California's Office of Suicide Prevention on July 1, 2027](https://www.sheppardhealthlaw.com/2025/12/articles/state-legislation/california-sb-243-setting-new-standards-for-regulating-and-ensuring-integrity-of-ai-companion-chatbots/)), each of which resets the niche's news cycle inside the publication window.

## Bottom Line

Eight serious books surround this one, and none of them is it. The wave — Haidt's category, Turkle's diagnosis, Twenge's rules, the AAP's guidance, Muldoon's and Susskind's analyses, Suskind's early-childhood flank, De Kai's philosophy — spends the next eighteen months teaching the buyer that her problem is real. The free institutional layer proves she is searching and hands this book its consensus provenance. The KDP spam proves the search term is live and undefended. The comps, sized with discipline, support a Good Inside–lane P&L with a Haidt-shaped ceiling priced at zero. And the niche itself — the clinician's protocol book for the parent whose kid is already attached — remains, as of July 2026, empty, with speed as the only durable defense of that fact.
