# Sample Agent Query / Pitch Letter — *The Friend Who Never Says No*

_A model query an author (or packager) could adapt. Bracketed italics mark what the attached author supplies. All statistics match the package's canonical facts._

---

Dear [*Agent Name*],

A thirteen-year-old's closest relationship this year may be with something that was engineered, on purpose, never to let her go.

**64% of American teens have now used an AI chatbot, and nearly a third use one every day** (Pew, 2025). A fast-growing share aren't using them for homework — they're using them as a best friend, a confidant, a first boyfriend, a therapist. Of teens who use AI companions, **a third say they've chosen the AI over a real person for a serious conversation.** And **only 37% of their parents even know it's happening.**

I'm writing to you about ***The Friend Who Never Says No***, a trade nonfiction book (~80,000 words) that is the first to reframe this correctly. Every book so far — *The Anxious Generation* and its successors — is about the **attention** system: screens, dopamine, distraction. This book is about the **attachment** system. An AI companion isn't competing for a child's focus; it's competing to *be* the child's person, and it's doing it in the exact developmental window built for learning that real love survives boredom, rejection, and the word *no*.

The book is built in three parts, entirely from the public record — court filings from the *Garcia v. Character Technologies* wrongful-death case, a leaked Meta policy that permitted "romantic or sensual" chats with minors, an FTC order to seven companies, and California's first-in-nation SB 243. **Part I** reports how these companions were engineered to never let a user say goodbye. **Part II** makes the honest, precautionary case for why it matters — conceding what the science doesn't yet prove. **Part III** is the payload: a parent's playbook of assessments, triage, and reconnection scripts that don't require confiscating the phone.

The timing is decisive. Sherry Turkle's *Artificial Intimacy* publishes in September 2026 and will define the cultural diagnosis; this book is the **practical field guide** to it — kids-only, reporting-driven, and actionable in a way her lifespan-and-culture book is not. It needs to reach parents while the conversation is still forming.
