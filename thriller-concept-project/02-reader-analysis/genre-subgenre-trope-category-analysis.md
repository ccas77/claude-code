# Genre, Subgenre, Trope & Category Analysis

How the Obsession Thriller sits inside the retail taxonomy, which tropes it must deliver, and where it will be shelved.

---

## 1. Genre stack (from broad to specific)

```
Fiction
└─ Mystery, Thriller & Suspense   ← #2 Kindle bestseller list, 700k+ titles
   └─ Thriller & Suspense
      ├─ Psychological Thrillers   ← #1 sub-genre; PRIMARY placement
      └─ Domestic Thrillers        ← "grip lit"; SECONDARY placement
         └─ [Obsession Thriller]   ← our positioning niche (not a formal category)
```

The **"Obsession Thriller" is a marketing position, not an Amazon category.** On retail it lives in **Psychological Thrillers** (primary) and **Domestic Thrillers** (secondary), reached additionally via keyword-triggered nodes. This is deliberate: single-craft, single-tribe shelving is what the also-bought algorithm rewards (per the skeptic analysis).

---

## 2. Trope demand map — what the book MUST deliver

Tropes readers **name and search for** (from the reader-language bank §2 and BookBub trend reports):

### Load-bearing (must have)
- **Unreliable narrator** — still the #1 named draw, *but* fatigued in its crude form. Deliver the **evolved 2026 version**: "psychologically opaque" narration — truth distorted by emotional bias, withholding and fragmentation, *not* a narrator who simply lies then confesses in the last chapter (the #1 complaint).
- **Obsession / toxic intimacy** — the desire-from-inside-the-danger charge. The differentiator.
- **The perfect life / person that isn't** — "nothing is as perfect as it looks."
- **Buried secret that resurfaces / secrets that unravel.**
- **The impossible situation** — "the protagonist learns of something that could not be true, but is" (BookBub's flagged *rising* psych-thriller hook).

### High-value (should have)
- **Dual timeline / then-and-now** (well-liked scaffold for withholding).
- **Deception signaled in the title** ("lie/lies" titles convert).
- **A morally-grey protagonist you root for against your better judgment** (the *You* / Amy Dunne effect).

### Avoid / handle with care
- **"It was all a dream" / narrator-lied-with-no-clues** → cardinal DNF sin.
- **Slow burn with no payoff** → only forgiven if the ending detonates.
- **Villain-too-smart / dumb-protagonist** plot conveniences → believability complaints.

## 3. Mood/discovery tags (StoryGraph + BookTok)

- **StoryGraph canonical stack:** `Dark · Mysterious · Tense · Fast-paced` — the native thriller signature. Our book adds an *Emotional* charge most clinical thrillers lack — a deliberate, ownable differentiator on the mood filter.
- **BookTok hooks:** "he/she is not who they seem," "I can't stop wanting them," "I should NOT have been rooting for them," "unhinged," "the twist broke me." Engineered to be repeatable in a 15-second video.

## 4. Category strategy (the 3-slot + keyword system)

Since the mid-2023 overhaul, KDP allows **3 selectable browse categories**, extended by **7 backend keyword slots** that can trigger *additional* nodes. *(packaging.md §3–4)*

**Recommended 3 categories:**
1. **Kindle eBooks > MT&S > Thriller & Suspense > Psychological** (moderately competitive — credibility/traffic)
2. **Kindle eBooks > MT&S > Thriller & Suspense > Domestic** (adjacent, high-intent)
3. **A narrower/less-competitive niche** (e.g., *Crime > Kidnapping* or *Suspense > Serial Killers*-adjacent, chosen per-book via Publisher Rocket "Sales to #10" so a modest launch day earns a #1 badge)

**Keyword slots** carry search phrases (demand + low competition) *and* category-trigger phrases. Full keyword build is in `../05-production-package/metadata-categories-keywords.md`.

## 5. Format/length conventions

- **Word count:** **~85,000** (psych-thriller norm; short enough to "finish in a night," long enough for KENP page-reads). Deliberately toward the *lower* end for binge velocity and rapid-release cadence. *(Canonical figure used across this package.)*
- **Chapter architecture:** **short chapters (1,200–2,500 words), each ending on a hook.** This is "the product" — every high-read-through comp uses it.
- **POV:** first-person (or close-third) obsessive narrator, optionally a **dual-timeline** or **dual-POV** counterweight to control information.
