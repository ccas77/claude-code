# Reader Demand Analysis

Who buys thrillers in the Obsession-Thriller lane, what they demonstrably want, and what visible demand signals prove it. Evidence is drawn from category data, trend reports, reader communities, and reviewer language (cited inline; full lists in `../research-data/`).

---

## 1. The market floor: demand is large, durable, and KU-native

- **Mystery, Thriller & Suspense (MT&S)** is the **#2 highest-selling bestseller list on Kindle**, with **700,000+ English-language titles** — a genre with both huge demand *and* huge supply. *(K-lytics)*
- **Psychological thriller** specifically is the **#1 sub-genre inside MT&S** and roughly the **3rd highest-selling category of 7,000+ Kindle categories**; Google search volume for "psychological thriller books" reportedly **doubled over five years**. *(K-lytics)*
- Thriller is estimated at **~20% indie market share**, and **~73% of top MT&S titles are KU-enrolled** — this is a genre readers consume *inside a subscription*, which is exactly where indie read-through economics work. *(secondary aggregates; see `../research-data/economics.md`)*
- Thrillers are cited as **~the second-most-profitable fiction genre on Amazon** (~$728M annual sales, secondary source). Not the biggest (romance is), but large and reliable.

**Implication:** demand is not the constraint. *Discoverability against saturation* is. The Obsession-Thriller position exists to win discoverability by carrying an emotional charge the crowded clinical-twist center lacks.

---

## 2. The demand signal we are actually targeting: *desire + danger*

The most important 2024–2026 demand shift for this project: the **"danger + desire" reader energy has moved to dark, morally-grey, obsession-driven stories** — dark romance, romantasy, villain/antihero narratives — and this migration is **BookTok-native** and growing triple digits, while classic light-danger romantic suspense is in "seeming decline." *(Dark Romance Crates 2026 trends; Atmosphere Press; All About Romance reader commentary)*

Simultaneously, the biggest *thriller* success of the era — Freida McFadden — proves an enormous appetite for **bingeable, twist-driven, character-obsessive psychological/domestic suspense** delivered in short chapters. *(Wikipedia; RD.com interview)*

**The gap between these two facts is the demand this project monetizes:** readers who want the *obsessive, toxic-intimacy voltage* of dark romance but delivered with a *real twist ending* and thriller pacing — not a guaranteed happy ending.

## 4. Purchase & retention behavior

- **KU borrowers read through series at higher rates than buyers** (the next book costs them nothing) — thriller series thrive in KU. *(economics.md §1, §3)*
- **Read-through benchmarks:** Book 1→2 typically **50–60%** (paid), rising to **80–90%+** by Book 3. A 50% read-through on a 5-book run makes each Book-1 reader worth ~**3 full-priced books** in lifetime value. *(economics.md §3)*
- **Binge triggers are structural, not thematic:** short chapters, chapter-ending mini-cliffhangers, constant reveals, and "a protagonist you actually want to root for" (even a terrible one, if *fascinating*). "Just one more chapter" is the single most-cited binge phrase. *(reader-language bank §4)*
- **DNF triggers to design against:** unearned/telegraphed twists, "the author lied to me for 300 pages," slow middle with no payoff, dumb-protagonist decisions, "unlikeable *and boring*." *(reader-language bank §3)*

## 5. Target-reader profile (the buyer)

**Primary reader — "the Unhinged-but-Discerning binge reader":**
- Predominantly **women, ~25–45**, heavy KU subscribers, StoryGraph/Goodreads-active, BookTok-influenced.
- Reads **fast and in volume** (multiple books/week); rewards "finishable in a night."
- Came up through **Gone Girl / The Girl on the Train → The Silent Patient → Verity → The Housemaid**, and is now **drifting into dark romance for the *charge*** but frequently frustrated by the mandatory HEA and thin plots.
- Wants: **an obsessive, morally-grey narrator; toxic intimacy; a twist that recontextualizes everything; and permission to root for someone they shouldn't.**
- Evangelizes: will post the "I should NOT have wanted them to get away with it" reaction — the free-marketing gold this concept is engineered to trigger.

**Secondary reader:** the **dark-romance reader** who wants a real ending, and the **clinical-thriller reader** who wants more emotional heat than McFadden's cool prose delivers. Both are captured by the *same* book because it is shelved in thriller but charged like dark romance.

---

## 6. What this means for the concept (hand-off to the tournament)

The concept must:
1. Put an **obsessive, unreliable, morally-grey narrator** at the center (desire from inside the danger).
2. Run a **single thriller engine** (no co-equal romance arc) with **short-chapter, cliffhanger** architecture.
3. Promise a **real twist / no guaranteed HEA** — the differentiator from dark romance.
4. Give a **high-concept, one-line hook** BookTok can repeat ("he/she is not who they seem — and I can't stop wanting them").
5. Be **series-scalable on author brand** (standalone-first, connected by voice/world), not on one recurring couple.
