# Blurb / Back-Cover Copy — *FEED* Book 1 (*FOR YOU*)

Built on the thriller blurb formula (packaging.md §2): **Hook → Premise → Stakes → Tease.** ~150–200 words. Written in the reader's own vocabulary (reader-language bank §5b). Does *not* reveal the twist.

---

## PRIMARY BLURB (retail / back cover)

> **She knows everything about the woman she's about to work for. The gate code. The brand of tea. The bruise the ring light was angled to hide.**
>
> Wren spends her nights labeling other people's worst moments for a machine. Her only comfort is @honestlyeloise — a wellness influencer whose 3 a.m. confessions feel meant for Wren alone. So when Eloise posts that she's *finally hiring someone to help me hold it together,* Wren already knows the neighborhood. The tea in the cupboard. The mug Eloise will hand her.
>
> She applies as a stranger. She gets the job.
>
> Inside the beautiful house, Eloise is warmer, sadder, and far more dangerous than the feed ever let on. And the last girl who slept in Wren's room — the assistant before her — is a deleted post and a missing-person report no one talks about.
>
> Wren tells herself she's here to save Eloise. She tells herself the feed *chose* her.
>
> The last girl believed that too.
>
> **A pulse-pounding, unputdownable psychological thriller about obsession, surveillance, and the terror of finally being seen — with a twist that rewrites everything. For fans of _You_, _The Silent Patient_, and _The Housemaid_.**

*(Word count ~185.)*

## SHORT BLURB (ads / newsletter / A+ header)

> She knew everything about the influencer she loved — the gate code, the 3 a.m. tea, the bruise the light was angled to hide. She talked her way into the house to save her. She swears the feed *chose* her for it. The last girl who slept in that room believed that too.
>
> **_You_ meets _The Silent Patient_ for the age of the algorithm.**

## Copy rationale (why this converts)
- **Opens on the hook, not a plot summary** (packaging.md §2.1) — the "she knows everything" list triggers immediate unease.
- **Uses reader vocabulary:** "unputdownable," "pulse-pounding," "twist that rewrites everything," "for fans of…" (reader-language bank §5b/5a) — the marketed genre signals readers scan for.
- **Names the emotional core** ("the terror of finally being seen") — the dark-romance voltage that differentiates it, sold honestly as dread, not romance.
- **Comp shorthand** does trust work: *You* (obsessive narrator), *The Silent Patient* (big earned twist), *The Housemaid* (bingeable, indie-native).
- **Ends on a dread-drop** ("The last girl believed that too"), not a resolved statement — the unresolved-question tease.
- **Withholds the twist** while *promising* one ("rewrites everything"), which the concept can actually deliver — protecting against the "unearned twist" 1-star (reader-language bank §3a).

## Honesty guardrail
Market the **"no safe endings"** promise plainly (in newsletter/BookTok, not necessarily the retail blurb). Setting the expectation of a dark, twisty, non-HEA read is what keeps the dark-romance crossover readers satisfied and prevents the "not what I expected" reviews the skeptics warned about.

## Copy guardrail — the "done this before" trap
Wren's past is a **pattern of engulfing, surveilling love that once ended in a death she failed to prevent — NOT a murder, NOT a serial pattern.** The tournament deliberately cut the "repeat killer" reading. Avoid copy that implies she's a serial predator (e.g. a bare *"she's done this before"* reads as *serial killer*, the exact framing we rejected). Steer every "again"/"before" line toward **love-as-surveillance that gets people hurt** ("she loved someone this way once…"), preserving the reader-proxy sympathy that makes the ending ache instead of curdle.
