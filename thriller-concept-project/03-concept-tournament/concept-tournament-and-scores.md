# Concept Tournament & Scores

Six original Obsession-Thriller concepts were generated in parallel from six distinct "obsession lenses," each judged by **three independent acquisitions-panel judges** on eight criteria, then the winner was attacked by three skeptics and rebuilt in synthesis. Raw outputs: `../research-data/01-raw-tournament.json`.

---

## The six competing concepts

| Lens | Working titles | One-line premise |
|---|---|---|
| **Parasocial-digital** ⭐ | *For You / Seen / Watch Time* | A grief-hollowed data annotator who has memorized a wellness influencer's entire feed talks her way into becoming her live-in assistant — insisting the algorithm *chose* her for it. |
| Proximity-caretaker | *The Cleaner / After You Leave* | A crime-scene cleaner fixates on the widower whose wife's blood she erased — then realizes the spatter doesn't match his story, and she destroyed the only proof. |
| Grief-replacement | *The Understudy / Tell Me You Miss Me* | A woman who builds AI "griefbots" from the dead's texts steps into one client's life and becomes his dead wife — only to learn he requested her by name. |
| Predator-reversal | *The Good Neighbor's Wife* | A woman convinces her neighborhood and the police that the recluse across the street is stalking her — while we slowly see she chose him and engineered every incident. |
| Folie-à-deux | *The Mirror Game* | A memory-clinic tech and her most damaged patient secretly rewrite each other's memory of the night a man died between them. |
| Confession-frame | *Tell Me How I Died* | A death doula narrates her own confession to a true-crime podcaster she chose very deliberately — never telling you the one thing that matters. |

*Weighting: revenue ×3, originality ×2, discoverability ×2, series ×2, demand ×2, buildability ×2, twist-earned ×2, genre-fit ×1.*

**The top four were within 1.4 points** — a deliberately close race, which is why the tournament didn't just crown #1 and stop. It **grafted the best element of each runner-up into the winner** (see below).

### Why parasocial-digital won
- **Highest demand + discoverability:** the most *phone-native, universally legible* hook. Everyone has felt parasocially close to an online stranger and been unnerved by a feed that "knows" them. That's a hook BookTok can repeat in one breath and every reader recognizes in themselves.
- **Cleanest single-craft buildability:** contemporary, one primary location + screens, **no forensic/medical/legal gatekeeper burden** (the crime-scene-cleaner and memory-clinic concepts carry more research/procedural weight). Googleable-in-a-weekend texture.
- **Strongest series engine:** "a new obsession every book, same dread" — the platform/feed is a repeatable, off-page amoral force generating a fresh obsessive narrator each book, no recurring couple.

### Why the others placed where they did
- **Proximity-caretaker (84.0):** superb voice and the strongest *dead-predecessor* device, but the biohazard-remediation procedural texture and Rotterdam setting add buildability friction; slightly less BookTok-legible hook.
- **Grief-replacement (84.0):** the most emotionally devastating and the best *uncanny "archive answers back"* device, but the griefbot premise flirts with tech-thriller/speculative shelving risk.
- **Predator-reversal (83.3):** brilliant "root for the monster" engine, but harder to sustain across a series and riskier on reader sympathy.
- **Folie-à-deux (80.3):** most literary and ambiguous; judges worried the dual-unreliable structure muddies the clean twist promise and the also-bought lane.
- **Confession-frame (76.3):** strong voice but the framing device felt more familiar (Verity-adjacent) and the podcast frame is now crowded.

## Adversarial pass — the winner's flaw and the fix

All three skeptics returned **survives = TRUE** and **original = TRUE** — but all three returned **twist_earned = FALSE** on the *original* winning draft, converging on the identical flaw:

> **The original concept had two twists that cannibalized each other.** Twist One framed the *algorithm/Dane as an active procurement system* that "aimed" Wren (making her a harvested victim). Twist Two framed *Wren as a repeat insertion-predator* whose own pattern caused a prior death. These collide on causality — if the system aimed her, she isn't the sole agent; if she's a repeat predator, the reveal is a disguised **"she-lied-then-confessed"**, the exact structure the positioning *forbids*. The clues couldn't discriminate between the two readings, so neither twist was earned.

**The prescribed fixes (all three skeptics agreed):**
1. **Demote the algorithm** from conspirator to **amoral amplifier** — it over-surfaces trauma content to obsessive clusters because that maximizes engagement (real, defensible, *scarier*), with no intent. Hand the crime to a **human with a mundane motive** (Dane — money, control, cover-up). This also removes the sci-fi literalization that risked tech-thriller mis-shelving.
2. **Re-sequence the twins so they reinforce:** Twist One (human crime + amoral amplifier) *removes the algorithm as a rival claimant to guilt*, so Twist Two's clues discriminate cleanly.
3. **Cut the "repeat killer."** Keep Wren a **genuine first-time in-house infiltrator** and the reader's proxy — so "finally being SEEN was the trap" lands instead of curdling into "I was tricked." Her prior death is someone she **failed to save**, not murdered: an earned *emotional-opacity* reveal, not a retracted plot fact.

The synthesis applied every fix and grafted the runner-up elements. The rebuilt, locked concept is in `../04-final-concept/final-concept.md`.

## What the tournament proves

The process did what a single brainstorm can't: it generated **six** viable concepts (all ≥76/100), scored them against the locked commercial positioning, and then — crucially — **used adversarial testing to catch a twist-logic flaw that would have produced exactly the "unearned twist" 1-star reviews the reader-language bank warns about.** The winning concept is stronger *because* it survived the attack, not despite it.
