# Comparable Thriller Titles & Series (2019–2026): A Comp Analysis for a Solo Indie

Prepared for a solo indie thriller author. Scope: psychological/domestic, small-town/crime, romantic suspense, and indie series with strong read-through. Each comp lists subgenre, hook, why it sold, series vs. standalone, indie vs. trad, and packaging notes. Sales figures are as reported by the sources cited; where a number is my inference rather than a sourced figure, I flag it as an estimate.

---

## How to read this list

Two kinds of comps matter to a solo indie:

1. **Breakout indie / hybrid successes** — proof of what one author can build without a Big Five machine (funnels, free book 1, ad-driven series read-through, digital-first publishers like Bookouture/Montlake).
2. **Trad titles that set reader expectations** — the covers, hooks, and structural conventions your readers have been trained on. You compete for the same shelf/thumbnail even if you'll never have the same budget.

The single loudest lesson across all 18 comps below: **short chapters, chapter-ending mini-cliffhangers, a one-line high-concept hook, and a series (or at least a repeatable formula)** are what convert browsers into bingers.

## A. Psychological / Domestic Thrillers

### 1. The Housemaid — Freida McFadden
- **Subgenre:** Domestic/psychological thriller (upstairs-downstairs).
- **Hook:** A young woman takes a live-in maid job for a wealthy Long Island couple — but the power dynamic (and who's really trapped) is not what it seems.
- **Why it sold:** BookTok/Facebook/Goodreads word-of-mouth engine; "one-sit read" structure — short chapters, heavy dialogue, relentless cliffhangers. Per Wikipedia, 2M+ copies and, as of Jan 2026, 141 weeks on the NYT trade paperback list; a 2025 film (Sweeney/Seyfried) reportedly grossed $250M+ globally, which fed the backlist.
- **Series/standalone:** Series (The Housemaid, The Housemaid's Secret, etc.) — a standalone premise that spawned sequels once it broke.
- **Indie vs. trad:** Hybrid. McFadden built her audience as a prolific digital-first/indie author; Grand Central/Bookouture handle print. This is the model indie template — indie velocity, trad distribution once it hit.
- **Packaging:** Bold condensed sans-serif title dominating the cover, single ominous domestic object/silhouette, high-contrast palette. Blurb hooks lean on "you won't see the twist coming" and the maid/master power inversion.

### 2. The Tenant — Freida McFadden (2025)
- **Subgenre:** Domestic thriller (rental-gone-wrong).
- **Hook:** A newly unemployed man rents his spare room to a "perfect" tenant, then his home and life curdle — hostile neighbors, mystery odors, someone who knows his secrets.
- **Why it sold:** McFadden's established brand + the same economical, downhill-with-no-brakes prose and mini-cliffhanger chapter ends. Debuted #1 NYT, held seven consecutive weeks; film rights to Amazon MGM/United Artists (Scott Stuber). McFadden named to TIME100 for 2026.
- **Series/standalone:** Standalone.
- **Indie vs. trad:** Hybrid (same as above).
- **Packaging:** Subtitle does overt comp work — "A Dark & Twist-Laden Psychological Thriller from the Author of The Housemaid Books." Lesson: your subtitle/tagline should carry the comp and the promise.

### 3. First Lie Wins — Ashley Elston (2024)
- **Subgenre:** Psychological thriller / con-artist romantic suspense.
- **Hook:** A con artist living under a stolen identity assigned by a shadowy handler ("Mr. Smith") falls for her own mark — then a woman using her real name shows up.
- **Why it sold:** #1 NYT and a Reese's Book Club pick (a discovery detonator). Shifting timelines and a morally-gray-but-likable heroine; Elston's YA background gave the romantic thread real emotional stakes. High-concept logline ("a con artist falls for her own lie") is book-club and BookTok catnip.
- **Series/standalone:** Standalone (with strong series potential in the character).
- **Indie vs. trad:** Trad (Pamela Dorman/Viking).
- **Packaging:** Clean, graphic, single-symbol cover; Reese's seal used as social proof. Blurb leads with the con/identity hook and the romance-as-danger angle.

### 4. The Couple Next Door — Shari Lapena
- **Subgenre:** Domestic suspense (the genre benchmark).
- **Hook:** A baby vanishes from her crib while her parents are at a dinner party next door — and every "ordinary" suburban character is lying.
- **Why it sold:** Reportedly 4M copies worldwide; WHSmith Book of the Year 2016, #1 UK adult fiction 2017. The everyperson-in-an-ordinary-suburb premise makes the threat feel personal. Lisa Jewell's cover blurb ("intensely bingeable") is a textbook peer-endorsement play.
- **Series/standalone:** Standalone (Lapena writes standalones as a brand).
- **Indie vs. trad:** Trad (Pamela Dorman/Viking).
- **Packaging:** Suburban house/quiet domestic imagery, muted palette, big author name. Defines the "ordinary neighbors, extraordinary secret" convention your domestic-thriller readers expect.

### 5. None of This Is True — Lisa Jewell (2023)
- **Subgenre:** Psychological/domestic thriller.
- **Hook:** Two women who share a birthday ("birthday twins") meet by chance; one insinuates herself into the other's life via a true-crime podcast frame.
- **Why it sold:** Jewell is a domestic-thriller institution; the podcast-within-the-novel structure feels contemporary and gives a built-in "unreliable narrator" scaffold. Strong book-club and Goodreads traction.
- **Series/standalone:** Standalone.
- **Indie vs. trad:** Trad (Atria/Century).
- **Packaging:** Photographic, moody, single-figure or object; author name as the primary brand asset (Jewell's name is larger than the title). Lesson: once you have read-through, the author name becomes the cover.

## B. Small-Town / Crime Thriller Series (the indie read-through engines)

### 6. Detective Josie Quinn — Lisa Regan
- **Subgenre:** Small-town police procedural / crime thriller.
- **Hook:** A detective in a small Pennsylvania town works cases entangled with her own traumatic past.
- **Why it sold:** The clearest digital-first success story here. Per Bookouture, the series surpassed 4M copies by July 2025 (3M+ by mid-2023). Regan was rejected by 150+ agents before Bookouture; she then signed a **15-book contract** (series targeting ~35 books). Long, reliable, same-detective series = compounding read-through.
- **Series/standalone:** Series (17+ and counting), each engineered to be bingeable in order.
- **Indie vs. trad:** Digital-first press (Bookouture) — functionally the indie-adjacent model: fast cadence, ebook/KU-led, print via Grand Central later.
- **Packaging:** Series-consistent covers — lone female figure in a bleak landscape (woods, road, field), cold desaturated palette, oversized emotional-hook title ("Her Dying Secret," "Save Her Soul"). Interchangeable template that signals "next in series" at a thumbnail.

### 7. A Riley Paige Mystery (Once Gone, etc.) — Blake Pierce
- **Subgenre:** FBI/serial-killer crime thriller.
- **Hook:** A brilliant, damaged FBI profiler hunts serial killers (Reacher-for-procedurals archetype).
- **Why it sold:** The purest **free-book-1 funnel**: Once Gone is permanently free with 1,000+ five-star reviews, funneling readers into 20+ paid sequels. Aggressive publishing cadence + multiple interlocking series (main series plus "The Making of Riley Paige" prequel series) maximize read-through and also-boughts.
- **Series/standalone:** Deep series (24 books) with spin-off series.
- **Indie vs. trad:** Indie (high-volume).
- **Packaging:** Uniform title formula ("Once ___"), lone figure in a dark/atmospheric scene, series number displayed prominently. The formula IS the brand.

### 8. DCI Ryan Mysteries (Holy Island, etc.) — LJ Ross
- **Subgenre:** Atmospheric small-town/regional British crime.
- **Hook:** A charismatic detective solves ritualistic, secret-laden murders in Northumberland's stark landscapes.
- **Why it sold:** Reportedly 11M+ copies since 2015, self-published via her own imprint (Dark Skies Publishing). Holy Island famously knocked The Girl on the Train off the UK Kindle top spot. Strong sense of place + a recurring, likable lead + fast cadence. Marketed to "fans of Louise Penny / Michael Connelly" (borrowing trad readerships).
- **Series/standalone:** Series (34+ books across DCI Ryan and spin-offs); each written to also work standalone.
- **Indie vs. trad:** Indie (self-published throughout).
- **Packaging:** Evocative real-landmark landscapes (islands, castles, moors), atmospheric rather than figure-driven, consistent series banding. Lesson: a distinctive, ownable setting can be your differentiator.

### 9. Detective Erika Foster (The Girl in the Ice, etc.) — Robert Bryndza
- **Subgenre:** London-set police procedural / crime thriller.
- **Hook:** A tough Slovakian-born DCI uncovers dark secrets beneath a powerful family after a body is found under ice.
- **Why it sold:** The Girl in the Ice reportedly sold 1M copies within five months of its 2016 release and hit #1 in UK/US/Australia; ~1.5M+ English copies, 30 translation territories, 7M+ across his catalog. Notably, Bryndza first built an audience self-publishing rom-coms, then pivoted to crime — proof that audience-building transfers across genres.
- **Series/standalone:** Series (9 books), each a standalone case within an arc.
- **Indie vs. trad:** Indie origin → hybrid (later Bookouture/Sphere/Grand Central).
- **Packaging:** Cold, blue-toned, single haunting image (ice/water), condensed title, "gripping serial killer thriller" descriptor baked into metadata for search.

### 10. John Milton series — Mark Dawson
- **Subgenre:** Action/assassin thriller (male-lead, Reacher-adjacent).
- **Hook:** A former British government assassin tries to atone for his past, one town and one wronged person at a time.
- **Why it sold:** The canonical **ads-driven indie** case. Millions of downloads; Forbes estimated ~$500K from Amazon alone in 2015. Dawson built a Facebook/Amazon ad funnel feeding a free "Starter Library," then monetized read-through across a long series — and turned the method itself into a business (Self Publishing Formula). Rights later sold to Hollywood.
- **Series/standalone:** Deep series (20+), plus spin-off series (Beatrix Rose, Isabella Rose) sharing a universe.
- **Indie vs. trad:** Indie.
- **Packaging:** Lone armed male figure, urban/noir palette, thriller-standard condensed type; reader-magnet free book as the top of funnel. Lesson: the funnel (free book → email list → ads → series) matters as much as the covers.

### 11. Jack Noble series — L.T. Ryan
- **Subgenre:** Former-military-operative action thriller (Reacher lane).
- **Hook:** An ex-Marine/covert operative pulled into conspiracies and hunts.
- **Why it sold:** One of the largest indie thriller catalogs on Amazon; reportedly millions of copies. Strategy = high volume + first-in-series free/discounted + interlocking series to drive also-boughts and read-through. (Specific per-title sales figures are not sourced here — treat "millions" as the author's reported catalog-level claim.)
- **Series/standalone:** Deep series + multiple connected series.
- **Indie vs. trad:** Indie.
- **Packaging:** Male-action-thriller conventions — silhouetted figure, weapon, high-contrast title. Series numbering front-and-center.

## C. Romantic Suspense

### 12. Melinda Leigh & Kendra Elliot (Rogue River / Widow's Island / co-written series)
- **Subgenre:** Romantic suspense (small-town, law-enforcement-family).
- **Hook:** Interlinked small-town Oregon suspense with strong recurring relationships — "Justified meets Blue Bloods."
- **Why it sold:** Melinda Leigh's books have reportedly sold 8M+ copies. The two authors were paired by their Montlake editor for **cross-marketing**: alternating novellas with individual mysteries but a shared arc — a 4-novella experiment that grew to 10 by reader demand. This is a masterclass in author cross-promotion and shared-universe read-through.
- **Series/standalone:** Multiple connected series and novella sets.
- **Indie vs. trad:** Montlake (Amazon Publishing) — hybrid/indie-adjacent, KU-heavy, high cadence.
- **Packaging:** Romantic-suspense conventions — a couple or lone woman in a moody natural setting, warmer palette than pure crime, emotional-plus-danger tagline. Novella bundling used as a discovery/read-through device.

### 13. Willow Rose (multi-series indie)
- **Subgenre:** Romantic suspense / mystery-thriller (some paranormal-romance crossover).
- **Hook:** Fast, emotional, hook-forward mysteries and suspense across many series.
- **Why it sold:** A prolific indie who leans on KU, rapid release, and BookBub-style promo. (I did not find a single authoritative unit-sales figure in these sources; treat catalog scale, not a specific number, as the takeaway.)
- **Series/standalone:** Many deep series.
- **Indie vs. trad:** Indie.
- **Packaging:** Series-consistent, emotion-and-threat covers; heavy use of first-in-series free and box sets. Lesson for RS specifically: emotional hook + threat hook in the same tagline.

### 14. Hannah Mary McKinnon (The Revenge List, Only One Survives, etc.)
- **Subgenre:** Psychological suspense with a romance-adjacent emotional core.
- **Hook:** Domestic/relationship betrayals turned lethal (revenge lists, sole survivors).
- **Why it sold:** A reliable annual standalone cadence (roughly one book/year, 2018–2025) building an author brand; work optioned for screen. Shows the standalone-a-year trad-suspense career path (contrast with the deep-series indie path).
- **Series/standalone:** Standalones.
- **Indie vs. trad:** Trad (MIRA/HarperCollins).
- **Packaging:** Bold graphic covers, single symbolic object, punchy one-word-ish concepts. Note she also writes rom-com under a pen name (Holly Cassidy) — a clean lesson in separating brands by genre.

## D. Atmospheric & Closed-Circle Standalones (trad titles that set reader expectations)

### 15. The Only One Left — Riley Sager (2023)
- **Subgenre:** Gothic/atmospheric standalone thriller.
- **Hook:** A home-care aide is assigned to an aging woman accused of murdering her family in 1929, in a decaying cliffside Maine mansion — did she do it?
- **Why it sold:** Instant NYT bestseller; near-universal "best of summer" placement. Sager's brand promise = one high-concept atmospheric standalone per year with signature twists. Readers specifically praised this as a return to form, underscoring how much a brand's consistency governs expectations.
- **Series/standalone:** Standalone (Sager's whole model).
- **Indie vs. trad:** Trad (Dutton).
- **Packaging:** Single cinematic, ominous setting (the house/cliff), atmospheric palette, big title. Sets the "one killer setting per book" expectation.

### 16. The Guest List — Lucy Foley (2020)
- **Subgenre:** Closed-circle / locked-setting mystery-thriller.
- **Hook:** A wedding on a remote Irish island; a body; everyone has a motive and a secret.
- **Why it sold:** NYT/Sunday Times bestseller, Reese's pick, 1M+ copies in 30 territories. The tight formula — isolated setting, multi-POV, everyone lying, Christie DNA (And Then There Were None) — is endlessly repeatable and reader-trusted.
- **Series/standalone:** Standalone (formula-as-series: The Hunting Party, The Paris Apartment, etc.).
- **Indie vs. trad:** Trad (HarperCollins/Morrow).
- **Packaging:** Graphic-illustrated setting (island/venue), stylized modern type, Reese's seal. The "trapped location + multi-POV" convention is what your closed-circle readers expect.

### 17. Ruth Ware (One by One, The Turn of the Key, etc.)
- **Subgenre:** Closed-circle/atmospheric suspense (the modern Christie).
- **Hook:** Isolated luxury settings (snowbound chalets, smart-home nurseries, remote islands) where trust collapses.
- **Why it sold:** Consistent NYT bestsellers; the closest peer to Foley. Ware's reliability trains readers to expect a claustrophobic setting + an unreliable narrator every outing.
- **Series/standalone:** Standalones (formula-driven).
- **Indie vs. trad:** Trad (Gallery/Harvill Secker).
- **Packaging:** Cold, atmospheric, single-location covers; author name as brand. Defines the atmospheric-suspense shelf your covers will sit beside.

## E. Two More Book-Club/BookTok Benchmarks (expectation-setters)

### 18. Bright Young Women — Jessica Knoll (2023) & Only If You're Lucky — Stacy Willingham (2024)
- **Subgenre:** Psychological/campus-adjacent thrillers.
- **Hook:** Knoll — a Ted-Bundy-inspired story that recenters the women/survivors rather than the killer. Willingham — college roommates and a fraternity-house murder next door.
- **Why they sold:** Both ride the campus-and-true-crime-adjacent wave and heavy Goodreads/BookTok buzz; Bright Young Women was a Goodreads Choice nominee. They signal the current appetite for feminist reframings and college settings.
- **Series/standalone:** Standalones.
- **Indie vs. trad:** Trad.
- **Packaging:** Bold, graphic, youthful-but-menacing covers; big titles; social-proof seals. Useful as trend-radar for what acquiring editors and BookTok are amplifying right now.

## Cross-cutting takeaways for a solo indie

- **Structure is the product.** Every high-read-through comp (McFadden, Regan, Pierce, Ross) uses short chapters + chapter-ending cliffhangers to manufacture "one more chapter" binge behavior.
- **The funnel beats the single title.** Pierce and Dawson prove that free/discounted book 1 → email list → ads → deep series is the indie money model. A standalone can break you out (First Lie Wins), but a series is what pays the mortgage.
- **A repeatable formula is a feature.** Foley/Ware/Sager (trad) and Pierce/Ross (indie) all sell a *predictable* promise. Pick a lane (closed-circle, small-town procedural, con-artist RS) and make your covers/titles telegraph it.
- **Covers must work at thumbnail.** Condensed bold sans (Bebas Neue/Impact/trade gothic), title occupying 30–50% of the cover, brutal contrast (white-on-black, yellow-on-charcoal), a single cinematic image. Domestic-noir specifically: a house at night, a woman walking away, navy/gray palette.
- **Subtitles and blurbs do comp work.** McFadden's "from the author of The Housemaid" and Lapena's Lisa-Jewell peer blurb show how to borrow trust. Put your comp and your promise in the subtitle/tagline.
- **Series consistency = the author becomes the brand.** Once read-through exists (Jewell, Ross), the author name grows larger than the title on the cover. Design your template to scale toward that.
- **Digital-first presses (Bookouture, Montlake) are the middle path** between pure indie and Big Five — fast cadence, KU/ebook-led, print later. Regan and Leigh/Elliot are the models.

*Estimate flags:* "Millions of copies" claims for L.T. Ryan and catalog-level figures for Willow Rose are author/marketing claims not independently verified in these sources; treat as directional. Box-office and per-week bestseller figures are as reported by the cited outlets.
