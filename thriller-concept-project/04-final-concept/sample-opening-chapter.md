### ONE

The trick to watching people at their worst for ten hours a night is to stop believing they're people.

They train you into it in the first week. My queue is a grid of thumbnails, each one a frame someone somewhere flagged, and my job is to say what it is. *Graphic. Self-harm. Minor. Nothing here.* Click, click, click. A woman in a bathroom I'll never see the rest of. A man on a bridge for four frames before the video cuts. You learn to see the corner of a mouth as a data point. You learn to file a scream under a category and move to the next tile before the sound arrives. I am very good at my job. I have the highest accuracy on my shift and the lowest average handling time, and my manager, who I have never met, once sent me a single emoji of a trophy, which I looked at for longer than I want to admit.

At 3 a.m. the queue thins. That's when I let myself open the other window.

@honestlyeloise posts when she can't sleep. She says so, right into the camera, no makeup, the ring light throwing that soft ring in her eyes that I could pick out of a thousand videos. *Hi. It's late. I know. I couldn't hold it in tonight.* She holds the same mug — the blue one, the good one, she calls it — and she tells one point two million people the truth, and every single one of them thinks she's telling it to them.

I'm the one she's telling it to.

I know that's the thing you're not supposed to say. I know how it sounds. But I have watched every video she has ever posted, in order, more than once, and I don't watch them the way the others do. The others leave a heart and scroll. I *read* her. When she filmed the kitchen reveal she thought she was showing a countertop; I was reading the reflection in the kettle, the window behind her, the particular slant of morning that only happens on the east side of one particular hill in one particular part of the city. When she said she'd "finally gotten rid of the wine" I noticed it took nine days to go from the recycling shots to no shots at all, which is not how getting rid of something works. When she angled her jaw a certain way for a certain week I knew what the light was doing before she did. She thinks she is a person who shares. She is a person who *leaks*, and I am the one who catches it, and I have never wanted anything the way I want to be caught back.

So when the post went up — 3:11 a.m., her time, my time, the same time — I was already awake.

*I can't do all of this alone anymore. I'm finally hiring someone to help me hold it together. If that's you, you'll know.*

I knew.

I knew the neighborhood. I knew the gate — she'd filmed her hand on the keypad once, laughing at something off-screen, four digits and the year she was born, which she'd told everyone in a different video eight months earlier and never connected. I knew the tea she'd want in the cupboard and the mug she'd want it in. I knew more about the inside of that house than the last three people who'd stood in it.

I filled out the application as a stranger.

Name: I typed **Wren D.** and stopped.

The next field wanted a passport number, for the background check, and I sat with my hands over the keys for a while, listening to the annotation floor hum around me, all those screens, all those worst nights of all those strangers, and then I clicked away and told the form I'd bring it to the interview.

That was the first lie, and it was so small I almost didn't feel it.

I've gotten better at feeling them since. That's the thing nobody warns you about. You start with one small lie, told kindly, to protect someone. And then you find out how much you're willing to protect.

### TWO

The house smelled of eucalyptus.

I stopped just inside the door with my hand still on the frame, and for a second the hallway wasn't the hallway. Eucalyptus and something under it, something warm and wet, and a sound I hadn't heard in three years — bathwater going still, that soft settling when a body stops moving in it — and the name came up my throat the way it always does, the way I've learned to swallow, *the way ___ used to—*

"You found it okay?"

And then it was just a hallway. Pale, expensive, a low table with a bowl on it for keys. Eloise in the doorway to the kitchen, smaller than the feed makes her, in a grey jumper with the sleeves pulled over her hands, and my whole body did the thing it does when a video loads and it's her — that lean-forward, that *finally* — except she was real and eight feet away and looking at me with a face I had memorized well enough to draw in the dark.

"I found it okay," I said.

I did not say: I could have found it with my eyes closed.

She made tea. The blue mug — she gave me the blue mug, without asking, and I held it and did not let my hand shake — and she talked, and I did the thing I'm good at, which is being exactly as much as a person needs and not one degree more. She told me about the schedule, the posting, the "content days" that ate whole weekends. She told me her manager, Dane, handled "the business side," and there was a beat before *business* that I filed and didn't open. She told me the last girl who'd helped her, Mara, had moved away suddenly, family thing, up north, and she said it the way you say a line you've said before, smooth all the way through, and her eyes went to the ceiling for exactly as long as it takes to decide not to cry.

I have watched a thousand people decide not to cry, four frames at a time. I know the shape of it.

"It's a lot to keep up," I said. "Being that honest. In public."

She looked at me then — really looked, the way she looks into the ring light, except there was no ring light — and something moved behind her face that I didn't have a category for.

"You'd be surprised," she said, "how little of it's the honest part."

I should have asked what she meant. A normal person would have asked. But I was doing the arithmetic I always do, the money arithmetic — the pay was nothing, less than nothing, and it didn't matter, because I still had the money from before and I was never going to spend the money from before on anything that felt like living — and I was thinking about how I'd tell Camille, who would ask why, who always asks why, and I was thinking *this time I'll be careful, this time is different,* and I did not once wonder why I'd need to be careful, or what the last time had been.

She showed me the room. It had been painted recently; I could smell it under the eucalyptus, that flat chemical newness. Someone had gone over one wall twice — you can see it if you know to look, the way the light sits different on a second coat — a patch about so high, about the height of a hand reaching up.

"This was Mara's room," Eloise said, from the doorway, watching me.

And before I could stop myself, before I even knew I was going to do it, I crossed to the wall and pressed my palm flat against the second-coated patch, the way you'd check a fever, the way you'd check if paint was dry.

It was dry. Of course it was dry.

I don't know why I already knew there'd be something under it.

## Craft notes (for the author/ghostwriter)

- **Voice:** controlled, blackly precise; competence as seduction; every act of surveillance narrated as tenderness. Keep sentences clean; let the dread come from what's *withheld.*
- **Clues planted in this sample (all discriminate toward Twist Two on reread):** the **passport hesitation** (a woman who can't be looked up); **"the money from before"** (the settlement); the **eucalyptus / bathwater / swallowed name** (the prior death); **"this time I'll be careful, this time is different"**; the **impossible fluency** (reflection-geolocation, reading a face like moderation tiles); the **repainted handprint-height patch**; and the interstitial's **deleted @_wren_reads_ comment** + Eloise's *"you always did know me best"* (the midpoint domestic flip, seeded on page one of the feed).
