# FINAL CONCEPT — *FEED* (Book 1: *FOR YOU*)

The selected, adversarially-tested Obsession Thriller. Series brand: **FEED**. Book 1: **FOR YOU**.

---

## The selected thriller subset — and why it won

**Subset: Psychological/Domestic thriller, positioned as the "Obsession Thriller."**

It won a 15-subgenre scored evaluation once the raw winner (romantic-suspense crossover, 8.7) was refined through adversarial testing. Three skeptics independently proved a *true* romance×thriller crossover imposes a fatal **dual-craft tax** and a **discoverability trap** for a solo author. The reconciliation kept the winning *demand signal* — readers want **desire + danger** — but delivered it through **one craft the author masters**: a single-engine psychological thriller carrying dark-romance emotional voltage, **thriller-shelved, no guaranteed happy ending.** Full reasoning: `../01-market-evaluation/`.

*FEED* is the purest expression of that position: it takes the **universal 2 a.m. feeling** of parasocial closeness to an online stranger and turns it into a knife — dark-romance intimacy intensity with **zero genre-bending**.

## One-sentence pitch

> A grief-hollowed data annotator who has memorized every frame of a wellness influencer's life talks her way into becoming the woman's live-in assistant to save her — and swears the feed *chose* her for it, right up until she's standing in the dead last-girl's room and can no longer hide that no one aimed her here: she found the wound and walked in, the same way she did once before.

**Comp shorthand:** *You* (Kepnes) × *The Silent Patient*, for the TikTok era, with the bingeability of *The Housemaid*.

## Target reader

The **"unhinged-but-discerning binge reader"** (see `../02-reader-analysis/reader-demand-analysis.md`): predominantly women 25–45, heavy KU subscribers, StoryGraph/BookTok-active, raised on *Gone Girl → The Silent Patient → Verity → The Housemaid*, now drifting to dark romance for the *charge* but frustrated by mandatory happy endings and thin plots. They want an obsessive, morally-grey narrator, toxic intimacy, and a twist that recontextualizes everything — plus permission to root for someone they shouldn't.

## Core premise

Wren D., 29, labels training data for a content-moderation AI — flagging violence, self-harm, and abuse frame by frame, ten hours a shift, until strangers' worst moments feel more real than her own life. Her one comfort is **@honestlyeloise**, a soft-lit wellness influencer whose 3 a.m. vulnerability posts feel addressed to Wren alone. Wren doesn't just watch Eloise — she reverse-engineers her: geolocates the kitchen from a reflection in a kettle, times the posting schedule to a cycle, catalogs the bruise the ring light was angled to hide. When Eloise posts that she's "finally hiring someone to help me hold it together," Wren already knows the neighborhood, the gate code pattern, the brand of tea. She applies as a stranger. **She gets the job.**

Inside the real house, Eloise is warmer, sadder, and more dangerous than the screen ever let on — and the last assistant, **Mara**, is a deleted highlight reel and a quietly archived missing-person post. Wren sleeps in Mara's repainted room, wears Mara's role, drinks from Mara's mugs. And she keeps narrating a comforting story: that the algorithm surfaced Eloise's pain to exactly the follower who'd show up — that she was *selected, harvested, chosen.* The truth she withholds from herself is worse and simpler. **No system aimed her. She found the wound herself and walked in — and she has done a version of this before.**

## The engines

### Obsession engine (the toxic-intimacy core)
**Parasocial intimacy weaponized into forensic knowledge.** Wren's job — annotating human suffering for an AI — trained her to see people as data to be labeled, and she turns that skill on Eloise, "knowing" her more completely than any friend while never having heard her voice unfiltered. The dread comes from a single fusion: **Wren's love is indistinguishable from surveillance, her tenderness from control.** The reader feels the seductive pull of finally being *totally seen* — then watches that exact longing become the vulnerability the system mines and the compulsion that killed the last woman Wren loved. *"I shouldn't want to be this close to her"* is the heartbeat; *"I only wanted to help"* is the lie the heartbeat hides behind.

### Protagonist engine (the narrator you root for against yourself)
**Wren D. is the reader's own 2 a.m. scrolling habit given a body and a purpose.** We root for her because her competence at reading people is a genuine thrill to sit inside and her devotion is *real* — she narrates every violation as love, making the reader complicit in reframing surveillance as tenderness. She is **psychologically opaque, not a liar**: she genuinely can't see her own darkness. She withholds *why* she can quit a job to work for near-nothing, *why* she gives only "Wren D.," *why* she crosses the street from a patrol car — because she is protecting herself from the memory of an earlier woman she "helped" from online, whose life she colonized with love-as-surveillance, and who died. **Not murder** — her smothering help was simply *there* at the end. She stays the reader's proxy the whole way (a genuine first-timer, not a cold serial actor), which is what lets the final ache land instead of curdling into "I was tricked."

### Antagonist engine (three human layers, no machine villain)
- **Surface — Eloise:** magnetic, drowning, complicit-by-silence in Mara's fate; at the midpoint, revealed to have **recognized Wren as her longtime superfan and hired her on purpose** — the watched chose the watcher. She wants a devotee obsessive enough to burn Dane down and lonely enough to adore her while doing it.
- **Deeper — Dane:** the growth-hacking manager who mines the feed's engagement data to identify whales and superfans, funnels them into an unpaid live-in "assistant" pipeline and a paid inner-circle tier, and controls Eloise's money and image. Mara discovered the exploitation and threatened to expose it; she died in a confrontation Dane staged as "she moved away." **Mundane motive: money, control, cover-up.**
- **Deepest and truest — Wren's own pattern:** the opposing force she cannot defeat is the story she tells about being a *rescuer* — the "I was chosen" grammar that stands between her and what she actually does to the people she loves.

### Central threat (the danger engine)
**The assistant before Wren is dead, and Wren is standing in her footprints.** Two braided dangers close at once: **Dane**, who will bury a second inconvenient girl to protect his scheme; and **Wren's own escalating pattern**, in which "helping" Eloise metastasizes into the same smothering surveillance that was present the last time a woman Wren loved stopped breathing. The clock is Eloise's next **"big vulnerable drop,"** which Dane is spinning up for engagement and which will detonate the confrontation — leaving open whether Wren will be Mara's avenger, Mara's successor as victim, or the thing that finishes what the house started.

## Stakes

- **Personal:** if Eloise's warmth was ever a targeting parameter, the one true thing in Wren's life is a lie; and if she *chose* this rather than being chosen, the loneliness she blames was never anyone's fault but her own.
- **Survival:** Mara died in this house; Wren occupies her role; Dane will kill again to keep the first death buried.
- **Moral:** to save Eloise — or herself — Wren may have to do the exact controlling, engulfing thing her buried past proves she is capable of.
- **Living stake on the page:** if Wren "saves" Eloise the way she "saved" the last one, **Eloise is the one who pays.**

## Escalation pattern

**SCREEN → HOUSE → BODY**, mirrored by **KNOWLEDGE → INTIMACY → ENGULFMENT.**

- **Act 1 (screen):** remote surveillance, the interview, getting hired. Tension from proximity and near-misses of being recognized as a superfan. Chapter hooks land on *things Wren already knows that she shouldn't.*
- **Act 2A (house):** living inside the real Eloise; intimacy deepens; small wrongnesses accumulate — Mara's archived post, the locked studio, Dane's dashboard, the wine bottles, the second phone, the paid-tier ledger. **Every comfort curdles into evidence.**
- **Act 2B (collapse of distance):** the relationship turns genuinely intimate *and* genuinely predatory at once; Wren realizes Mara was killed, not relocated.
- **Midpoint domestic flip:** the watched was watching — **Eloise has a folder on Wren and hired her knowingly.**
- **Act 3 (real world → back into the house):** the next drop goes live; Dane moves to contain the second girl; the climax collapses to the two women alone in the house, where Wren must face that **no one aimed her, that she came both times,** and choose whether to become — one final time — the smothering weapon her own history predicts.

## Twist architecture (why it's earned)

**Twin twists, re-sequenced so they reinforce instead of cancel** (the exact flaw the skeptics caught and the synthesis fixed):

1. **TWIST ONE (external, Act 2, human not systemic):** Mara didn't leave — she *died*, and Dane staged the departure to protect an exploitation scheme. The algorithm is only an **amoral amplifier**: it over-surfaces Eloise's rawest trauma to high-parasocial-risk clusters and concentrates whales because that maximizes engagement — no intent, no design to harm. Dane monetizes the output and killed to keep it hidden. *This is real (vulnerability content genuinely over-indexes to obsessives), so tech-literate readers nod instead of scoffing.*

2. **MIDPOINT (domestic flip):** Eloise holds a folder on Wren and hired her knowingly — the intimate watcher-watched inversion, kept inside the house.

3. **TWIST TWO (primary, TRUE — the opaque-narrator reveal):** Wren's entire *"the feed chose me / I was selected / harvested"* narration is **the lie she tells herself, not the reader.** No one aimed her. She found the amplified wound and inserted herself — **and she has done a version of this before:** an earlier woman she "helped" from online, whose life she engulfed with love-as-surveillance, and who died with Wren's smothering "help" present at the end. Not murder — an order, an investigation, a name she can't finish saying, a settlement that funds her "near-nothing" year. *"I came to save her" is the mask her guilt wears.*

**Why it's earned, not a cheat:** Because Twist One removes the algorithm as a rival claimant to guilt, Twist Two's clues **discriminate cleanly** — every one points unambiguously to "she did this before and it ended in a death," not to "she was prey." On reread the snap is *of-course*: every "the feed reached for me" rereads as self-exculpation; every act of love rereads as recognizable, escalating, fatal behavior. It is **a truth the reader assembles from what Wren can't bear to say — NOT a fact retracted in chapter forty.**

### The clues planted (the twist's receipts)
1. Wren gives only **"Wren D."** on the application and hesitates at the passport field — never explained; rereads as a woman who can't be looked up.
2. She never states how she affords to quit and work for near-nothing; one line about **"the money from before"** she moves past — later legible as a settlement tied to the earlier death.
3. She **crosses the street from a parked patrol car** and closes an incognito tab fast when a **memorial page** loads — planted as ambient anxiety, rereads as guilt and an order against her.
4. She reads the curated-vulnerability trap with **impossible fluency** (reflection-geolocation, grooming cadence, how a helper embeds) — narrated as instinct, rereads as *experience.*
5. A recurring sensory memory she refuses to finish: **eucalyptus, bathwater going still, a name she starts and swallows.**
6. Her rescuer overcorrection: **"this time I'll be careful, this time is different"** — "this time" never explained.
7. Her **passive, self-exculpating grammar** — "the feed reached for me," "I was chosen" — clusters exactly when she's about to do something invasive.
8. Dane's dashboard column labeled **"high-intent"** beside handles and dollar figures — marketing jargon, rereads as a target list of whales.
9. Eloise's rawest 3 a.m. posts **spike the engagement graphs hardest**, and Dane thanks her the next morning — amoral optimization, no malice.
10. The **archived missing-person post** for Mara, whose assistant inbox still auto-forwards and whose drafts hold **one scheduled post still queued to publish** (the archive answering back).
11. **"The Kettle Club"** — a paid inner-circle tier whose ledger shows months of payments from the same superfans; whale-mining in plain sight.
12. Wine bottles migrating recycling→trash, the locked studio, Dane's cheap **second burner phone.**
13. Eloise says something **only Wren's anonymous superfan account would know** ("you always did like the blue mug") — planted as warmth, rereads as the midpoint reveal.
14. Wren **repaints over a handprint-height smudge** in Mara's room without being asked, without letting herself wonder why she knew to — the annotation-trained reflex to erase evidence, and her own pattern, in one gesture.

## Series engine — why this scales

**FEED — connected standalones, one per book,** each a different obsessive narrator whose parasocial fixation collapses into the real world: a fresh voice, victim, and platform every time (Book 1 wellness influencer; then a true-crime podcaster; a dead creator's still-posting account; a rising musician; an AI-companion chatbot). **No recurring couple.** The connective tissue is the **platform itself and its recommendation system as an amoral, evolving off-page force** — never a sentient villain, always the scarier thing: a machine that keeps rewarding creators for bleeding in public and keeps concentrating whales, with no intent required. Occasional shared minor figures (Dane's growth agency; the moderation-annotation underworld) reward returning readers without gating new ones.

**Book 1 fully resolves** Wren, Eloise, and Dane; the amplifier survives untouched — guaranteeing the next harvest and the next obsessive.
**Brand promise: "a new obsession every book, same dread."**

## Why readers continue to Book 2 and beyond

They finish Book 1 having fallen for a voice, then learning the real horror isn't a killer or a conspiracy — it's an **amoral system that will keep rewarding women for bleeding in public and keep producing lonely people who love strangers too much, forever, with no one to punish.** So Book 2's promise is another irresistible obsessive, another stranger-you-think-you-know, another collapse of distance — powered by the same off-page amplifier that just proved it never stops. *Same dread, brand-new obsession, earned twist* — the McFadden/Kepnes rapid-release engine delivered on **author brand** rather than a recurring couple.
